The bakery began with a wall that was not yet a wall, a foundation dug into a lot that had been a cabbage plot the previous spring. The man who dug it was named Aleks, and he was thirty-one that year, and he had come to the street with a wife, a mule that died within the month, and a plan drawn in charcoal on the inside of his coat. He built the oven first and the shop around it, so that for a season the oven stood open to the weather like a shrine, and he fed it green wood that spat and smoked until he learned the trick of curing it. His wife, Marta, sold the first loaves out of a basket in the doorway before the door was hung.

They had four children in six years, of whom two lived. The street then was mud in spring and dust in summer, and the carts came through twice a day, and the bakery smell reached the corner where the well stood, so that people said you could find the shop blindfolded by walking toward the bread. Aleks worked the oven for thirty-eight years. He rose at three, and he was said never once in that time to have overslept, and he grew deaf in one ear from the roar of the fire and stooped from the peel. Marta died in the winter of a fever that took a third of the children on the street. Aleks did not remarry, though women were suggested to him, and he wore his grief the way he wore flour, invisibly and everywhere.

The son who took the oven was Tomas, the younger of the two who lived, because the elder had gone to the city to work on the rail lines and was killed there by a falling beam at twenty-four, unmarried, buried in a place his family never saw. Tomas had learned the oven at his father's elbow and hated it and stayed anyway, which is the common history of most trades. He married a girl named Sofi from the far end of the street, a seamstress's daughter, and he widened the shop by knocking through the wall into the room where his mother had died, and he put in a counter of oak that his grandchildren would one day lean against without knowing whose hands had planed it.

Tomas ran the bakery through the first war, when flour was rationed and he stretched it with potato and bran and once, in the worst month, with sawdust so fine he told no one, and the loaves of that year were dark and heavy and are still remembered by the very old as the bread of the hard time. He kept three men from starving on credit that was never repaid and never asked for. He buried his father in the same plot as his mother. He had five children, of whom four lived, and the shop by then employed a boy to carry deliveries and a woman to work the counter, and the street had gained a cobbled surface and gas lamps and, near the end of Tomas's life, the first automobile any of them had seen, which frightened the delivery horse into a wall.

The daughter who took the oven was Elena, the third of the four, and she took it because her brothers had gone, one to the priesthood, one to the sea, one to a factory two towns over that made bicycle parts and then, later, other things. Elena was the first woman to work the oven in the family and the neighbors remarked on it for perhaps a year and then forgot, because her bread was good and memory on the street was short and practical. She converted the wood oven to coal and then, after the second war, to gas, and she kept the old brick oven standing in the back though she never lit it again, and she said it held up the roof, which was partly true.

Elena lived through the second war, which was worse than the first on the street, though she spoke of it seldom. The shop was closed for eleven months in the middle of it and the windows were boarded, and when she opened again the counter woman was gone and did not return and no one said where. Elena married late, at thirty-six, a widower named Piotr who kept the accounts and did not bake, and they had one son together, born when she was forty, whom they named Jan after no one in particular. Elena worked the oven until she was seventy-one and her hands would no longer close all the way, and she sat in a chair by the counter for two years after that, correcting the boys who worked the dough, and then she died in her sleep in the room above the shop, which was the room where three generations had by then been born.

Jan took the oven, though by his time it was less an oven than a bank of steel machines with dials, and he modernized without sentiment. He tore out the oak counter and was talked out of it only halfway, so that a section of it was preserved and set into the new laminate surface like a relic in a reliquary, and customers rested their coins on it without a thought. He painted the front and hung a new sign and put in a glass case with a light inside it, and he sold not only bread but pastries filled with cream and small cakes iced in colors his grandmother would have found alarming. He married a woman named Irena who ran the front while he ran the back, and they had two daughters and a son, and the street by then had lost its well and its lamps and its cobbles and gained a traffic light at the corner where the shrine of the open oven had once stood.

Jan lived through the years when the small shops closed one by one, the butcher and the ironmonger and the two competing groceries, replaced first by nothing and then by a supermarket at the edge of town, and the bakery survived because bread is bread and people still wanted it warm and near, and because Jan was stubborn in exactly the way his great-grandfather had been, though he never thought of the man once. He installed a machine that took plastic cards, and he stood behind it the first day watching it print its small receipts with something that was not quite wonder, and he told his son that he could remember his own grandmother lighting wood, which was a lie, because Elena had converted the oven before he was born, but he believed it as he said it.

The son, who was named Aleks after a great-great-grandfather whose grave he had never found, did not want the oven and said so, and went to the city to study numbers, and the two daughters married and moved away, one near and one far. So that Jan, in the end, sold the shop to a young couple from another part of the country who kept the name on the sign because it was good for business and did not know whose name it was. They found the section of oak set into the counter and thought it charming and left it. Behind the wall the old brick oven still stood, cold for eighty years, holding up the roof, and no one alive knew it was there.
