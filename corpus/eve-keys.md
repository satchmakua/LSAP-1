There is a particular slant to the streetlight that has come through this same gap in the curtains for as long as I have had a body to lie under it, a thin amber wedge falling across the ceiling in the exact place it fell when I was six and convinced the shape was a doorway to somewhere, and lying here now with the moving boxes stacked and taped and labeled in my own handwriting by the door, I find I cannot stop smiling into the dark because tomorrow at ten o'clock a woman named Priya whom I have met only twice will put two keys into my open palm and close my fingers over them and say congratulations the way people say it when they mean it, and I will drive across the city with the boxes shifting in the trunk and I will climb three flights and I will fit the smaller of the two keys into a lock that has never known my hand before, and the door will swing into rooms that are, for the first time in my life, entirely and only mine.

I keep picturing the emptiness of those rooms, which is not really emptiness at all but a kind of held breath, the floors bare and honey-colored in the light that comes in from the west in the late afternoon, and I know already where the table will go because I stood in the kitchen last week and saw it there, saw the whole future arranged itself around a table I do not yet own, saw the morning coffee steaming and a book left open face-down and a bowl of lemons because I have always wanted a bowl of lemons and never had the surface to put them on, and it strikes me lying here that half of being happy is simply having a place to set things down, a windowsill for the small stones you cannot throw away, a shelf for the mugs, a hook by the door for the one coat, and I have wanted this so long and so quietly that the wanting had gone almost silent, folded up small inside me, and now it is unfolding all at once and taking up the whole night.

Downstairs my mother has left the hall light on the way she always leaves it, and I can see the faint line of it under my door, and I think of all the nights that line has been there while I slept or didn't sleep, the night before exams, the night after the first heartbreak when she came up and sat on the edge of the bed and said nothing that helped and every bit of it helped, the nights of being seventeen and furious and certain I would leave this house and never look back, and how funny it is that the leaving, when it finally comes, feels nothing like that furious version imagined, feels instead like the way you let go of someone's hand at a train station, gently, both of you already turned toward what comes next but the warmth of the palm still there for a moment, still there.

I will hang the blue curtains in the bedroom, the ones I bought two years ago and kept in their packaging like a promise I was making to a self I hadn't met yet, and I will learn which stair creaks and I will learn the sound the radiator makes when it wakes up in October, that knocking, that clearing of an old iron throat, and I will learn the names of the people across the hall and the particular way the light lies down in the evening and where in the neighborhood the good bread is, and there is such pleasure in the not-yet-knowing, in the whole city of small facts waiting to be gathered, that I almost wish I could freeze time exactly here on the threshold where everything is still possible and nothing has yet been decided by the stubborn shapes of actual furniture.

And I remember being small in this very bed and my grandmother telling me, while she smoothed my hair, that when she came to this country she had one suitcase and a name of a cousin written on a piece of paper, and how she stood in a room the first night with the suitcase still closed because opening it would have made it real, and how she said the first thing she ever did in a place of her own was to boil water, just to boil water and stand and watch it, because a kettle on a stove was the whole meaning of home to her, and I think tomorrow I will boil water first, before the boxes, before anything, I will fill a pot and set it going and stand in my kitchen in the western light and watch it come to a rumble and I will do it for her, and for the girl who bought the blue curtains, and for the six-year-old who saw a doorway in the ceiling and was, it turns out, entirely correct.

There is so much I am leaving that I ought to feel it more as loss, the smell of this house which is bread and dust and the particular soap my parents have used for thirty years, the exact give of this mattress worn to the shape of me, the tree outside the window that I watched grow from a sapling and that now brushes the glass, the kitchen table with the burn mark from the pot my brother set down too fast the summer he learned to cook, the whole archive of a childhood pressed into these walls like flowers in a book, and yet what I feel is not loss but a strange gratitude that runs alongside the leaving like a second river, because none of it is being taken from me, it is only being set down where I can always come back to it, and I will come back, I will come back on Sundays and sit at that burned table and my mother will fuss that I am too thin and I will let her, and the coming back will be sweeter for the going.

I think of all the ordinary ceremonies waiting: the first time I lock the door behind me and feel the click as something that means me, the first meal cooked badly and eaten anyway standing up because there is no chair yet, the first thunderstorm heard from my own bed with my own roof taking the rain, the friends who will climb the three flights and fill the rooms with noise and leave and let the quiet come back, the plants I will buy and probably kill and buy again, the slow accretion of a life chosen rather than inherited, and it is almost too much, this abundance of unlived days, and I press my hand flat against my chest as if to keep it all from spilling.

The amber wedge has slid a little across the ceiling the way it always does as the night turns, and I let my eyes follow it, and I think how in a week I will be lying in a room whose ceiling I have not yet studied, learning its cracks and its watermarks, its own particular geometry of light, and I will lie there just like this, awake and glad, and I will think of this last night in this bed, and I will be grateful all over again, and somewhere between that gladness and this one I will finally, finally fall asleep, though not yet, not quite yet, because the night is warm and mine and full, and I want to stay awake a little longer just to feel it.
