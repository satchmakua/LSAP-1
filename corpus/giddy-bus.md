And the bus comes wheezing up to the curb like it's been running to meet me, doors folding open with that rubber gasp I've heard ten thousand mornings and never once loved until right now, this exact now, this morning after, when I climb the steps two at a time and tap my card against the reader and it beeps green, green, the little arrow flashing me forward, forward, go, you belong here, and I do, I do, I actually do, it's official, there's a certificate and everything, it has a seal on it, an embossed golden seal that catches the light if you tilt it and I tilted it maybe forty times last night in the kitchen under the bad bulb until my sister told me to go to bed, but who sleeps, who could sleep, not me.

The driver nods. She just nods, an ordinary nod, and I want to grab her by the shoulders and tell her, ma'am, ma'am, do you know, but I don't, I just beam at her like a lunatic and find a seat by the window, the window, mine.

Everything's wet out there. It rained overnight and the whole street is lacquered, every surface doubled, the traffic lights bleeding red and green into the puddles like somebody spilled paint for me, only for me, a little parade laid down in water. Look at that. Look at the hydrant. The hydrant! Squat and yellow and rusting at the bolts and it's the most magnificent thing, a small gold king on the corner, presiding.

We pull away and the city just unrolls.

I've taken this bus so many times I could ride it with my eyes shut, crosstown, the long slow grind past the laundromat and the place that sells phone cases and the church with the crooked cross, but today somebody's rehung the whole world, somebody came in the night and made it new, and I press my forehead to the glass and it's cool and a little greasy and I don't even care, I love the grease, I love the smudge of every forehead that came before mine.

There's a man asleep against the pole in the back and his mouth is open and it's beautiful. It is. I know how that sounds. But there he is, trusting the whole bus with his sleeping self, trusting all of us, and I'm one of the us now, I'm a fully credentialed us, and I could weep, and I almost do, right there between the laundromat and the light.

The light changes. Green.

I passed. I keep saying it inside myself like pressing a bruise except it doesn't hurt, it's the opposite of a bruise, it's whatever the opposite of a bruise is, a warm spot, a coin in the pocket you forgot about. Passed. All those questions I drilled at the bus stop and in the shower and once, embarrassingly, out loud on this very bus while a woman edged away from me — how many amendments, who writes the laws, name one branch, name a war — and yesterday in that fluorescent room the officer with the tired kind eyes asked me and I answered and answered and answered and my voice didn't even shake that much and he said congratulations and he meant it, I think he meant it, he's said it a hundred times a week but he meant it to me, and I floated out of there, I have been floating for eighteen hours, I am floating now on this bus over the wet street.

And nobody's impatient, or maybe I'm just deciding nobody's impatient, and a boy gives up his seat without being asked and she lowers herself down like a whole ceremony and says thank you baby and the whole thing is so tender I have to look back out the window before I start up with the almost-crying again.

Out there the sun's trying. Behind the clouds, shoving. There's a seam of gold at the edge of a rooftop water tank, one of those big wooden barrels up on stilts that I always thought were ridiculous, so old-fashioned, so temporary-looking, and today it's a monument, it's a lighthouse, it's the most local possible thing, a barrel of water on a roof, and the light hits the seam of it and I think somebody made that, somebody climbed up there and built that, hands did that, and hands do everything, this whole staggering place is just hands and hands and hands.
