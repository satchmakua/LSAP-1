The last announcement had come and gone, and now the board above the platform reset itself with a soft mechanical shudder, dropping its rows of names and times into a blankness that meant nothing was expected. I stood beneath it because I had chosen this spot earlier, when the station was fuller, when standing here had seemed sensible, a place to be found. There is a way of standing that assumes arrival. I had been standing that way for some time.

The floor was tiled in a pale grey that the cleaning cart had left wet in long overlapping arcs, and the arcs were drying now unevenly, so that the floor looked marbled, veined, like something older than the building. A man pushed the cart into a corridor and did not come back. Somewhere a door with a bad hinge opened and shut, opened and shut, and each time it shut the sound arrived a half-second late, as though the station were slightly larger than it looked, as though there were more of it than I could see.

I had the ticket still, though the ticket was for a train that would not be taken now, a return that had assumed there would be two of us. I turned it over in my coat pocket the way you turn a stone at the shore, not looking at it, only knowing its edges. The edges were soft already. Paper wears fast against the hand.

The trains kept coming. That was the thing I had not anticipated, that the world would continue to deliver its trains on their schedule regardless, that the timetable was indifferent to whether the person on it existed anywhere along its route. A train came in from the north and settled with a long exhalation, and the doors released their small crowds, and I watched every face the way one reads a page in a language half-forgotten, hopeful and unequipped. None of the faces was the face. The crowds thinned. The train drew its doors closed and went on to wherever the schedule sent it, and the platform was emptier than before, and I understood that emptiness is not a single condition but a series of them, each one lower than the last, a staircase going down into a room you did not know the building had.

There was a bench, and I did not sit on it. To sit would have been to admit a duration. Standing kept the thing provisional. I have noticed this in myself before, this bargaining with posture, as if the body could negotiate with what has already been decided elsewhere, in some office of the actual, by clerks who do not take appeals.

A woman sat on the bench with two bags and a child asleep against her arm. She looked at the board, then at her phone, then at the board. Her waiting was a different species from mine. Hers had an object still in transit, a certainty merely delayed. I envied her the shape of it. Delay is a kind of wealth. Delay assumes the thing exists and is only late. What I had was not delay. I had not yet found the word for what I had, and I did not go looking for it, because the word was somewhere near me in the station, patient, and I did not want to be introduced.

The lights were the kind that do not flicker but hum, a hum you stop hearing and then hear again all at once, so that its return feels like an event though it has been there the whole while. I heard it return. I let it be an event. There is not much, at a certain hour, in a certain state, and you take the events that offer themselves.

I thought of the message I had sent and the message I had not received, and I did not take out the phone to confirm this, because I already carried the confirmation in the particular hollow beneath the sternum where such things are kept. The body files them there. You can feel the drawer of it. I have felt it before and knew the shape of the feeling, though the feeling arriving now was not lessened by the knowing. Recognition does not dilute. It only means you cannot pretend to be surprised, which is its own small deprivation, the loss of even the dignity of shock.

Two attendants passed, talking in the flattened voices of people at the end of a shift, and one of them laughed, and the laugh was not cruel, was only human and elsewhere, belonging to a life that was proceeding, and I heard it recede down the corridor and mix with the bad hinge, and I thought: the station is full of lives proceeding, and I am standing in the one place where a life has stopped. Not mine. I was breathing, I was upright, I was turning a ticket in my pocket. But some thread that ran out from me into the arriving trains had gone slack, and I could feel it lying now across the tiles, unattached at the far end, and I did not reel it in because I did not yet want the confirmation of the slackness in my own two hands.

The board above me stayed blank a long moment and then produced, from its blankness, one final departure. The time was not far off. It was the last of the evening. After it there would be nothing until the early service, that grey hour when the cleaners return and the first commuters stand where I was standing with their own certainties, their own small delays. The board offered the departure as though it were offering me something, an out, an ending I could participate in. I could go. I could carry the ticket to the gate and let the machine take it and step onto a train and be delivered, singular, to the return I had booked for two. I looked at the number of minutes. I did not move toward it. Neither did I decide not to. I have found that the largest refusals are made this way, by the body simply declining to enact them, standing where it stands, letting the minutes pass through it like water through a grate.

The woman with the child rose. Her train, then. She gathered the bags and adjusted the sleeping weight against her and walked toward the platform edge with the practiced tilt of someone carrying more than she can comfortably hold, and she managed it, and the child did not wake, and I watched her go with something I did not name because to name it would have been to make it larger than I could carry. She would arrive somewhere. Someone would meet her, or no one would, but either way she was in motion toward the fact of it. I stayed still and let her motion be the thing that happened in the frame while I was the thing that did not.

The station kept emptying. It has a limit, this emptying, but I did not reach it. I stood beneath the board with its single glowing departure and its steady countdown, and the hum returned again as an event, and the hinge opened and shut its half-second late, and somewhere the wet arcs on the floor finished drying into nothing, and the minutes on the board grew fewer, and I turned the softened ticket over once more against my palm, and I did not look at the number, and the train, when it came, came for someone else.
