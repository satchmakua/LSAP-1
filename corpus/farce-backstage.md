"Where's the dagger?" Marlene hissed, palm out, eyes on the stage lights bleeding through the curtain gap.

"On the table."

"This is a banana."

"That's the table."

"Then the table is lying to me, Kev, because I need to stab the Duke in forty seconds and I am holding a piece of fruit."

Kevin looked at the props table. The props table looked back with its little masking-tape labels, all of which had migrated, curled, and swapped positions during the interval when somebody's cat—nobody would say whose cat—had been let loose in the wings to catch a mouse that turned out to be a lost sock.

"Just—use the banana."

"Use the banana."

"Point it. It's dark out there. Fringe theater. Squint through it."

"I am not stabbing the Duke with a banana."

"Duke's on!" whispered Priya from the flat, and shoved Marlene, who went through the split in the curtain still arguing, and the audience saw a woman in Elizabethan velvet advance upon the Duke with murder in her heart and a banana in her fist.

The Duke—Gerald, sixty-one, a retired dentist who had learned his lines in the car—did not miss a beat. "Villain! You come armed?"

"I come armed," said Marlene, "with the fruit of my vengeance."

The audience, which had paid nine pounds each, roared. They thought it was a choice.

Backstage, Kevin was already sprinting for the table because the next cue was the letter, the crucial letter, the one that revealed the Duke's secret, and he grabbed the thing labeled LETTER and it was a rubber chicken.

"Priya."

"What."

"Why is the letter a chicken."

"That's not the letter."

"It says letter."

"Everything says everything, the labels are—the cat—look, the letter's a small flat white thing, does that look flat and white to you—"

"It looks like a chicken."

"Then it's the chicken, give me the chicken—no, don't give me the chicken, I need the goblet—"

"For what?"

"The poisoning scene, Kev, keep up, we open the second act on a poisoning and I have got"—she snatched an object—"a kazoo."

Onstage, Gerald was stalling. Gerald was very good at stalling. Gerald had once filled ninety seconds of a root canal with a monologue about the migratory patterns of geese, and he now turned to Marlene and said, "But soft—does a servant not approach with news?"

No servant approached with news. The servant, Toby, nineteen, was in the wings holding what he believed to be a scroll and was in fact a fly swatter, staring at it, mouthing *this isn't right, this isn't right.*

"Toby, go!"

"It's a swatter."

"It's a scroll now, go, be a servant, deliver the swatter, say it's a scroll—"

Toby walked on and bowed and held out the fly swatter and said, "My lord, urgent news, sealed and swift," and Gerald took the fly swatter with the gravity of a man accepting a state document and unfolded it—it did not unfold, it was a fly swatter—and read from it: "Ah. The Duchess. Lives."

Marlene, still holding the banana, said, "She lives?"

"She lives," said Gerald, brandishing the swatter, "and here it is writ."

"Then all my plans," said Marlene, improvising like a woman running downhill, "are"—she looked at the banana—"gone soft."

The audience wept. The audience thought this was the cleverest thing they had seen. In the second row a man wheezed and had to be patted.

Backstage the director, Dennis, arrived, red as a stop sign, whisper-shouting through his own molars. "What is happening. Why is my tragedy funny. Why is the Duchess alive, she dies in Act One, she is DEAD, she is my strongest death—"

"The chicken," said Kevin.

"The what?"

"The letter was a chicken so Priya used the swatter as the scroll and the swatter said the Duchess lives—"

"The swatter didn't SAY anything, the swatter is a SWATTER—"

"I know that and you know that but Gerald read it—"

"Gerald read a fly swatter."

"Gerald will read anything, Dennis, you cast a dentist, he has no fear."

"Where," said Dennis, "is the poison?"

Priya held up the kazoo.

Dennis looked at the kazoo for a long time. From the stage came Gerald's voice, ringing, delighted, off the rails and loving it: "Fetch me the poison'd cup, that I may end this masquerade!"

"There is no cup," said Priya. "There's a kazoo and a banana and a chicken and I think a whisk."

"Give him the whisk," said Dennis, and then, "no, I—what am I saying, don't give him the whisk—"

"Too late," said Kevin, because Toby had already grabbed the whisk and gone, propelled by the terrible momentum of theater, and presented it to Gerald as the poisoned cup, and Gerald raised the whisk to the lights and gazed into it as though it brimmed with doom.

"Ah, sweet release," said Gerald, and mimed drinking from a whisk.

Marlene watched him drink from a whisk. Marlene had trained for three years at a real academy with a real diploma and she watched a retired dentist drink poison from a kitchen whisk in front of nine-pound-a-head paying strangers and something inside her, some professional dam, some last levee of dignity, gave way, and she started to laugh, and she turned upstage so they wouldn't see, shoulders heaving, banana trembling.

"She weeps," announced Gerald to the house, magnanimous, "for the wicked feel remorse at last."

"I do not weep," said Marlene, choking, "I—" and she couldn't finish, and the banana slipped, and she caught it, and the catch was so absurd, the little bobble and grab, that the front row lost it entirely, and now the whole theater was one animal howling.

Dennis had his head against a flat. "Nine pounds," he was murmuring. "They paid nine pounds for a tragedy."

"They seem happy," said Kevin.

"They're supposed to be devastated."

"They're crying, technically."

Onstage Gerald staggered, magnificently, from the whisk-poison, one hand on his heart, one hand aloft, and he looked to the wings and hissed, low, actor to crew, "The crown. Someone bring the crown, I die a king."

Kevin lunged at the table. He grabbed the object labeled CROWN. It was a colander.

He looked at Priya. Priya looked at him. Dennis looked at both of them and said, very quietly, "Do not."

Kevin brought the colander to Toby. Toby carried the colander onstage and set it, with immense ceremony, upon Gerald's dying brow, and Gerald—Gerald, who had by now understood everything, who had grasped that he was the captain of a sinking ship of props and had decided to go down conducting the band—Gerald wore the colander like a diadem and delivered, through the drain holes, the finest death of his amateur life.

"Thus falls," he gasped, "a great and foolish man."

The colander slid over one eye. He left it. He died. He died beautifully, the light catching in the little holes, and the house came apart, stamping, the man in the second row now genuinely alarming, and Marlene knelt over the corpse-king with her banana laid tenderly on his chest like a rose, and Priya from the wings blew, softly, the last mournful note of the kazoo, and Dennis, in the dark, put down the clipboard he'd been strangling and began, helplessly, against every plan he had ever made, to applaud.
