Look about you, my friends, look about you this morning and see who has come. You have come from the far farms and the near, from the cottages along the lane and the houses hard against the hill. You have come with your children washed and your boots scraped clean and your hands, your good hands, folded in your laps at last. You have come out of the weather and into the warm, out of the long labor and into this hour of rest, out of the scattered days of the year and into this one gathered morning. And I say to you: it is right that you have come. It is right that you are here. It is right, above all, that we should stand together beneath this roof and lift up our voices, for the year has turned, the fields are shorn, the barns are full, and the season of thanks is upon us.

Consider what your hands have done. Consider the winter, when your hands broke the frozen ground and mended the broken fence and carried the fodder through the dark to the lowing beasts in the byre. Consider the spring, when your hands cast the seed upon the turned earth, scattering, scattering, trusting what the eye could not yet see. Consider the summer, when your hands rose before the sun and lay down after it, when your hands blistered and healed and blistered again, when your hands did not rest though the body begged for rest. Consider the autumn, this very autumn now behind us, when your hands took up the scythe and the sickle, when your hands bound the sheaf and stacked the stook and gleaned the last of the fallen grain. Your hands did this. Your patient hands, your aching hands, your faithful hands did all of this, and the field that was bare in the frost is a field of gold no longer, for the gold is gathered in.

But I ask you, who sent the rain? I ask you, who warmed the seed in the dark of the soil? I ask you, who bade the green blade climb toward the light, and swelled the ear, and ripened the grain, and held back the storm in the very week the harvest lay open to the sky? Not your hands, friends. Not your hands, for all their faithfulness. Your hands cast the seed, but they did not command the sun to shine upon it. Your hands bound the sheaf, but they did not weave the wheat within its husk. You labored, and your labor was good, and your labor was blessed — but the increase was given. The increase was given. Say it with me in the quiet of your hearts: the increase was given, and we did not earn it, and we did not make it, and still it was poured out upon us, full measure, pressed down, running over.

So let us give thanks. Let us give thanks for the rain that fell when the ground was thirsty and held off when the hay lay drying. Let us give thanks for the beasts of the field, for the ox that pulled and the horse that hauled and the ewe that yielded her lamb in the cold of March. Let us give thanks for the sun that we cursed in July and blessed in September, for the wind that carried the chaff away and left the good grain behind. Let us give thanks for the seasons that keep their ancient covenant, that come and go and come again, that never once in all the years of our fathers and our fathers' fathers have failed to turn. Let us give thanks that we are alive to see another harvest home.

And let us give thanks, my friends, for one another. For it is not one pair of hands that brings in the year. When the storm threatened old Thomas's upper field, whose hands went to help him? Yours. When the widow could not cut her own small plot, whose scythes were carried up the lane at dawn? Yours. When a man fell ill in the very throat of the harvest, who took his rows and his labor upon their own bent backs and said nothing of it after? You did. You did. This is the thing I would have you carry from this hour above all others: that the harvest is a harvest of the whole, that no sheaf stands alone, that we are bound to one another as the stalks are bound within the sheaf, and that when we lift the burden together the burden is made light. You are not each of you alone upon your farm. You are a people. You are a gathered people, and this morning you are gathered here.

Hear now the joy that rises in this place. Hear the children shifting in the pews, restless for the day. Hear the old ones humming under their breath the tunes they sang when they were children too, in this same chapel, on this same morning, in the years long gone. Hear the boards creak beneath your feet, the boards your grandfathers laid, the boards that have held up glad and grateful people harvest after harvest after harvest, without number, without end. This is not a house of mourning today. This is not a house of fear. This is a house of gladness, a house of plenty, a house where the door stands open and the hearth is warm and the welcome is for all. Rejoice, then. Rejoice, for you have every reason. Rejoice, for the labor is finished and the fruit is safely stored. Rejoice, for you are together and you are well and you are here.

And now — do you smell it? Do you smell what waits for us? Through that wall, in the hall next door, there is a table, and the table is laid, and it groans beneath the weight of your goodness. There is the bread of this year's wheat, warm from the oven and broken to be shared. There is the fruit of your orchards, red and gold, heaped high in the baskets your own hands have woven. There is the honey of your hives and the butter of your churns and the ale that has waited in the cool cellar for this very day. There is meat and there is milk and there is the last sweet fruit of summer preserved against the winter. That table was bare a year ago. That table is laden now. And I tell you, the table did not lay itself, and neither did it lay itself alone, for every dish upon it came from a different hand, and each was carried in this morning as a gift, and all of it is set out for all of us, and none shall go hungry from this place today.

So rise now. Rise, all of you. Rise with thanks upon your lips and gladness in your hearts. Rise as one people, who labored as one people and were blessed as one people. Let us go in together to the table that waits, let us break the bread together and pour the cup together and eat together the good gift of the good year. Let us go in with joy. Let us go in with thanks. Let us go in, my friends — the harvest is home, the table is spread, and the feast is ready. Come. Come. Come and be filled.
