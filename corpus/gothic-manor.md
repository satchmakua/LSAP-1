The carriage abandoned me at the gates as though it too feared the place, wheeling away into the gathering gloom with such indecent haste that I stood there abandoned, my single trunk beside me, while the first low mutter of thunder rolled across the desolate moor. Above me reared the manor of Ellwrayth, its black towers clawing at a sky the colour of a drowned man's cheek, and I confess that my heart, which I had believed inured to terror by long habituation to solitude, quailed within me like a candle set before an open casement. How grim it was! How pitiless in its ruin! Every window seemed a socket emptied of its eye, and the great oaken door hung upon its hinges with the sullen patience of a thing that had swallowed generations and awaited, with monstrous appetite, another.

I mounted the crumbling steps. The ivy that shrouded the façade rustled though no wind yet stirred, and I could not suppress the fancy — absurd, I told myself, the fevered issue of a mind wearied by travel — that the house breathed, that beneath its mantle of decay some vast and ancient lung expanded and contracted in slumber. The door yielded to my hand with a groan so human, so freighted with lamentation, that I started back and pressed my palm against my breast to still the frantic hammering there. And then, as if in answer, the heavens opened their first sob of rain.

Within, the darkness was of a quality I had never known — not the mere absence of light but a positive presence, a substance, thick and cloying, that seemed to lap against my ankles as I crossed the threshold. My lantern, feeble spark against that engulfing night, threw a trembling circle upon the flagstones, and I beheld a hall of ruined magnificence: a staircase spiralling upward into shadow, its balustrade carved with writhing figures whose faces, worn to smoothness by the centuries, yet retained an expression of such exquisite anguish that I wept to look upon them. Tapestries hung in tatters from the walls, and where the moth and the damp had not devoured them I discerned scenes of the hunt, of feasting, of a woman in white fleeing eternally through a wood of iron trees. Dust lay upon everything like the ash of some incinerated joy.

O, but the dread that seized me then! It rose not from any single horror but from the totality, the accumulation, the sense that I had entered not a house but a wound — a great gash in the fabric of the ordinary world through which some other, colder reality bled slowly through. I called out. My voice, thin and unmanned, fled from me into the recesses of the hall and did not return, and the silence that followed was more terrible than any answer could have been.

The storm now hurled itself against the manor in earnest. Rain lashed the windows with the fury of a thousand pleading hands; the thunder no longer muttered but bellowed, and each concussion sent a shudder through the ancient timbers so that the whole edifice seemed to sway and moan about me like a vessel foundering upon rocks. Lightning — sudden, savage, sublime — flooded the hall with a light more ghastly than the dark, and in that instant of revelation I saw, or believed I saw, upon the landing of the great staircase a figure. Pale. Motionless. Regarding me. Then the darkness fell again like a portcullis, and I stood trembling, uncertain whether I had beheld a living soul, a portrait, or the mere phantasm of my own o'erwrought imaginings.

I could not remain below. Some compulsion — call it madness, call it the perverse magnetism by which the moth is drawn to its immolation — bore me toward the stair. I ascended. Each step protested beneath my weight with a shriek that mingled hideously with the tempest's roar, and the carved and anguished faces of the balustrade seemed to turn, ever so slightly, to follow my passage. Higher I climbed, and higher, until the hall below was lost in a well of blackness and I was suspended, as it were, between the earth I had forsaken and some heaven I dared not name.

Upon the landing there was nothing. No figure, no ghost, no lurking horror — only a corridor stretching before me into an obscurity so absolute that my lantern's light seemed to dissolve within it, consumed as a drop of water is consumed by the desert. And yet the air here was changed. It was colder — colder than the grave, colder than the spaces between the stars — and it carried upon it a perfume both sweet and corrupt, the odour of lilies left too long in a sealed and airless room. I pressed forward, my breath rising in ghostly plumes before my face, and the walls on either hand pressed close, oppressive, intimate, as though the corridor itself narrowed to swallow me.

A door stood ajar at the corridor's end. Behind it a light burned — a dim, uncertain, greenish glow that pulsed like a failing heart. I approached it as a condemned man approaches the scaffold: with dread, yes, but also with a horrid eagerness, a longing to have the worst at last made manifest, to be delivered from the tyranny of not-knowing into the merciful certainty of doom. My hand upon the door. The wood cold as flesh three days dead. And a whisper — I swear it, a whisper — rising from within, a voice ancient and androgynous and unspeakably weary, that seemed to pronounce my name, though how it could have known my name I dared not ask.

I flung the door wide.

The chamber beyond was empty. Empty! A bed with mouldering hangings, a chair overturned, a mirror veiled in cobweb and grey with the accumulated forgetfulness of decades — and upon the far wall, a great cracked window through which the lightning came, again and again, in silent detonations that lit the room like the flickering of some infernal magic-lantern. There was no whisperer. There was no one. And it was this emptiness, this vacancy where I had steeled myself to meet a horror, that broke something within me; for the mind may arm itself against the seen but has no defence against the absence that mocks its terror. I sank to my knees upon the rotten boards and let the great storm of my dread break over me as the greater storm broke over the house.

And then — O merciful heaven, or heaven's opposite — I raised my eyes to the veiled mirror, and the lightning came, and in its glare I beheld reflected there not my own countenance but another: a woman's face, white as the moon, her eyes two wells of an infinite and patient sorrow, her lips parting as if to speak the name she had already spoken. She lifted one translucent hand toward the glass. Toward me. And the whole manor of Ellwrayth cried out around us in the voice of the wind, and the light failed, and I knew — with the awful, dawning, irrevocable certainty of a nightmare from which there is no waking — that I had not come to this place as a stranger at all, but as one long expected, long summoned, and now, at last, come home.
