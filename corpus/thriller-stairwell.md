The alarm cut out on the forty-first floor. Silence dropped like a shutter.

Vance hit the fire door with his shoulder. It banged open. Concrete steps spiraled down into gray light. He took them three at a time.

Behind him, a door slammed. Then boots. Two sets. Maybe three.

He counted floors by the painted numbers. 41. 40. His hand skidded on the rail. He gripped harder. The metal was cold and slick with old paint.

A shout above. Not words. Just a bark, a signal.

He jumped the last four steps to the landing. His knees took it. He kept moving.

39.

The stairwell smelled of dust and rust. Emergency lights glowed at each turn, dull orange. Between them the shadows pooled deep.

He heard the boots gaining. They knew the tower. He did not.

At 37 he shoved through the fire door into a corridor. Wrong move. He knew it the second the door shut behind him. But he was committed now.

Fluorescent tubes flickered overhead. The corridor ran straight, lined with locked office doors. A cleaning cart sat abandoned halfway down. He ran to it. Grabbed the mop bucket. Dumped it.

Gray water spread across the tile.

He kept going. Reached the far end. A junction. Left or right.

The door behind him crashed open.

He went left.

A man rounded the corner into the water. His feet went out. He hit the floor hard, gun skidding away across the tile. He swore.

Vance did not look back. The corridor bent. A stairwell sign glowed red ahead. SERVICE. He hit the bar and burst through.

These stairs were narrower. Bare steel. No paint, no numbers. Utility pipes ran along the wall, sweating condensation.

He went down.

The clang of his own steps rang up the shaft. There was no hiding it. He just had to be faster.

Two flights. Three. A rat scrambled across a landing. He nearly slipped. He caught the rail.

Above, the door opened again. Voices now, low and controlled. They were not panicking. That was worse.

He reached a landing with a door marked ELECTRICAL. Locked. He tried the next flight. It ended at a steel plate bolted across the shaft.

Dead end.

He turned. The only way out was the electrical room.

He kicked the door beside the handle. It held. He kicked again. The frame splintered. A third kick and it gave.

Inside, darkness. Banks of breakers. A low hum. Cables thick as his wrist snaked up the walls into the ceiling.

He pulled the door shut behind him and jammed the cleaning cart's dropped screwdriver — no, he had no screwdriver. He shoved a coiled cable against the base of the door. Useless. He knew it was useless.

He moved deeper into the room. There had to be another way. Every service room had two exits. Fire code.

He found it in the far corner. A crawl hatch, low, set in the wall. A maintenance run.

Boots on the landing outside. The door handle turned.

He dropped to his knees and pulled the hatch. It swung inward. Beyond it, blackness and the smell of dust and machine oil.

He went in headfirst.

The door behind him opened. A flashlight beam swept the room. He pulled his legs in and yanked the hatch shut behind him, holding the inner edge with his fingertips.

The beam crossed the hatch. Passed. Came back.

He held his breath.

A voice, close now. "Room's clear."

Another. "Check the hatch."

He let go and scrambled backward into the dark. The tunnel was tight, maybe three feet high. Ductwork above, cable trays below. He crawled fast, palms grinding on grit.

Behind him the hatch banged open. Light flooded in. It threw his shadow long down the crawlspace.

"Contact! Service run!"

He crawled harder. The tunnel forked. He took the drop — a vertical chute where the run stepped down a floor. He went in feet first, braced his back against one wall and his boots against the other, and slid.

He hit the bottom and rolled out into another horizontal run. Above him, a man's face appeared at the top of the chute, then a hand, then a gun.

Vance kicked sideways into a cable tray and pulled. The whole tray came loose in a shower of dust. Cables spilled down the chute in a black tangle, catching the arm, the gun.

A curse. A jam.

He crawled on. Ahead, gray light. A grille.

He reached it. Kicked. It bent. Kicked again and the grille tore free of its screws. He slithered out.

Another stairwell. A real one. Wide, painted, numbered. 34.

He stood. His legs shook. He forced them steady and ran down.

33. 32. He heard the crawl run behind him disgorging its pursuit somewhere above. They would take the stairs now. Faster than crawling.

31. The gap between his steps and theirs began to close again.

At 28 he swung out into the lobby of a floor under renovation. Plastic sheeting hung from the ceiling in long milky sheets. Scaffolding stood against the windows. The floor was bare subfloor, screws and offcuts everywhere.

He ran through the plastic. It clung to his face. He tore it away.

A window bay stood open where the glass had been removed. Wind came through it. Twenty-eight floors of nothing beyond.

Scaffolding ran outside the opening. A ladder bolted to the frame led down.

He heard the fire door slam behind him.

He climbed out onto the scaffold. The wind grabbed him. The city spread below, small and bright and very far. He did not look at it. He put his hands on the ladder and went down.

The rungs were cold and wet. His feet found them by feel. Rung. Rung. Rung.

Above, a face appeared at the window opening. Then the barrel of a gun.

The first shot cracked the air beside his head and sparked off the scaffold pole. He dropped the last few rungs to the platform below and rolled behind a stack of façade panels.

A second shot. The panel jumped.

He looked down. Another ladder, one level lower. Below that, a construction hoist — a caged elevator clamped to the outside of the tower.

He crawled along the platform behind the panels. Reached the ladder. Went down.

Above, the man was climbing out onto the scaffold now, slow, careful in the wind. He could not shoot and climb at once. That bought seconds.

Vance reached the hoist. The cage door was up. He hauled it down. The control panel had two buttons. Up. Down. He hit down and grabbed the frame.

The hoist lurched, then began to descend, slow and grinding, hauling him down the outside of the tower.

Twenty-six. Twenty-five.

The scaffold shrank above. The man reached the hoist's level and stopped, gauging the drop, deciding.

He did not jump.

Twenty. Fifteen. The wind eased as the tower's bulk gave shelter. Ten. The street came up to meet him, sidewalks, parked vans, a man in a hard hat looking up in confusion.

The hoist bumped to a stop at ground level.

Vance threw up the cage door and ran.
