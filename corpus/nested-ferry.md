I am telling you this on the ferry, which matters, because the woman who told it to me told it to me here too, or on a ferry very like it, the same route north through the black water, the same bar with its bolted stools and the bottles ranked behind their little brass rail so they will not walk in the swell. She had grey in her hair and a way of holding her glass without drinking from it. I am telling you the way she told me, or as near as I can, though already I feel the words shifting under me the way a deck does, and you must forgive me where I slip, because I was not there and she was, and inside what she was there for there is a man she never met at all.

She said that she had it from a fisherman. A retired one, she said, meaning he no longer went out, though she doubted a man ever retires from the sea so much as the sea retires from him. He was drinking rum that night, she said, and she was drinking whatever she was drinking, and he began, as such men do, without invitation. She said he said: I knew a keeper once, out on the Sgeir light, and everyone thought him mad, but I'll tell you what he told me, and you can think what you like.

Now here I have to stop, because I have made an error already, and I want to be honest with you. She did not say the Sgeir light. I have put that name in myself, from some other evening, and it is wrong of me. She named no rock at all. She said the fisherman named it, but that when he named it she did not know the place and could not hold the sound of it, and so in her telling it had become simply "the light," and in mine it must stay simply the light, because I will not lend it a borrowed name and call that truth.

Very well. She said that he said: the keeper was a young man when he went out, and by the end he was not old but he was finished, and between the going out and the finishing there were three winters. And the fisherman told her — she was quite clear on this, that it was the fisherman's own colouring and not the keeper's — that in the first winter the keeper wrote everything down, and in the second winter he wrote less, and in the third winter the log had only weather in it, wind and glass and the state of the sea, as though the man inside the words had quietly stepped out and left the instruments talking to themselves.

But the story, the real story, the one that survives at the bottom of all this like a stone at the bottom of a well — that the keeper told the fisherman with his own mouth. The fisherman had put in at the rock to bring oil and news, and the keeper made him tea and would not let him go until the tide turned, and in that hour he told it. And the fisherman gave it to the woman word for word, he swore, and the woman gave it to me the same, and I give it to you now, and you must understand that four mouths lie between you and the man, and each of us has breathed on it, and yet.

He said — the keeper said, this is the keeper now, you must hear the change in it, the older, plainer speech of a man who spoke to no one for weeks and so spoke very carefully when he spoke at all — he said: There is a woman comes up the stair on the nights of hard weather. I do not say she is drowned and I do not say she is living. She comes up out of the well of the stair when the sea is loudest and she stands where the light turns, and the light goes through her the way it goes through rain, and she asks me the same thing every time. She asks, Is it lit? And I say, It is lit. And she says, Then I can go down. And she goes down.

Here the fisherman had laughed, the woman said. He had laughed to cover something. And he told her — I am back with the fisherman now — that he had said to the keeper, straight out, You are alone too long, man, that is all it is, and the keeper had not argued. The keeper had said only: That is what I told myself the first winter. And the keeper had said: The first winter I answered her to be kind. The second winter I answered her because I was afraid of what she would do if I did not. And the third winter —

And here the woman stopped, on the ferry, with her glass in her hand, and she said to me that here the fisherman had stopped too, exactly here, and had turned his glass on the bar in a slow circle, and she had thought he would not finish it. And I must tell you that I have felt the same reluctance arrive in me now, at this same turn, as though the story itself puts a hand on the teller's mouth. But it was told to me and so I will tell it.

The keeper said — this is the deepest voice, the stone at the bottom — he said: The third winter I understood that she was not asking me. She was asking the light. She had never once looked at me. She was asking the light whether the light was doing its work, because that had been her work, before, whoever she had been, and she could not go down and rest until she knew the ships were safe. And I understood that I was not the keeper of the light. I was only the one who says the word she needs to hear. The light does not need me. She does not need me. I am the mouth that says *it is lit*, and any mouth would do, and when I am gone another mouth will say it, and she will not notice the difference, because she has never once looked at the man, only ever at the flame.

And the fisherman told the woman — she was firm that this was the fisherman's ending and not the keeper's, that the keeper had simply stopped there and poured more tea — the fisherman told her that within the year the keeper was taken off the rock, not dead, only emptied, and that the man who replaced him reported nothing at all, no woman, no stair, no voice, a perfectly ordinary posting, and that this proved the keeper had been merely lonely and unwell.

But the woman did not believe that was proof, and told me so, and I do not believe it either, and I will tell you why, and then I am done. Because the new keeper, the ordinary one, the one who saw nothing — he too, every night of hard weather, without knowing why, would climb the stair and stand where the light turned and say aloud, to no one, to the empty glass and the wheeling beam, *it is lit*. He told this to no fisherman. It went into no log. The woman had it from the man himself, years after, an old man by then in a home above the harbour, and she leaned close to him the way I am leaning close to you now, and she asked him why he said it, and he said he did not know, only that if he did not say it he could not sleep, as though someone were waiting on the word.

And the sea is loud tonight. Do you hear it. Is the light lit, do you think. Say it is. Say it anyway.
