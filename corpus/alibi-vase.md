Let me be completely honest with you, because I think honesty is the only thing that matters in a situation like this. I was not even home when it happened. I was at my sister's, out in Aldergrove, which is a good forty minutes if the bridge is clear, and it is never clear, so call it an hour. I remember distinctly because we were watching that program she likes, the one about the auctions, and I said to her, isn't it funny, we're watching people sell antiques while my own antique sits at home all alone. That was the joke I made. You can ask her.

So you understand I have no firsthand knowledge of the breakage itself. What I can offer you is a reconstruction, an honest reconstruction, based on what I found when I returned. The vase was on the floor. In several pieces. Near the sideboard, where it had always stood, on that little runner my mother crocheted, which is not itself insured but which I mention for the picture. And the window was open, which I never leave open, which tells you everything. The wind came through. That's the long and short of it. The wind took the curtain, the curtain caught the vase, and down it came. I heard it, actually — I heard the whole thing, that particular sound, glass turning to gravel, you never forget it.

Now, I want to correct myself, because I said I heard it and that's not right, that's me getting ahead of the story. I couldn't have heard it, could I, being in Aldergrove. What I mean is that I heard it in the sense that my sister's neighbor heard something, a report of a sound, secondhand, and I have simply absorbed that report so thoroughly that it feels like memory. The mind does that. You've seen this in your line of work, I'm sure. People convince themselves. I am the opposite of those people. I am telling you where the seams are so you can see I have nothing to hide.

The window, then. Let me be precise about the window, because precision is what you want and precision is what I pride myself on. The window in the front room does not open, it's been painted shut since we bought the place, so obviously I misspoke, I meant the window in the kitchen, which is the room adjacent, and there is a draft that runs between the two when the kitchen window is up. A cross-breeze. I've felt it many times. That breeze is more than capable of lifting a curtain, and if you'd ever felt it you'd not doubt me for a second. I left the kitchen window open because it was a warm day. It was March, yes, I know, but we had a warm spell, you'll recall, everyone was remarking on it, and I opened the window before I left for my sister's and I thought nothing of it.

And I'll tell you frankly, I did think of it, once, on the drive out — I thought, did I leave that window open — and I nearly turned back. I want you to know I nearly turned back, because that is the kind of careful person I am. But my sister was expecting me and you don't turn a car around on that bridge for a window. So the responsibility, if we must speak of responsibility, is really the wind's, or the weather's, or God's, if you like, and I don't see how a person can be blamed for the weather.

Let me address something before you raise it, because I can see you looking at the fragments. You're wondering about the pattern of them. How they landed. I've thought about this too. The pieces are, I grant you, a little far from the sideboard for a straight fall. But things bounce. Ceramic bounces and skitters, it's the most unpredictable material, ask any restorer. My father collected these pieces and he used to say you could sneeze in the next county and one of them would leap off the shelf out of sympathy. They are delicate to a fault. So the distance means nothing. If anything the distance proves the fall was violent and sudden and entirely beyond the reach of human hands, since no human was present, since I was in Aldergrove.

I realize I've been saying Aldergrove and I want to make sure that's clear, because my sister actually moved last spring, she's in Cloverdale now, which is the same direction, more or less, same highway, so when I say Aldergrove I mean the general area, I mean out there, away from the house, which is the only point that matters. The exact municipality is not the point. The point is my absence, which is total and which two people can confirm, my sister and her neighbor, though the neighbor I'd rather you didn't trouble, he keeps odd hours and he's not well.

Here is what I think happened, and I offer it humbly, because I wasn't there. The warm air came in. The curtain lifted. But — and this is a detail I'd forgotten until this very moment, sitting here with you — there was also the matter of the cat. Not my cat. A cat that comes around. It gets in through that kitchen window when I leave it open, which is exactly why I so rarely leave it open, which is exactly why leaving it open that day was such an aberration and such bad luck. This cat is a menace. I've spoken to the neighbors about it. And I put it to you that the cat came in, and the wind and the cat together, well, you can imagine. I don't say the cat did it. I say the cat could have done it and the wind could have done it and between the two of them the vase never stood a chance, and I, personally, did nothing, could have done nothing, was doing nothing but sitting in a warm living room an hour away watching strangers sell furniture.

You're very quiet. I hope you don't think I'm rambling. I ramble when I'm upset and I am upset, that vase was my mother's — my father's — it was in the family, it doesn't matter whose, it was ours, and to lose it like this, to a window and a breeze and an animal, is a grief you may not appreciate if you've never owned anything old. I'm not asking for your sympathy. I'm asking for what's fair. The policy covers accidental breakage and this could not be more accidental. There was no one home to have an accident. That's almost a contradiction in terms and I want you to sit with it. An accident that happened to no one, caused by no one, witnessed by no one.

So write down whatever you need to write. Write down that I was in Cloverdale. Write down that the window was open, the front one or the kitchen one, whichever satisfies the form. Write down the cat if it helps. What I'd ask, what I'd only ask, is that you note somewhere, in your own hand, that I told you everything, plainly, the way an innocent person does, without leaving a single thing out.
