The Grey Heron, *Ardea cinerea*, is a bird of solitary and patient disposition, standing motionless upon the margins of slow water for the greater part of the day.

*Motionless. Sure. Tell that to the one at Aldbury that legged it across the shallows like a man who's remembered he left the gas on. Whoever wrote this watched a stuffed one in a case.*

It stands with neck retracted, presenting to the observer an aspect of grave contemplation, and strikes at its prey with a sudden extension of that neck too rapid for the eye to follow.

*Grave contemplation my foot. It's asleep. I've stood forty minutes watching one "contemplate" and it was digesting a frog the size of my thumb, and when it finally moved it fell over. A heron waking up is the least graceful thing in England. It's all knees.*

The heron is by nature a shy and retiring creature, and will not tolerate the near approach of man.

*Wrong again. There's one down at the boat hire that stands on the pontoon at half four every afternoon because the tea kiosk chucks out its bread ends then, and he'll take it off a child's hand and glare at you like you owe him money. Shy and retiring. He mugged a spaniel.*

Its cry, when disturbed, is a harsh and disagreeable *fraaank*, uttered as it rises upon broad and deliberate wings.

*This bit's fair. That's the one true sentence on the page. It does sound like a door that hates its hinges. First time I heard it I dropped my sandwich in the water and the heron, and I want to be precise about this, looked pleased.*

We turn now to the Kingfisher, *Alcedo atthis*, that jewel of our waterways, whose plumage combines the azure of the summer sky with the burnished copper of autumn.

*Oh here we go. Everybody's favourite. The word "jewel" is going to appear about nine times, watch.*

So brilliant is its livery that the fortunate observer, catching sight of the bird in flight, might well imagine that some living gem had been loosed upon the current.

*Two. And no. Nine times out of ten you don't see a gem, you see a blue smudge going forty miles an hour making a noise like a squeaky wheel, gone before your brain files it under "bird." Living gem loosed upon the current. He's never seen one either. He's copied this off a biscuit tin.*

The kingfisher nests within a tunnel excavated in the sheer bank of a river, a burrow which it keeps in a state of scrupulous cleanliness.

*Scrupulous cleanliness. It is a hole full of fish bones and it stinks. I've had my nose near one. Scrupulous. The bird lives in a midden and comes out looking like a jewel, which is honestly the most annoying thing about it and I say this with love.*

The male is distinguished from the female by the wholly black bill; the female bears a lower mandible of orange, as though she had dipped it in the sunrise.

*As though she had dipped it in the sunrise. Right, that's it, I'm keeping that one. That's the first line in this whole book with a bit of blood in it. Whoever wrote it must have been in love that week. Everything else reads like a man reciting a form. But that — dipped it in the sunrise — that's someone who actually looked. I forgive him the biscuit tin.*

---

The Dipper, *Cinclus cinclus*, alone among our passerine birds, possesses the singular habit of walking beneath the surface of running water in pursuit of aquatic larvae.

*Now this. THIS is the good stuff and he writes it like a tax return. A small brown bird WALKS ALONG THE BOTTOM OF THE RIVER. Underwater. On its little feet. Against the current. And the man calls it a "singular habit," like it's a fondness for cheese. Sir. It is a bird that has decided gravity and drowning are other birds' problems.*

It may be observed upon a midstream boulder, bobbing rhythmically, before entering the torrent without evident hesitation.

*The bobbing. He got the bobbing but he doesn't say WHY it's funny. It does about two hundred little curtseys a minute like it's apologising to the river before going in. Then it just — plops. No fuss. Comes out three feet downstream and does the curtseys again. I laughed out loud on a cold morning at a bird and a man walking his dog looked at me and I didn't care.*

The white breast of the dipper is thought to render it less conspicuous to its prey when viewed from below against the pale sky.

*"Is thought to." By whom, pal? You? Have you asked a larva? This is what I mean, the whole book's like this — some Victorian in a waistcoat who "thinks" things at the river and writes them down as law. I've watched dippers for six winters and I don't know why it's got a white front and neither do you and that's fine, we could just SAY that.*

---

The Mute Swan, *Cygnus olor*, is perhaps the most familiar and best-loved of all our river fowl, an emblem of grace, fidelity, and serene contentment.

*Three lies in one sentence. Grace, until it walks. Fidelity, until you ask the second brood. And serene contentment — I have been chased the length of a towpath by a serenely content swan and I am a grown adult who does not run from things.*

Popular belief holds that the swan is silent throughout its life and utters but a single song at the point of death.

*The swan is not silent. The swan hisses like a punctured lilo and grunts like a small furious pig and the wings, when it goes over, make a noise like someone shaking out a wet duvet with menace in their heart. Single song at death. It's been threatening me continuously and in good health for years.*

Pairs are believed to mate for life, and their devotion has long furnished poets with a ready image of constancy.

*Furnished poets. That's the giveaway, isn't it. Half this book isn't about birds at all, it's about what men wanted the birds to be — patient herons for the patient man, jewelled kingfishers for the romantic, faithful swans for the wedding speech. Nobody wrote down what the birds actually DID because the birds actually doing things would've spoiled the moral.*

The observer who studies these creatures with due patience will find in them a mirror of the finer human virtues.

*No he won't. He'll find a heron falling over, a kingfisher living in a stink-hole and looking flawless, a bird that walks underwater for a laugh, and a swan that wants him dead. Which is better. Which is so much better than virtues. I don't want a mirror, I've got one at home and it's discouraging. I want the thing that isn't me, going about its wet business, entirely indifferent to whether I've turned up. That's the whole appeal. That's the only reason to stand in a cold field before the light comes.*

*Still. "Dipped it in the sunrise." I'll grant him the one. Everybody's allowed one sentence where they forgot to be respectable and just told the truth about a bird. His was that. This, all of this, in the margins, in a hand nobody asked for — this is mine.*
