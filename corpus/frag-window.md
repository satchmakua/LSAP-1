The light going, and with it the edges of things—the window holding her reflection now more than the street, so that she stood half in the glass and half in the falling blue, unfinished, a woman drawn in pencil and then left. She thought: I will remember to. And forgot the end of it, the tail of the thought slipping under like a fish beneath a dock, silvered, gone. Below, a man in a grey coat crossed against the light and did not look up. Why would he. No one looks up. We keep our eyes on the wet stones and think we are the only ones thinking.

Mother used to say dusk was the hour the dead came back to look in windows, and she had not believed it then, seven years old with the jam on her wrist, but she believed it a little now, standing here, because who was that at the edge of her own face in the glass if not everyone she had ever been, pressed flat and looking in. The lamps came on one by one down the avenue, small yellow admissions. Somebody lighting them somewhere, or a switch, a hand, a clock that governed it. Time. She had meant to write the letter. The letter she had meant—

—and the man in grey is thinking of his supper, of the woman who will not have kept it warm, of the particular way the door will not quite close and the draught that comes under it in November like a cold tongue, he is thinking there is no money for the coal or there is money but not enough and the arithmetic of it goes round and round in him as he walks, three and six, three and six, and above him in a window a woman he will never see stands watching the street go blue and he does not know she pities him, he would not want it, pity, he has his pride like a stone in the pocket, he grips it—

The letter she had meant to write to Eleanor. Dear Eleanor. And then nothing, because what was there, after all these years, the years like coats hung one over another on the same hook until you could not find the first one, the one you had worn when you were happy, if you had been happy, and she supposed she had, there had been mornings, there had been a garden with the wet grass and the sound of a bee in the foxglove like a small motor of contentment, droning, droning, and someone calling her name across it—was it her name?—the syllables coming apart in the sun.

Now a girl below, running, her plait undone, running the way children run without destination only for the sake of the running itself, the body a joy, and the woman at the window felt her own knees remember it, the ache of it that was not ache but its opposite, the going-fast into the coming dark, careless of mothers. Careless. She had been careless. Or she had been careful, so careful, and it came to the same emptiness in the end, the careful and the careless both spilling their handfuls into the same grey water.

Six o'clock somewhere striking. She counted and lost count. Between four and five the whole afternoon had gone, had it, the whole of it, and what had she done, she had stood, she had watched the kettle, she had not made the tea, the kettle had grown cold and she had not noticed, the way you do not notice your own breathing until you attend to it and then can think of nothing else, the rasp and pull of it, the small machinery, and she attended now and felt herself absurdly alive at the window in the dusk, the blood going round, the lamps coming on.

The girl running is thinking nothing, or thinking in colours, the blue coming down and the wet gold of the lamps and the puddle she means to jump and does jump, splash, the triumph of it, and she is thinking Papa will lift me, he lifts me at the door, and she does not think this in words, she thinks it in the muscles of her arms already lifting, in the anticipation of the height, the world tilting up as she goes up, and this is the whole of her philosophy and it is complete and it wants nothing—

Eleanor at the seaside that year, holding her hat against the wind, laughing, saying something the wind took, and she had leaned in to hear it and not heard it, and now she would never hear it, the sentence gone forever into the salt air of nineteen-something, a whole sentence Eleanor had given her and she had let the wind have it, careless, and perhaps it had been the most important thing anyone ever said to her and she stood here forty years on trying to lip-read the mouth of a woman laughing on a beach that no longer existed. Was Eleanor even. She did not know if Eleanor even.

The dark comes up out of the ground first, she thought, it does not fall, that is the lie of it, it rises, it pools in the areas and the doorways and the gutters and climbs the walls like water rising in a well, and only the sky stays light a while longer, the last of it, a thin drowned green above the chimneys, and we call it evening and think it gentle, and it is not gentle, it is the tide of the whole turning world coming in over us and we let it.

Someone on the third floor opposite drew a curtain. A yellow square went out. Another mind, hidden now, another whole country closed off behind cloth—who lived there, what did they eat, whom did they fail, and she would never, it was the not-knowing that pressed on her tonight, the sheer number of the windows and behind each one a person as full as herself, as full of unfinished letters and lost sentences and gardens with bees, and all of them thinking themselves the centre, and all of them right, and all of them wrong. Multitudes. She was a multitude and so was the man in grey and so was the running girl and none of them would ever open to the others, they passed, they were lamps lighting one by one and then a hand drawing the curtain.

The man in grey has reached his door and it does not quite close.

The girl is lifted, is lifted, laughs.

Dear Eleanor, I have been meaning. All these years I have been meaning to tell you that on the beach, when the wind—

But the wind had it. It has it still. Somewhere the sentence goes on being blown along the shingle, out over the grey, and she lets it go, she opens her hand in the dark of the room and lets it go, and turns from the window at last, or does not turn, stands, the tea cold, the lamps below all lit now, the whole street a held breath of yellow in the risen dark, and she thinks: I will remember. And does not finish it. And the glass gives her back her face, dim, doubled, a woman half-drawn and left, looking in at herself looking out, and below, unseen, the lives go on closing their small yellow doors against the coming of the enormous ordinary night.
