"There's no such thing as luck," said Dobrin, licking the point of his pencil. "None. Zero. I'll mark that down as my opening position."

"You can't mark down your own position as a point," said Weft.

"I can mark whatever I like. It's my napkin."

"It's the tavern's napkin."

"Then it's neutral territory, and I claim it in the name of reason." He drew a tidy line down the middle and wrote D on the left, W on the right. "Now. Luck. There is only cause and effect, chains of it, going back to the beginning of time. What you call luck is only a chain you didn't happen to see."

"Beautiful," said Weft. "So the man who wins the lottery, the ticket in his pocket by the same causal chain as the man who loses—"

"Precisely. If you knew every atom, you'd know the winner in advance. No luck. Only ignorance dressed up in a bow."

"Then explain to me," said Weft, and here he leaned back so his chair complained, "why they call it a draw and not a calculation."

"Because people are lazy."

"Point to me," said Weft, reaching for the pencil.

"How is that a point? You made a joke about vocabulary."

"Language is the fossil record of what people actually believe. If luck were nothing, no tongue on earth would have a word for it, and every tongue does. That's not laziness. That's evidence." He put a firm mark under his own initial.

Dobrin snatched the pencil back. "By that logic ghosts exist, and dragons, and the honest politician. Every tongue has words for those too."

"Fair," said Weft, and, being an honest man, he crossed out his own mark. "Take it back. Language proves belief, not existence. One to nothing to you."

Dobrin, startled by the concession, made the mark almost gently. "Thank you. I didn't expect that."

"I'm not a fanatic. I'm a gambler. There's a difference. A fanatic thinks he's right. A gambler knows the odds and still feels the touch of the thing when it comes." He drank. "Let me put a case to you. Last spring I bet on a horse called Second Cousin. Three legs of a decent animal and one leg of an apology. Forty to one. It won by a nose because the favorite sneezed at the gate. Now. Cause and effect, you say. Fine. What caused the sneeze?"

"Dust. Pollen. A fly. A speck of the jockey's snuff."

"And what put the dust there, on that morning, in that nostril, at that instant?"

"The wind. The stable-boy's broom. The turning of the whole enormous world."

"So the turning of the whole enormous world," said Weft, spreading his hands as if presenting it on a plate, "arranged itself for the express purpose of paying me forty to one. And you say there's no luck. I say the universe conspired, and I have the winnings to prove it."

"That's not conspiracy, that's coincidence."

"And what is coincidence but luck with a longer word?"

Dobrin opened his mouth, shut it, and wrote a small reluctant stroke under W. "A point. A cheap one, but a point. Coincidence is the thing I mean, and I called it something else to make it sound respectable. One all."

"You keep giving me points I didn't strictly earn."

"I keep giving you points you argued me into and I'm too proud to deny. It's the same as earning. Now let me land a real one." He set the pencil crosswise between them like a duelist laying down terms. "Suppose luck is real, as you say. Then it can be had, and lost, and had again. Yes? A quantity."

"A tendency, more like. A weather."

"No, no, hold to it. If it's a weather, I've already won, because weather is just physics we can't predict, which is my whole argument. You said quantity earlier, when you counted your winnings. Pick one."

Weft chewed his lip. "A quantity, then. To keep my forty to one."

"Good. Then answer me this. Two men flip a coin a hundred times. One gets sixty heads, one gets forty. Is the first man luckier?"

"On that day, plainly."

"And if they flip a thousand times more, the split creeps toward even, as it must. Where did his luck go? Did it drain out of him like a bath? Was it lent, and called back in?"

"It—" Weft stopped. He looked at the coins of foam sliding down the inside of his glass. "It evened out."

"It evened out because it was never there. There was no substance to leave, because no substance arrived. You saw sixty heads and christened it luck, and the world, having no memory and no favorites, simply went on flipping." Dobrin drew a long confident stroke. "Two to one. And I earned that one clean."

"You did," said Weft. "Damn you, you did." He drummed his fingers. "But answer me back. You say the world has no memory and no favorites. Granted. Then luck is not in the world. Fine. I never said it was."

"You said forty to one—"

"I said I felt the touch of the thing. The thing isn't in the coin, Dobrin. It's in the man watching the coin. Luck is not a property of events. It's a property of attention. It's the shape a life takes when a person notices the times the coin came up for him and forgets the times it didn't."

"Then it isn't real. You've just described a mistake in bookkeeping."

"Have I? Is love real?"

"Don't."

"Answer the man. Is love real?"

"...Yes."

"And where does it live? Not in the world. The world has no favorites, you said so yourself. Love is a property of attention, the shape a life takes when a person notices one face among the thousands and forgets the rest. By your own knife, love is a mistake in bookkeeping. So either love is not real, in which case I'll want a very stern word with my wife about the last thirty years, or a thing can be real by living in the attention of a man. And if love can, luck can."

The tavern went on around them, indifferent, world-shaped, favoring no one. Dobrin sat very still. Then he picked up the pencil, and instead of marking his own side he made a slow, deliberate stroke under W, and beside it a second, and beside that a third.

"That's three points at once," said Weft. "You can't give me three for one argument."

"I can. It's my napkin."

"It's the tavern's."

"It's neutral territory," said Dobrin, "and I have just surrendered it. One for the syllogism, which was elegant. One for making me think of my own wife, which was cruel and effective. And one," he set the pencil down, "for being right, which I concede in full and will deny tomorrow morning."

Weft looked at the tally: two strokes for D, four for W. He reached over, took the pencil, and with great care crossed out two of his own until they were even again.

"Now what are you doing?"

"Evening it out," said Weft. "You gave me points I didn't earn, out of friendship. That's not luck. That's not attention. That's the one thing I can't argue is anything but real, and it doesn't belong on a scoreboard." He folded the napkin and put it in his pocket. "Two all. We'll call it a draw."

"A draw," said Dobrin, "or a calculation."

"A draw," said Weft, and signaled for another round, "and let the record show the favorite sneezed."
