The clock above the bar says nine forty-one. Derek taps the microphone twice. It thumps.

"Right," he says. "Tiebreaker."

Two tables are left. The Ferrets by the fire. The Dog and Bone by the window. Everyone else has turned their chairs round to watch.

"One question," Derek says. "Nearest answer wins. No conferring."

"There's four of us," says Maureen, from the Ferrets.

"One answer per table. Pick your writer."

The Ferrets look at each other. Maureen points at Gordon. Gordon shakes his head. She points again. He takes the pen.

At the Dog and Bone table Sandra already has the pen. She clicks it. Clicks it again.

"Sandra," says her husband. "Stop clicking."

She stops.

Derek unfolds a slip of paper. He holds it at arm's length. He pats his shirt pocket.

"Glasses," he says.

"Behind you," says the barman.

Derek turns. He finds them on the till. He puts them on. Nine forty-two.

"Here we go," he says. "In metres. To the nearest metre." He clears his throat. "What is the height of Nelson's Column, including the statue, in metres."

The room breathes out.

"Including the statue," Derek says again.

Gordon writes something. He covers it with his hand.

"That's not a fair one," says Maureen.

"It's on the card," Derek says. "Write your answers. You've got two minutes."

He looks at the clock. So does Sandra. So does Gordon. Nine forty-two still. The second hand crawls off the twelve.

"Fifty," whispers Maureen.

"Don't," says Gordon.

"I'm just saying."

"You're conferring."

"I'm breathing."

Gordon puts his other hand over the first hand. Sandra taps the pen against her lip. Her husband leans back. His chair creaks.

"Fifty-two," Sandra says under her breath.

"Sandra."

"I'm thinking out loud."

"Think in loud somewhere else."

At the bar a man lifts his pint. He drinks. He sets it down. The glass rings on the wood.

Nine forty-three.

"One minute," Derek says.

Gordon has stopped writing. He is staying still. His pen is on the paper. He isn't moving it. Maureen watches his hand. She watches the clock. She chews the inside of her cheek.

"You've put something," she says.

"I've put a number."

"What number."

"A number."

Sandra writes. She crosses it out. She writes again. Her husband watches her cross it out.

"Why'd you cross it out," he says.

"Because."

"Because what."

"Because I changed my mind."

"To what."

She doesn't answer. She writes.

A phone buzzes on a table near the dartboard. Everyone looks at it.

"Phones off," Derek says. "That's cheating."

"It's my daughter," says the woman.

"Turn it over."

She turns it over. The buzzing hums through the table, then stops.

Nine forty-four.

"Thirty seconds," Derek says.

Gordon looks at his number. He looks at the clock. He puts the pen down. He picks it up. He adds a mark. Maureen leans in.

"Did you change it?"

"I underlined it."

"Why."

"So it's clear."

Sandra holds her paper up to her chest. She stares at the middle distance. The fruit machine flashes behind her, no sound, just colours going round.

The second hand climbs. It passes the six. It passes the nine.

"Ten," says Derek. "Nine."

Sandra writes one last digit.

"Eight. Seven."

Gordon puts both hands flat on the table, on top of the paper.

"Six. Five."

"Sandra," her husband says.

"Four."

She stops writing.

"Three. Two. One. Pens down."

Two pens go down. One rolls. Gordon catches it before it falls off the table. Nine forty-five.

"Bring them up," Derek says.

Gordon stands. His chair scrapes. He walks the paper to the front. Sandra stands. She smooths her paper. She walks it up. They arrive at the same time. They hand the slips to Derek. They stand there.

"Sit down," Derek says. "I'll read them out."

They don't sit down.

"Sit down."

They go back. Gordon sits. Sandra sits. Her husband puts his hand on her knee. She moves her knee.

Derek holds the two slips. He looks at the back of the room.

"Answer's on the card," he says. "Let me get it."

He picks up the card. He turns it over. He reads it. His lips move.

"Right," he says.

The bar has gone quiet. The man with the pint doesn't drink. The fruit machine flashes. Nine forty-six.

"The Dog and Bone," Derek says. He reads their slip. "Fifty-two."

There is a small noise from the window table.

"The Ferrets," Derek says. He reads their slip. "Fifty-one."

There is a small noise from the fire table.

Derek looks at the card again. He looks at it for a long time. Long enough that Maureen shifts in her seat.

"Come on, Derek," someone calls.

"Give me a minute."

"You've had a minute."

Derek holds the card at arm's length. He tilts it to the light. He mouths a number.

"The correct answer," he says, "is fifty-two metres."

Nine forty-seven.

"Yes," says Sandra. She stands halfway up. Her husband pulls her back down.

"Hang on," Derek says.

"You said fifty-two."

"Hang on."

Gordon is on his feet. "There's a footnote," he says.

"There's no footnote," says Derek.

"There's small print. There's always small print on these."

"There's no small print."

"Turn it over."

Derek turns it over. He looks at the back. He turns it back.

"There's nothing on the back," he says.

"Read the whole thing," Gordon says. "The whole line."

Derek sighs. He reads it. "Nelson's Column. Height including statue. Fifty-two metres. Approximately."

"Approximately," Gordon says.

"Approximately," Maureen says.

"So it's approximately fifty-two," Gordon says. "Which means fifty-one is inside it."

"Fifty-two is exactly it," says Sandra.

"There's no exactly if it says approximately."

"That's not how it works," Sandra says.

"That's exactly how it works."

"Derek," says Sandra. "You said the correct answer is fifty-two."

"I read what's on the card."

"So fifty-two."

"Fifty-two is what's on the card."

Gordon is still standing. "And we said fifty-one, which is one metre off, and if it's approximate then the whole thing's a coin toss."

"It's not a coin toss," Sandra says. "You lost by a metre."

"It's approximate."

Nine forty-eight.

Derek holds up the card. "The card says the answer is fifty-two. Whoever wrote fifty-two is nearest to fifty-two."

"That's them," says her husband.

"That's you," says Derek, pointing at the window.

Sandra stands. Her chair goes back. It nearly falls. Her husband catches it.

"But approximately," Gordon says.

"Sit down, Gordon," says Maureen.

"It says approximately."

"Gordon."

He sits. He puts the pen on the table. He pushes it away.

"The Dog and Bone win," Derek says. "Fifty-two."

The window table cheers. It's a small cheer. There are four of them. The man with the pint claps twice. The fruit machine flashes.

Sandra hugs the person next to her. Her husband raises his glass.

Gordon looks at his paper on the table by Derek. Fifty-one, underlined.

"One metre," he says.

"One metre," says Maureen.

Nine forty-nine. Derek puts the card down. He picks up the microphone.

"Bar's open," he says.
