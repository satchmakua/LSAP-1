The car comes in.

It comes in low and gold and screaming, the nose dipping under braking, the front splitter kissing a shimmer of heat off the concrete, and then the whole machine seems to hang for one long undivided instant in the mouth of the box, suspended between the roar it was and the silence it is about to become, floating on its own deceleration like something poured rather than driven, the rear wing trembling, the exhaust ticking with the sudden withdrawal of throttle, so that every one of the eighteen men waiting sees it arrive not as an event but as a slow arriving, a growing, a filling of the marked rectangle on the ground with three-quarters of a ton of hot metal easing itself precisely onto the taped cross where the front axle must stop and does stop, exactly, perfectly, dead.

Wheels lock.

The jack man is already under.

His arm goes in beneath the nose the way a hand goes into a glove that has been worn every day for years, no search in it, only placement, the lever seated, the fulcrum found, and he throws his weight down and back and the front of the car rises in one smooth continuous lift that carries the two front tyres clear of the ground with the tyres still turning, still spinning off their last held velocity, so that for a fraction of a heartbeat the whole car balances on its rear axle and the raised front floats and the released wheels spin in open air, silver blurs going nowhere, and the light off the rims fans across the visors of the men crouched beside them like a struck match thrown wide.

Left front. Right front.

Guns hit nuts.

The scream of the impact wrench is not one sound but four sounds and then eight, layered and simultaneous, a hard bright shrieking that flattens the air, and inside that shrieking the single central nut on the left front is turning, is loosening, is off, and the gunman's thumb has already flicked the direction of the drive before the old wheel is off the hub, and the tyre-off man has both gloved hands on the used rubber and is pulling it toward his chest and turning his shoulders and it is gone, swung away and behind him into the arms of the catcher, who takes it against his body and pivots and it is out of the frame, out of the world, discarded, and where the wheel was there is now only the naked hub gleaming and the threaded stub and one half of one second of nothing.

New tyre on.

It arrives held at chest height by a man who has cradled it since the car was still a corner away, and he does not so much lift it as let it fall into place with a guided inevitability, the bore of the wheel sliding onto the pins, seating, home, and the gunman is already there before it has fully stopped moving, the socket dropping over the nut with a precision that looks like magic and is only ten thousand repetitions, and the trigger is squeezed and the nut spins down and bites and torques and the light on the gun goes green.

Green.

He throws his hand up.

Across the car the right-front man throws his hand up in the same instant, a mirrored gesture, two arms rising like the arms of a single body, and the jack man reads both hands, reads them without looking, feels them, and does not drop the car yet because the rears are not done, because the rears are still living their own separate seconds down at the other end of the machine where two more men are locked into the same choreography one beat behind.

At the rear the used tyre comes away.

It comes away hot, the surface of it greasy and blistered and blued, chunks of marbled rubber flaking off it as it moves, and the man who pulls it feels the weight of the heat through his gloves, a living warmth, and he turns with it the way a dancer turns, all the mass of it swinging through the arc his body has cut ten thousand times before, and it too vanishes rearward into the catcher's grip, and the new one is coming in already, already sliding, already home.

Gun on the rear left.

Gun on the rear right.

The shriek again, doubled, and the driver in the cockpit sits motionless with both hands high on the wheel and his eyes fixed forward on the line of light at the pit exit, and he is a still point at the centre of all this violence of purpose, breathing once, only once, in the whole span of it, his chest rising slow under the belts while around him the world is a storm of hands.

Rear left, green.

Rear right, green.

Hands up.

And now for one held moment that seems to spread and open and deepen far past the tenths of seconds it actually occupies, all four gunmen have their arms raised and all four wheels are torqued and locked and true, and the front jack man is watching the last hand rise, and the rear jack man is watching too, and the two jacks feel each other across the length of the car like two ends of one drawn breath, and the whole crew hangs in that raised-arm stillness the way the car itself hung in the mouth of the box at the start, a symmetry closing, a circle completing, the beginning and the ending of twelve seconds meeting in the middle of the machine.

Front drops.

The nose falls.

It falls fast and it falls hard, the tyres slamming down onto the concrete and taking the weight, the suspension compressing and settling with a single metallic knock, and the rear drops behind it a hairsbreadth later so that the car settles nose-then-tail like an animal lowering itself to run, and the two jack men are already flinging their levers clear, already stepping back and out, their bodies folding away from the car in a motion so practised it looks like retreat but is really release, a letting-go, a giving of the machine back to its own momentum.

The lollipop lifts.

The man in front raises the sign and the car is free and the light goes green above the exit and all of it, every part of it, arrives at once in the single unbearable simultaneity that the whole twelve seconds has been building toward, the freed wheels and the settled weight and the raised hands and the lifted sign and the driver's foot already coming down.

Clutch out.

The rear tyres bite.

And the car leaves.

It leaves the box in a surge that throws it forward out of stillness into speed, the fresh rubber gripping and squealing and gone, the exhaust snarling back up through its own octaves as the throttle opens wide, and the gold body pours itself out of the marked rectangle and down the pit lane and away, gathering, screaming, becoming again the thing it was before it came in, and the eighteen men stand in the sudden empty heat of its going with their arms still half-raised and their chests heaving in the first breath any of them has taken, watching it shrink toward the light, and somewhere among them a single voice breaks open into a shout of pure animal joy.

Twelve seconds.

Gone.
