The chair knows me better than I know it. I have sat in it so long that the cushion holds the exact map of my sitting, and when I lower myself down there is a moment of recognition, mine or its, I cannot say which, and then we are the same shape again. Outside the window the light is doing the thing it does. I mean it is late, or early. The plate on the table has crumbs on it. Someone ate here. I ate here, probably, because there is no one else, though I keep waiting for the sound of the kettle, which means someone must be making tea, which means.

I had a thought and it went out like a match. That happens. You strike it and there is a small flare and then the smell of the flare and then nothing, only the dark thumb-print of where the flame stood. I was thinking about the crumbs. No. I was thinking about the kettle. The kettle is cold. I put my hand on it to check and it is cold, so no one is making tea, so the sound I heard was not the kettle, so the sound I heard was.

Margaret would know. Margaret always knew where the thread went when I dropped it. She would pick it up off the floor without a word and put it back in my hand and I would go on, and I did not thank her because you do not thank someone for breathing near you, you just breathe near them back. Margaret. The name has a weight in the mouth like a smooth stone. I hold it there. I am not going to say what has happened to Margaret because if I do not say it then perhaps it has not yet happened, perhaps it is still in the part of time that has not arrived, and I can go into the kitchen and she will be at the sink with her back to me and her shoulders doing that small careful work, peeling, or wringing a cloth, and she will not turn around because she knows the sound of me, and I will not need her to turn around.

The light is doing the thing it does. Late, or early.

There was a boy. He had my face, or I had his, we passed the face back and forth like a ball in a garden. He ran and I could not run so I stood at the fence and he ran, and there was a dog, and the dog had a name I have kept somewhere in a drawer of me that I cannot now open. The boy is the one who ate the crumbs. No. The boy is old now. The boy is sitting in the chair and the chair knows him. I am confusing the boy and the chair and the crumbs. Let me put them in a row. The boy. The chair. The crumbs. There. Now they are in a row and I can look at them one at a time and none of them will run off.

But the boy ran off. That is the whole trouble with the boy. He was here and I turned to say something to Margaret about how fast he was getting, how you could not keep them small no matter how you held them, and when I turned back to the fence the fence was old and grey and the garden had grown up over the place where the dog was buried, and I was the one at the fence still, only my hands on the top rail were these hands, these spotted things, these things like the backs of leaves.

Who put these hands on me.

I want to be calm. Margaret says. Margaret said. There is a tense I keep missing, it is just to the left of the word, and I reach for it and my fingers close on the wrong one. Margaret is at the sink. Margaret is not at the sink. Both of these are sitting in the room with me and I cannot make them stop being both.

The clock on the mantel has a face but no hands, or it has hands but I cannot read them, the two are the same to me now, a face that will not tell me anything. Ten past. Ten past what. The light says late. The light says early. I have been sitting here since a morning I cannot locate and it may be the evening of the same day or it may be years, the crumbs would know, crumbs go stale in hours, I could touch one and know how long, but I do not want to touch them, they are the boy's crumbs, they are the last of him and I will not disturb them.

Someone will come. That is a thought I have on a schedule, though the schedule is torn. Someone comes. A woman with a soft voice and a card on a string around her neck, and she calls me a name that is my name, and she says the day of the week as though it were a gift, Tuesday, she says, laying it in my lap, and I hold the Tuesday and it does not help, because a Tuesday is only useful if you have the other days pressed against it, and I have lost the other days, they have slid off the table like the thread off my lap, and now there is only this one loose Tuesday, if it is Tuesday, and it may already be the Tuesday after or the Tuesday before.

Margaret. I am saying it now because the not-saying is worse. Margaret is gone. I felt the shape of the sentence coming from a long way off, the way you feel a train in the rail before you hear it, a humming in the metal of you, and now it is here and it is true and in a moment it will not be true again, it will slide back into the part of time that has not arrived, and I will get up and go and find her at the sink. This is the mercy of me now, that the grief cannot stay. It comes and stands in the room enormous and then it forgets why it came, the way I forget, we forget together, the grief and I, we are old men who meet on a bench and cannot recall what we were about to say.

The boy is at the fence. I will not turn around this time. I have learned my lesson about turning around. If I keep my eyes on him he cannot get old, he cannot become the hands, he cannot become the chair. The dog's name is. It is on the tip. It is nearly. It has four letters and a bark in the middle of it and Margaret used to call it in from the back step with a rising note at the end.

The kettle. Someone is making tea.
