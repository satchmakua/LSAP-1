The soup came first, which Marion had not expected. She had ordered the tomato because it was the safest thing on the menu, and now here it was, pale and skinned over, in a white bowl that had a chip on the rim she turned away from herself.

"They've changed the tables," Daniel said. "Used to be wood."

"Did they."

"Or maybe it was a different place. There was a place near here with wood tables. Could have been that one." He picked up his spoon and set it down again. "I don't know why I said that. I haven't been down this way in years."

"Ten," Marion said. "It's been ten."

He looked at her, and she watched him decide not to correct the number, though she could see he wanted to, could see the arithmetic starting behind his eyes and then being put away. "Something like that," he said.

The waiter came and asked if everything was all right, and they both said it was, quickly, in the way of people relieved to be given a small task. When he left, the silence he had interrupted was still there, waiting, and they had to take it up again.

"How's the soup," Daniel said.

"Fine. Warm."

"Mine's cold in the middle. They do that. Take it out of the fridge and don't heat it through." He stirred it. "You have to stir it."

"You should send it back."

"No," he said. "No, it's fine. I don't mind."

She remembered that about him, the not minding, the way he would sit with a thing that was wrong rather than name it and make it worse. Their father had been the same. She had spent years thinking it was patience and then understood it was something else, a refusal, a way of keeping the surface unbroken so that nothing underneath had to be looked at. She stirred her own soup, which did not need stirring, so that her hands had something to do.

"The drive wasn't bad," she said. "I thought it would be worse. There's usually roadwork on the bypass."

"They finished the bypass. Last spring."

"Did they. I don't come this way either."

"No," he said. "I suppose you don't."

The main course took a long time. They talked about the weather, which had turned, and about a cousin's daughter who had married someone neither of them had met, and about whether it would rain before Marion had to leave. Daniel said the sky looked like it. Marion said she had brought an umbrella just in case, and he said she was always the one who brought the umbrella, and there was a small warmth in it, a memory offered carefully, and she took it carefully.

"You were," he said. "You always had the umbrella and the plasters and the little bottle of water. Mum used to say you were born forty."

"She said that about you too."

"Did she." He looked pleased and then not pleased, as though the pleasure had led him somewhere he hadn't meant to go. He picked up his water glass. "How is your knee. You said on the phone. The knee."

"Better. It's the stairs that get it. I've been thinking about moving somewhere with no stairs."

"You'd sell the house."

"Maybe. Eventually. It's a lot of house for one person."

He nodded and did not say anything about the house being one person's, though the fact of it sat between them, the fact that it had been two people's and then their mother's alone and then, at the end, a house full of people who did not speak to one another over the arrangements. He had taken the clock. She had not wanted the clock but she had wanted him to ask her before he took it, and he had not asked, and that was six years buried under one sentence about stairs.

The plates came. Chicken for her, the fish for him, and for a while they ate, and the eating was a kind of mercy because it gave the quiet a purpose. She noticed he ate the way he always had, methodically, the vegetables first and then the rest, saving what he liked. She wondered if he still did everything the same, if a person could stay so consistent in the small things while everything large had shifted underneath.

"Is that good," she said, meaning the fish.

"It's all right. A bit dry."

"You always order fish and it's always dry and you always say it's all right."

He laughed, a short sound, surprised out of him. "I do, don't I."

"You could learn."

"I could." He put down his fork. "I don't, though. I keep hoping."

She thought there was more in that than fish and decided not to reach for it. If she reached for it he would fold up. She knew this. She had learned it over a lifetime and had forgotten none of it in ten years, and the not-forgetting was its own kind of grief, that she could sit across from someone she saw twice a decade and still know exactly which way he would flinch.

"The garden," she said. "You still have the garden."

"Some of it. I let the back go. It got to be too much." He turned his glass a quarter turn on the cloth. "I kept the roses. The ones from the old house. I dug them up when I moved and I didn't think they'd take but they did."

"Mum's roses."

"Grandad's, really. She just kept them going." He said it lightly and then his mouth did something, a small movement he controlled. "They flower late now. Later than they used to. I don't know if that's the roses or the weather."

"The weather," Marion said. "Everything's later now."

"Yes." He looked at the roses in his mind and she watched him look at them, and this was the closest they had come, this talk of a plant that had belonged to people who were dead, and she understood that they would not come closer, that this was the meeting held at the safe distance of flowers.

"I'd like to see them," she said, and was surprised she had said it.

He was surprised too. He looked at her and then at the tablecloth. "They're nearly done for the year," he said. "You'd want to come in June. Early June."

"June, then."

"If you're this way."

"Yes," she said. "If I'm this way."

It was not a promise and they both knew it was not a promise, and yet it was more than had passed between them in years, this June that neither would name again and neither would quite forget. He picked up his fork. She picked up hers. The rain, when it came, came softly against the window, and Daniel looked up at it.

"There," he said. "Told you."

"You did."

"You'll want the umbrella."

"I've got it," she said. "I always have it."

He smiled, and it held for longer this time, and neither of them said anything about the smile or about June or about the clock or about the ten years or the woman who had said they were both born forty and who was not there to say it now. The waiter came and asked about dessert, and they looked at each other, and she saw him wanting it to go on and not knowing how to make it, and she felt the same, and neither of them found the words.

"Just the bill," Daniel said. "Thank you."

"I'll get it," Marion said.

"No," he said. "Let me. Please."

She let him.
