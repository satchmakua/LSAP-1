Front hall. One coat rack, oak, four hooks, three of them empty. One umbrella stand containing two umbrellas, one of which does not open, and a wooden ruler marked in both inches and centimeters. One runner rug, brown, worn through in a strip down the center corresponding to the path from the door to the stairs. Twelve inches of that strip show the underlayment. One small table with one drawer. In the drawer: a church key, a rubber band, two AA batteries of unknown charge, a receipt from a hardware store dated eleven years prior, a single earring, and a key that fits no lock currently in the house. On the wall, one mirror, spotted at the corners. Behind the mirror, a rectangle of paint darker than the surrounding wall.

Living room. One sofa, three cushions, upholstered in a plaid of green and rust. The middle cushion is firmer than the two flanking it. Two armchairs that do not match the sofa or each other. One television, forty-two inches, mounted on a stand that once held a smaller television, as indicated by the bracket holes. One coffee table with a water ring near the left corner. Beneath the coffee table, one magazine dated the previous spring and one crayon, blue. One bookshelf, five shelves. The books number one hundred and forty. Sixty-one are hardcover. Of the paperbacks, thirty-two have cracked spines. Two of the books are the same title in two editions. One shelf holds no books but four framed photographs. In the first, four people stand on a beach. In the second, two of those people, older, stand in front of the same house whose contents are here recorded. In the third, a dog. In the fourth, nothing that can be identified, the print having faded to a uniform amber. One lamp, floor model, brass, the shade tilted seven degrees from level. One fireplace, brick, no fire. On the mantel: one clock, stopped at 4:20; one ceramic bird; one empty candlestick; one bowl of matchbooks from restaurants, nine of them, three from the same restaurant.

Kitchen. One refrigerator, white, one magnet in the shape of a strawberry, one magnet in the shape of Ohio, one photograph of a child held by the strawberry, one grocery list held by Ohio bearing the words milk, bread, and the beginning of a third word that stops after two letters. Inside the refrigerator: one jar of mustard, one jar of pickles containing two pickles, one bottle of ketchup nearly empty, one stick of butter, one box of baking soda for odor, opened. One stove, four burners, the front left burner cleaner than the others. One oven containing one baking sheet. Twelve cabinets. The cabinet above the stove holds spices, twenty-three jars, four of them the same spice, one of them empty but replaced. The cabinet to the left holds glasses, sixteen, of four different patterns, four of each, which is a coincidence. The drawer beneath holds silverware: eight forks, seven knives, nine spoons, and one implement whose purpose is not obvious. One dish towel hangs from the oven handle. One dish towel hangs from a hook. One dish towel is in a drawer. The floor is linoleum, patterned to resemble tile, worn to gray before the sink.

Dining room. One table, seats six, expandable to seat eight by means of a leaf currently stored against the wall behind a curtain. Six chairs. The seat of one chair has been reupholstered in a fabric that does not match the other five. One cabinet, glass-fronted, containing dishes not used, a set of twelve, gold-rimmed, one saucer chipped. One tablecloth in a drawer of the cabinet, folded, ironed, stained faintly in one quadrant. One chandelier, five bulbs, three working. Beneath the table, on the carpet, four indentations from the table legs and four more from an arrangement of the table now abandoned.

Master bedroom. One bed, queen, one mattress, one box spring. The mattress is more depressed on the left side than the right. Two nightstands. The left nightstand holds a lamp, a glass with a dried ring in the bottom, a book with a receipt used as a bookmark at page forty-eight, and reading glasses. The right nightstand holds a lamp, an alarm clock unplugged, and nothing else in view, though the drawer contains a deck of cards, a tube of hand cream, and a single cufflink. One dresser, six drawers. Three drawers empty. The others contain folded clothing: socks paired, undershirts, a sweater with a moth hole at the elbow. One closet. On the left rail, garments spaced evenly. On the right rail, garments spaced evenly. Between them, a gap of eleven inches where the rail is empty. On the closet floor, seven pairs of shoes and one shoebox containing letters, forty of them, bound with a ribbon, and one containing photographs, and one containing nothing but a lock of hair.

Bathroom. One sink. One mirror cabinet. Inside: aspirin, expired; a razor; three prescription bottles, two of them empty; dental floss; a thermometer. One bathtub with a shower. One shower curtain, opaque, spotted at the lower hem. One bar of soap, cracked. Two towels on the rack, one blue, one white, the white one grayer than the blue. One scale, its dial resting three pounds below zero. One window, frosted, that does not fully close.

Second bedroom. One bed, twin. One desk. One chair. The walls bear the marks of tape at eleven locations where posters were once affixed and later removed, the paint slightly lighter at each. One bookshelf, mostly empty, holding four trophies for participation, a shoebox of comic books, and one snow globe depicting a city, the water evaporated to half. One closet, empty but for wire hangers, sixteen of them, and one baseball glove on the shelf. The window looks out on the driveway.

Basement. One furnace. One water heater. One washing machine. One dryer, its lint trap full. One workbench along the far wall, its surface marked with rings and burns and one clamp bolted to the edge. Above the workbench, a pegboard, its outlines drawn in marker for tools, twenty-two outlines, fourteen tools present. Shelving holds paint cans, eleven, each labeled with the room in which the paint was used, some rooms named that no longer bear that color. One bicycle, adult, tires flat. One bicycle, child, tires flat. Boxes, thirty-one, labeled in marker: CHRISTMAS, TAXES, BOOKS, KITCHEN MISC, and seventeen labeled only with a year. One freezer, chest-style, humming, containing frozen vegetables, a wrapped cut of meat, and two ice cream cartons, one empty and returned to the freezer.

Garage. One car, absent, the space it occupied marked by a stain of oil. One lawn mower. One rake. One shovel. One coil of hose. One bag of fertilizer, opened, the top rolled and clipped. One shelf of jars containing screws sorted by size, four jars, and nails, three jars, and one jar of assorted items sorted by no principle. One tennis ball hangs from the ceiling on a string at the exact height to touch the windshield of the car that is not there. One calendar on the wall, turned to a month four years past, the days unmarked but for one, circled, on which nothing is written.

Attic. Insulation. Dust. One trunk, locked. One suitcase, empty. One string of lights, tangled. One chair with a broken leg. One box marked DO NOT THROW AWAY, its contents unexamined. One window, round, through which the yard is visible, and the fence, and the sign at the end of the driveway that reads SOLD.
