The form required only three things: my name, the reference number printed on the notice, and a signature confirming that I understood the terms of the transfer. I had all three. I had brought the notice, folded once and kept flat in a plastic sleeve so the ink would not smudge, and I had practiced my signature the night before, not because I had forgotten how to write it but because I wanted it to be clean, unhesitating, the signature of a person who understood the terms of the transfer.

At the counter the clerk took the form and read it with the attentiveness of someone reading a language they have only recently learned. Then she set it down and said the reference number was a provisional number.

I said the notice had told me to bring the reference number.

She agreed that the notice had said that. The notice, she said, was correct. But a provisional number could not be entered into the system as a final number, and the field on the form required a final number, and so the form, though correctly filled, could not be processed until the provisional number had been converted. She said this without impatience, as though describing the weather in a country I would never visit.

I asked how the number was converted.

She slid a second form across the counter. This one was for the conversion of a provisional reference to a final reference. It required my name, the provisional number, and a signature confirming that I understood the terms of the conversion. I filled it in there at the counter, using the pen chained to the shelf, and I signed it cleanly, and I slid it back.

She read it with the same care. Then she said the conversion could not be performed at this counter. This counter handled transfers. Conversions were handled at the counter along the corridor, past the pillar, the one with the blue sign. She was sorry. She said she was sorry in the way one says a word that has lost its meaning through repetition, and she was already looking past me to the person behind.

The counter with the blue sign had no queue, which I took for luck. The man there wore the same lanyard as the woman, the same pale identity card turned face inward so I could see only the blank reverse. He accepted the conversion form and read it and told me it was the right form. I felt something loosen in my chest. Then he told me that the conversion could not be processed today because conversions were batched, and the batch for this week had closed at eleven, and it was now half past eleven, and the next batch would open on the following working day, which, he added, was not tomorrow, because tomorrow the office observed a scheduled closure for the reconciliation of records.

I said I would come back on the day after tomorrow.

He said that would be sensible, and then he paused, and looked at the form again, and said that I should be aware the provisional number had a validity period. He turned the notice over and pointed to a line of small print I had not read, which stated that provisional numbers expired thirty days from issue. The number had been issued twenty-nine days ago.

I said then I must convert it today.

He said the batch had closed.

I said the number would expire before the next batch opened.

He agreed, still without impatience, that this appeared to be the case. He said that when a provisional number expired before conversion, the applicant should apply for a reissue. He produced a third form. It required my name, the expired provisional number, the reason for the lapse, and a signature confirming that I understood the terms of the reissue. I asked what I should write as the reason for the lapse, since the lapse had not yet occurred and would only occur because the batch had closed, which was not something I had done. He said that in that case I should write that the reason was administrative. I wrote *administrative*. I signed cleanly. He read the form and said it was correct and that the reissue could not be processed until the number had actually expired, because you could not reissue a number that was still valid. He suggested I return on the thirty-first day.

The thirty-first day was three days away. The office was closed on the day between. I did the arithmetic twice.

I asked, keeping my voice level, whether the reissued number would itself be provisional.

He said it would.

I asked whether the reissued provisional number would then require conversion.

He said it would, and that conversions were batched.

I stood at the counter for a moment. Behind me the hall was very quiet, the quiet of a large room with a high ceiling and few people, where footsteps announce themselves long before they arrive. A woman was mopping the floor near the entrance in long unhurried strokes, laying down a wet sheen that dried behind her almost at once, so that she seemed to be cleaning the same square of tile over and over without moving forward. The lights hummed. A screen above the counters displayed a number and a letter, and after a time the number changed and the letter did not, and I could not have said what either referred to.

I went back to the first counter. There was a queue now, and I joined it, and when I reached the woman I explained, as clearly as I could, that I was caught in a loop: the transfer needed a final number, the final number needed a conversion, the conversion needed an unexpired provisional number, the provisional number was about to expire, the reissue needed the number to have already expired, and the reissued number would be provisional again. I said all of this in order. I was proud, in a small dim way, of having held it all in my head.

She listened. When I finished she nodded, and I thought for a moment she would tell me it was all a misunderstanding, that of course such a loop could not exist, that a sensible exception applied. Instead she said that this was a known situation, and that for this situation there was a specific procedure. My chest loosened again, foolishly, the body learning nothing. She reached under the counter and brought out a form. It was a good form, thick paper, more official than the others, and at the top it said, in bold, *Application for Resolution of Circular Deferral*. It required my name, all relevant reference numbers, a description of the circular deferral, and a signature confirming that I understood the terms of the resolution.

I filled it in. It took some time to list the numbers. I described the loop in the box provided, and the box was not large enough, and I continued on the reverse where a line invited me to, and I signed at the bottom, cleanly, the signature of a person who understood the terms.

She read the whole thing. She turned it over and read the reverse. Then she set it down and aligned its edge with the edge of the counter, gently, with one finger.

She said the application was correct. She said applications for the resolution of circular deferral were reviewed by a separate office, and that the outcome would be communicated by notice. She said I should bring the notice, when it came, to this counter, and that it would contain a reference number.

I asked whether the reference number would be provisional.

She looked at me then, for what felt like the first time, and I saw in her face not cruelty and not even indifference but a kind of tiredness that went further down than any single day could account for, the tiredness of someone who had stood a long time on unstable ground and had stopped noticing it move.

She said she couldn't say. She said it would depend.

Then she looked past me, at the next person, and said, next, please.
