The best man stood up first, which was correct, because Denny had rehearsed it in the car park for forty minutes with a warm can and a folded piece of paper that had by now gone soft as a leaf.

"Right," he said. "So. Marcus. My oldest friend, though he's not the oldest thing in this room —"

"Cheeky," called Nan Pearl from the top table, delighted already, tapping her rings on her glass so it rang like a small alarm.

"— present company excepted, Pearl, you're a jewel — anyway, I've known Marcus since we were seven, and I want you all to know he was exactly the same then as he is now, except shorter and slightly less bald."

"He's not bald," said the bride, Josie, loyally.

"He's thinning strategically," Denny agreed. "He parts his hair the way a farmer irrigates a field. Now, I could tell you the story about the caravan, but Josie's parents are here, so instead I'll say only this: to Marcus and Josie, who deserve each other in the very best way. Cheers."

It was a good toast. It was a modest toast. It sat down at eleven minutes and thought itself finished.

It was not finished, because Nan Pearl was already rising, one hand pressed flat to the tablecloth for ballast, and the other lifting a sherry the colour of a stained-glass window.

"I'll be brief," she announced, which caused Marcus's father to close his eyes as though in prayer. "I'm eighty-nine, so I've earned the right not to be. I remember Marcus being born. I remember him being a very ugly baby — no, love, you were, you looked like a cross little apple — and I said to his mother, God rest her patience, I said, that one will marry well or not at all. And look." She swept the sherry toward Josie, sloshing a little onto the trifle. "He married well. He married a girl who laughs at his jokes, which is more than his mother ever managed. To the newlyweds — and to me, for predicting it."

The hall roared. Someone at the back, one of the cousins, banged the trestle table so the urn hopped.

That was when the other grandmother, Bibi, who had flown in from Karachi and had opinions about English sherry ("like drinking a raisin's apology"), unfolded herself from her chair with tremendous dignity.

"Pearl," she said, "has spoken beautifully." A pause, generous as a landmine. "For an amateur."

"Oh, here we go," said Pearl, thrilled to her marrow.

"I have been to two hundred weddings," said Bibi. "Perhaps three hundred. In my family we do not marry quietly; we marry with helicopters. So when I tell you this is a good wedding, a small wedding, a wedding in a hall that smells of other people's Tuesdays —" she gestured at the faint gymnasium ghost of the room "— you must understand it is high praise. Josie, beti, you are marrying into a family who says 'I'll be brief' and then talks until the moon comes up. You will be happy. You will also be tired. To the couple. And," she added, sitting with the finality of a closing door, "to helicopters, may they one day be affordable."

Marcus's father clapped so hard he lost a cufflink under the table and did not go after it.

By now a pattern had established itself the way weather does, and everyone in the hall could feel the next front coming in. The caterer, a broad calm man named Ferdy who was not supposed to speak at all, had begun to hover near the microphone with the untroubled certainty of a man who owns the plug sockets.

"While the puddings settle," Ferdy said, and the room laughed before he'd said anything, purely on the strength of his apron, "I want to say I have catered forty-one weddings this year and this is the first where the grandmothers have gone toe-to-toe like a title fight. I've had to keep the vol-au-vents warm through two speeches already. If there's a third grandmother in the building, please, I'm begging you, declare yourself now so I can plan the crumble."

"There's only two of us!" cried Pearl.

"Then we are safe," said Ferdy, "and the crumble is safe," and he bowed and retreated to his trays, and the applause followed him like a happy dog.

It was into this warm and orderly chaos that the door at the far end opened with the particular squeak of a door that has been oiled by nobody, ever, and in came Colin.

Colin lived at number nineteen. Colin had not been invited. Colin was wearing a tie.

"Don't mind me," said Colin, minding everyone tremendously, threading between tables with a plate he had located from somewhere and loaded with a startling percentage of the sausage rolls. "I heard the speeches through the wall. Lovely acoustics, these old halls. Built in nineteen-oh-six for the temperance society, ironically." He arrived at the front as though summoned. "I couldn't not come, could I. You can't hear that much joy through a party wall and just sit there with your feet up watching the darts."

"Colin," said Marcus, "how are you."

"Better for being here," said Colin, and — because the microphone was simply standing there, warm from Ferdy, radiating opportunity — he picked it up.

A ripple went through the top table. Josie put her hand over her mouth. Denny leaned back with the expression of a man who has bet on the underdog and watched it grow wings.

"I don't know the bride," Colin began, "and I only know the groom because he lends me his ladder and never asks for it back, which tells you everything about the quality of the man. And I know both these fine grandmothers by their voices, which come through my kitchen wall on a Sunday when they visit, and I want to say — Pearl, your laugh has kept me company through two winters and a divorce. Bibi, I've never met you, but you had me at helicopters." Bibi inclined her head like a queen accepting a county. "So here's my toast, from the man next door, the man behind the wall, the man who wasn't asked but came anyway because that's what walls are for — they're thin so you can hear when it's time to be neighbourly."

The hall had gone very quiet, quiet in the good way, the way a held breath is quiet.

"To Marcus and Josie," said Colin, lifting a paper cup of squash. "May your walls always be thin enough to hear each other laughing. And may I always be invited to the next one, so I don't have to break in."

And the hall did not break — it swelled. It went up the way bread goes up, all at once and warm. Pearl was on her feet again clapping and Bibi was on her feet too, and the two of them looked at each other across the ruined trifle and, in a moment nobody had planned and everybody would tell wrong for years, they linked arms. Ferdy came out of the kitchen with the crumble held aloft like a trophy because there was, blessedly, no fourth grandmother. Denny started the applause that turned into a song that nobody quite knew the words to but everybody sang anyway, filling the wrong bits with hums and the sort of la-la-la that means we are too happy to bother learning this properly.

Josie leaned into Marcus. "We should have him round," she said, meaning Colin.

"We should," said Marcus. "We really should."

And through the thin, generous, temperance-society wall, number nineteen sat empty and dark, its owner here, its darts abandoned, its kitchen listening to nothing at all, because for once every single voice it usually eavesdropped on was already in the room, singing badly, together, and glad.
