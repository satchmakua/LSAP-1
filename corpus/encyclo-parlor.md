**Correspondence** (also *the Postal Game*, *Silences*, regionally *the Long Wait*) is a parlor game for three to nine players in which participants exchange written messages under conditions of enforced misunderstanding. It is classed among the deferral games, a family distinguished from the wagering and the guessing games by the fact that no player may confirm whether they have won until all others have conceded that they have lost. The game is notable for having no formal end state recognized by all authorities, a feature its defenders regard as its principal virtue.

**Object.** The stated object is to be understood. This is not achievable. A subordinate and achievable object—to be misunderstood in a manner that other players find charming—governs actual play.

**Equipment.** A quantity of paper cut into uniform slips; one pencil per player, traditionally unsharpened at both ends so that a player must borrow the means to write and thereby incur an obligation (see *Debt*); an opaque vessel, historically a hatbox, into which slips are deposited; and a timekeeper's implement, most often a domestic clock turned to face the wall.

**Basic rules.** Play proceeds in *rounds of address*. At the opening of each round, a player nominates another as the *correspondent* by placing a blank slip before them. The nominator then writes a message on a slip of their own and deposits it in the vessel. The correspondent, who has not seen the message, writes a reply and deposits it likewise. Only after both slips are committed are they withdrawn and read aloud by a third party, the *reader*, who must read them in the order retrieved rather than the order written. Points are not scored. Instead, the assembled players award a *nod*, a silent inclination of the head, to whichever exchange produces the most fortunate accident of meaning. A player who receives seven nods is said to be *understood* and withdraws from further play, remaining at the table as a *witness* (compare the retirement conventions of the tile games, where the withdrawn player instead departs the room and is spoken of thereafter in the past tense).

**Fouls.** It is a foul to reply to what was actually written rather than to what one imagined was written. Enforcement is difficult, since intention cannot be inspected, and in practice the foul is called only against players who reply *too* aptly, aptness being taken as evidence of peeking. It is a graver foul to speak aloud during a round. The penalty for speech is that the offender must write everything, including their concession, for the remainder of the evening—a punishment that many players report enjoying.

**Origins (disputed).** Three accounts contend, and no reconciliation has been achieved.

The first, the *domestic account*, holds that the game arose in households where a married couple, no longer on speaking terms yet obliged to share a hearth, developed a system of slips passed through intermediaries, and that the guests, observing this, mistook necessity for entertainment and asked to be taught. This account is favored by those who find the game melancholy.

The second, the *epistolary account*, attributes the game to a postal district in which letters were routinely delayed by such intervals that all correspondence became a species of reply to forgotten questions, and residents, adapting to the condition, learned to answer letters they had not yet received. This account is favored by those who find the game clever.

The third account holds that there is no origin, that the game has always been played, and that any memory of learning it is itself a misunderstanding of the kind the game exists to celebrate. This account is favored by those who have won.

**Regional variants.** In the *northern* form, slips are written in the dark, and the reader is also blindfolded, so that the exchange must be reconstructed afterward from the players' contradictory memories of what was read. Scoring in the northern form is accordingly a matter of dispute conducted with great warmth.

In the *coastal* variant, a slip may be *returned unopened*, which counts neither as a message nor as a silence but as a third thing for which no player has ever agreed a name. Coastal play is regarded as advanced.

In the *mountain* variant, the vessel is passed out a window to a confederate who substitutes slips of their own devising, and the players' object shifts from being understood to detecting the substitution. Purists deny that the mountain variant is Correspondence at all and classify it instead among the deception games, alongside the shell diversions. Mountain players return the compliment by declining to recognize purists.

A minor *nursery* variant, played by children, dispenses with writing altogether: the child folds a blank slip, delivers it with ceremony, and both parties agree to have understood everything. This variant has the highest recorded rate of satisfaction and the shortest games.

**Tournament customs.** Competitive Correspondence is administered by regional associations that agree on almost nothing except the necessity of their own existence. A sanctioned tournament is played over the course of an evening called a *sitting*, and a player who arrives late is not admitted but is nonetheless counted present, on the principle that a correspondent's absence is itself a form of message.

The *seating draw* is conducted by lot, but the lots are drawn silently and the results are not announced; each player deduces their own seat by observing where the others fail to sit. The championship title is not a title but an obligation: the winner must host the following year's sitting and must, at that sitting, lose. This custom ensures that no player is champion twice, and that the office rotates through the disappointed. It is considered poor form to win with evident intention. The most admired champions are those who appear not to have understood that they were playing.

A *silence trophy*—an empty frame—is awarded to the exchange judged most beautifully misunderstood across all tables. The trophy is not displayed but is kept turned to the wall in the home of the recipient, in the manner of the game's own clock, so that the honor can be described but not seen.

**Notable scandal.** The best-documented controversy in the game's history is the *Affair of the Two Slips*, arising at a provincial sitting when a correspondent, replying to a nomination, produced a reply of such precise relevance to the nominator's message that the assembly fell silent. Under the rules the aptness constituted prima facie evidence of peeking, and the foul was duly called. The accused, however, maintained that they had not peeked, that the correspondence was a genuine accident, and that to penalize a true understanding as though it were a cheated one was to misunderstand the entire premise of the game.

The dispute divided the associations. One faction held that understanding, if genuine, is the game's fulfillment and must be honored. The opposing faction held that genuine understanding is the game's death, and that a player who truly understands has ceased to play and should be gently escorted from the room. The matter was submitted to arbitration, and the arbitrators, after long deliberation, delivered their finding on a folded slip which, when opened, proved to be blank. Both factions claimed the ruling as their own. The accused was neither cleared nor condemned but was declared *understood*, awarded the seven nods retroactively, and required to withdraw as a witness—a resolution that satisfied no one and is therefore generally regarded as correct.
