THE CONDUCTOR, forty-one years on the line:

Ten past midnight, or near enough, we stopped. Not braked hard — I want that understood, we did not brake hard, whatever the woman in coach three will tell you. It was a gentle setting-down, the way you'd lay a sleeping child in a cot. Signal fault, I assumed, and I still assume it. You get them out past the reservoir where the wires sag. I went the length of the train with my lamp, and everything was ordinary. People wanted to know things and I told them what I knew, which was nothing, and I told it kindly. Three hours. I'd put it nearer two and three quarters, but I won't quarrel over minutes with folk who slept through most of it. When we moved again it was like nothing had happened, because in truth nothing had. A train stopped in a field. That is all any of you saw. That is all there was.

THE WOMAN IN COACH THREE:

He's lying, or he's forgetful, and I don't know which is worse in a man his age. It was a violent stop. My tea went straight off the little table and scalded my wrist — look, you can still see the mark, though it's faded now, everything fades. We were flung. And it was not midnight. My watch said quarter to one and my watch has never lost a second in twenty years, it was my mother's. There was a smell, too, when we halted. Like a struck match, sulphur, held under your nose. Nobody else admits to it but I have a sensitive nose and I trust it above any man's lamp. And there were lights out in the dark. Low, orange, moving against the way the wind was blowing the grass. Moving against the wind, I said, and the young man beside me laughed, but he wasn't looking, he was looking at his telephone the whole time, so what would he know.

THE YOUNG MAN WITH THE TELEPHONE:

Yeah, no, I saw everything, I was awake the whole time, unlike basically everyone. There were no lights. There was nothing. I had my phone out because there was no signal — zero bars, which never happens on that stretch, I commute it — so I was sort of waving it around trying to catch something and I could see out the window fine, and it was black. Flat black field, one dead tree, no orange lights, no lights of any kind. The lady next to me kept gasping like she was in a film. Honestly it was so boring I timed it. Fifty-eight minutes. I've got the screenshots, I took one when we stopped and one when we started, and it's fifty-eight minutes, not three hours, people are so dramatic. There was a noise though. Right at the end, before we moved. Like something big turning over underneath us. I felt it in my teeth.

THE DRIVER:

I don't discuss it. I gave my statement to the company. Signal was green the whole time — that's the part nobody prints. Green. I had no reason to stop and I did not choose to stop; the train stopped itself and would not answer me. I sat with my hands on the controls for what the company calls a period of technical anomaly, and I looked at a green light burning steady in the middle of an open country where there was no earthly reason to keep me, and I did not get out of my cab, and I will not tell you why I did not get out of my cab. When she released me — the train, I mean, I say she out of habit, don't make anything of it — I drove on to the terminus and I have not driven that route since and I do not intend to. That's my statement. It hasn't changed and it won't.

THE CHILD, aged nine, as recorded:

There was a man in the field waving. I saw him from the window when Mummy was asleep. He had a lantern like the conductor's but greener and he was waving it slow, like this, hello, hello, hello, and I waved back but he didn't wave the ordinary way, he waved like he wanted us to come out, and I nearly did, I put my hand on the door thing but it was too high. He was standing where there wasn't any ground. That's the part I keep saying and everyone keeps not listening. He was up a little bit in the air, standing on nothing, waving. And then the train went and I watched him get small. He wasn't sad. He just kept waving till I couldn't see. I think he does it every night for the trains and nobody ever comes.

THE STEWARD FROM THE BUFFET CAR:

Look, I'll be straight because I've no dog in this. We stopped, fine. But I want to tell you what happened in my car specifically, because that's what I know. My stock went off. All of it. The milk was fine at eleven — I'd checked, I always check — and when we halted it turned, curdled solid, every carton, in the space of the stop. Sandwiches gone grey. The ice in the machine melted clean through and there was warm water pooling and I mopped it in the dark because the lights had dimmed to nothing, running off the batteries. Three hours of that. And here's what I'll swear to: I looked at the clock behind the till, the electric one, and it was running backwards. Slow, but backwards. Second hand going the wrong way round. I watched it a long while because there wasn't much else to do. When we moved off it snapped forward again and caught up, tick tick tick, like it was ashamed of itself. You can call the milk what you like. Milk doesn't lie to me. I've handled milk thirty years.

THE PASSENGER IN THE LAST CAR, who slept:

I'm sorry, I've nothing for you. I was asleep before we left the city and I woke at the terminus with a crick in my neck and a station announcement telling me to mind the gap. If we stopped, I stopped with it. I heard afterward there'd been a delay, and I thought, good, that's why I got such a rest. People on the platform were pale and talking fast, clustered, the way people cluster after a wedding or a crash, and I felt I'd missed something I'd not been invited to. That's the whole of my night. A field I never saw. I keep thinking I ought to be frightened, hearing them all, and instead I feel left out, which I know is the wrong feeling. But there it is. I slept through the only strange thing that ever happened to me, and I'll never know if I was lucky.

THE CONDUCTOR, again, near the end of his shift:

I've heard what they're all saying now. The lights, the man, the clock, the milk. I'll say it once more and then I'm going home. A train stopped in a field for a while, and then it went on. That's the truth of it, the boring middle of it, and the boring middle is nearly always the truth. But I'll tell you one thing I left out, because it's small and it means nothing. When I walked the length with my lamp, every single passenger who was awake was looking out the same window. The left side. All of them, quiet, faces turned the same way, toward the field. And there was nothing out that window. I checked. There was nothing there at all.
