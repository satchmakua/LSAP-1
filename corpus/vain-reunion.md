I had been dreading the reunion, if I am honest, not for my own sake but for theirs, because I know the effect my presence tends to have on a room, the way conversation organizes itself around me like iron filings around the obvious magnet, and I did not want the evening to become, as these things so often do, simply a procession of people wanting a moment with me. But one has obligations. One cannot deprive people of the thing they have driven forty minutes and paid for parking to experience. So I went.

I arrived a little late, which was deliberate, because an entrance loses its shape if the room is not already warm and expectant, and I will say that the moment I came through the double doors into the gymnasium I felt every head turn. Several people, I noticed, immediately stopped talking. A hush, you could call it. One woman near the punch table put her hand to her mouth. I gave a small wave to the room in general, a gracious sort of wave, the wave of a person who understands he cannot possibly greet everyone individually and wishes to distribute himself fairly, and I made my way toward the center of things, which is always, wherever I go, wherever I happen to be standing.

Now, the interesting thing about a crowd that badly wants to speak with you is that it often disguises its eagerness. This is a subtlety that would escape a less observant man. Marjorie Feldstein, for instance, whom I had not seen in some thirty years, saw me approach and turned, with what I can only describe as a kind of overwhelmed haste, to speak very intently with the man beside her. She did not look back at me even once. You see the psychology there. She wanted so much to talk to me that she could not trust herself to do it calmly, and so she had to steady her nerves first by pretending to be absorbed in something else. I understood completely, and I did not press her. I am a patient man with the shy ones.

The food, I should mention, was buffet-style, which I have always found to be a democratizing and pleasant arrangement, and I positioned myself near the carving station because it is a natural gathering point and I wished to be available. People did gather. A whole cluster of my old classmates stood not four feet from me the entire evening, laughing, reminiscing, gesturing, clearly hoping I would insert myself, and out of respect for their fun I let them work up to it. They never quite did. Twice one of them, Dan Okafor I think, glanced in my direction and then said something to the others that made them all laugh very hard, and I laughed too, from where I was standing, to show that I was in on it, that I was a good sport, that the great man could take a joke, though I confess I had not heard the joke and it may not have concerned me at all, though of course it did.

There was a slideshow. Old photographs projected onto a sheet at the front of the room, and I waited with some anticipation for my own face to appear, given the number of activities I had been central to, the plays, the debate team, the time I nearly organized a recycling drive. I appeared once. In the background of a photo of the marching band, slightly out of focus, half of me, cut off at the shoulder by the frame. But I understood this immediately as a compositional choice, a deliberate restraint on the part of whoever assembled the slideshow, because to show me fully and in focus would have unbalanced the entire retrospective, would have made every other photograph feel small, and so they had, tactfully, given me only a corner. It was, in its way, the greatest tribute of the night. I nodded at the screen. A man next to me said, "You okay?" and I said I had never been better, which was true.

I want to be clear that I did not spend the evening waiting to be approached, because that would suggest a neediness entirely foreign to my nature. Rather, I circulated. I circulated generously. I approached a group discussing someone's grandchildren and I said, "And what have you all been telling people about me over the years?" — a light opener, a warm invitation for them to share the anecdotes I knew they must have hoarded — and there was a pause, a beautiful, full pause, the kind of pause that only happens when people are so moved by a question that they must gather themselves, and then a woman said, "Sorry, who are you again?" which is exactly the sort of thing people say when they are pretending not to be starstruck. It is a defense mechanism. I have read about this. I told her my name and she said "Oh" in a tone of dawning recognition and turned back to the others, and I felt I had made her whole evening, giving her that small private thrill of having spoken to me and then being able to affect that she barely remembered.

The organizers had set out little name tags, the peel-and-stick kind, and I found mine near the end of the alphabet in a stack that was, I noted with satisfaction, largely untouched — meaning most people had not needed a tag to be recognized, and neither, obviously, did I, though I wore mine anyway to make the shy ones more comfortable. There were a great many name tags remaining, in fact, far more than the number of people present, which I took as evidence that many had planned to come chiefly to see me and had been too overcome at the last moment to actually walk in. The parking lot had been quite empty. Overwhelmed, all of them, sitting in their cars.

Toward the end I gave what amounted to a short address, standing near the DJ table, offering a few words of gratitude and reflection to those nearby, perhaps five or six people who had not yet drifted off, and I spoke movingly, I thought, about time and memory and the bonds that outlast decades, and one of them, an older gentleman I did not recognize, actually left in the middle of it, which struck me as enormously poignant — a man so affected by my words that he could not remain in the room and had to go compose himself in the hall. I did not follow him. Some emotions are private. I let him have his dignity.

When the lights came up and people began collecting their coats I stood by the exit so that everyone would have to pass me, so that no one would be denied a final farewell, and they filed past, each of them, and I said "goodnight" and "wonderful to reconnect" and "don't be a stranger" to each one, and to my great gratification not a single one of them lingered or made a scene or begged for my number, which showed a restraint, a collective dignity, that frankly moved me, because they understood, all of them, that a man like me cannot belong to any one of them, that I must be shared, that I must go back out into the world where I am needed. I drove home very content. It had been, everyone would surely agree, entirely about me.
