Behold the green, that emerald parliament of the well-earned afternoon, mown into stripes so scrupulous that the very grass seemed to have submitted itself to discipline, where upon this Tuesday of tolerable wind and generous light the assembled retirees of the Municipal Bowling Association gathered as the Achaeans once gathered upon the windy plains, though these came not with spear and shield but with cardigans against the treachery of shade and thermoses of a tea grown lukewarm through long postponement of the decisive moment. The sun, that ancient umpire who has adjudicated the fates of empires and now condescended to preside over the fates of pensioners, hung low and partial in the western quarter, casting the long shadows of two contending champions across the rink like the shadows of colossi, when in truth the colossi measured, the one, five foot four in his orthopedic shoes, and the other, five foot six only if you granted him the lift in his heel that he did not admit to and no one had the cruelty to mention.

Now had the tournament, spanning three tender weeks of April, winnowed the multitude of the hopeful down to these two: Harold, called the Unshakeable, whose composure at the mat was likened by admirers to the calm of deep water and by detractors to the sluggishness of a man half-asleep; and Reginald, surnamed the Slice, for the treacherous curving bias he coaxed from every bowl, a man whose forearm, though seven decades old, still delivered the wood with the cunning of a serpent descending. Between these two the score stood knotted at twenty and twenty, and a single end remained to crown the victor, so that the whole green held its breath as the sea holds its breath in the trough before the swell, and the ducks upon the ornamental pond, sensing perhaps the gravity that had descended, ceased their squabbling over a sodden crust and drifted into what could only be called respectful formation.

Reginald bowled first, and his wood departed his hand as departs the falcon from the falconer's fist, swinging wide upon its outward arc as though disdaining the jack entirely, feinting toward the ditch and the ruin of the gutter, before the bias took hold and drew it home, curving, curving, a green planet obedient at last to an invisible sun, until it kissed the little white jack with a click as soft and as final as the closing of a jeweler's case, and came to rest a mere thumb's breadth from the target, so near that a gasp went up from the folding chairs and old Mrs. Pemberton dropped a boiled sweet that rolled, unregarded, beneath the refreshment table into the dominion of ants.

Then rose Harold, the Unshakeable, and he did not hurry, for he was a man who understood that time, having taken so much from him already, might now be made to serve, and he wiped his palm upon the seat of his trousers with the deliberation of a priest cleansing his hands before the sacrifice, and he took the mat, and he sighted along the rink with one eye narrowed like a mariner reading a coast, and he lowered himself upon the ache of both knees into that crouch which the physiotherapist had expressly forbidden, and he sent his final bowl forth into the world. It ran true and it ran fast, too fast, the murmuring assembly agreed, a bowl born of desperation rather than art, and it seemed for the length of a long-drawn breath that it would blunder past the jack and squander everything in the pointless outfield of the rink—but then the bias, that faithful genius that lives coiled within the wood as fate lives coiled within the thread the three sisters spin, laid its hand upon the sphere and turned it inward, and it slowed, and it slowed, and it came to the jack, and it came to Reginald's bowl, and there in the deepening amber light it settled against neither one nor the other clearly, so that no eye in that congregation of failing eyes could pronounce which wood lay nearer to the coveted white.

A silence fell then such as had not fallen upon the green since the Great Drainage Dispute of a bygone summer, a silence into which even the traffic beyond the hedge seemed to hush its distant thunder, and every soul present bent forward, and the question passed from lip to lip like a torch in a relay of runners: which was nearer? which had won? And into this suspended hour stepped the appointed Marker, a man named Cyril upon whom the whole apparatus of destiny now devolved, and he knelt, and he produced from the sacred pouch at his belt the measure—that little coiled string with its brass caliper, humble instrument, yet in this moment invested with an authority to rival the scales in the hand of blind Justice herself—and he extended it toward the jack.

But here arose the crisis, here the peripety upon which all tragedy and all triumph turn, for as Cyril applied the string, Reginald the Slice cried out that the jack had been jostled, that Harold's very bowl in its arriving had shouldered the white a hair's breadth toward Harold's advantage, and thus the measurement itself was corrupted at its root, a measurement of a false position, an oracle consulted after the god had been bribed. And Harold the Unshakeable, shaken at last, rose to the fullness of his five foot four and declared that the jack lay precisely where the honest physics of the game had deposited it, and that to allege otherwise was to slander the very grass beneath their feet.

Then did the assembly divide as a city divides in faction, the partisans of Reginald muttering of justice betrayed and the partisans of Harold invoking the rules in their green-bound book, and the dispute swelled until it seemed the whole park had ground to its foundations to a halt: the jogger froze mid-stride upon the perimeter path; the mother arrested her pram; the man with the metal detector lowered his headphones; and even the ice-cream van, that mobile shrine to summer, cut its tinkling anthem in respect or in confusion. And Cyril, poor Cyril, mediator between mortals and the truth, held the caliper aloft and trembled, for he alone must speak the verdict, and the verdict of a Marker, once given, is as the verdict of the Fates, irreversible, thundering down the generations of the club's long minute-book.

He measured Reginald's wood: four and one-eighth inches from the jack, he intoned. He measured Harold's, and his hand shook, and the whole green leaned as a field of wheat leans before the wind, and he read: four and one-sixteenth inches. By that hairsbreadth, by that trembling sixteenth of an inch, that margin thinner than the difference between one heartbeat and the next, was Harold the Unshakeable made the champion of the Municipal Green, and the applause broke like surf upon a shore, and Mrs. Pemberton wept into a paper napkin, and Reginald, defeated but noble, extended his cunning right hand in the ancient clasp of the vanquished, and the sun, satisfied, slipped at last behind the hedge, and the ducks resumed their squabbling, and the ice-cream van, released, sang once more its silvery hymn unto the long and golden evening.
