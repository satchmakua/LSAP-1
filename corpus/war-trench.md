The dark before was worse than the dark during. In the trench the men breathed and did not speak, and the breathing was a wet animal sound passed from mouth to mouth down the line, and every one of them was counting though none of them knew the number they were counting toward. Rust flaked from the fire-step. A rat moved along the parados, fat and unhurried, and no one killed it. The sky to the east had begun to bruise, a thin grey seam splitting the black, and that seam was the thing they all watched because when it opened wide enough the whistle would come.

Water in the trench bottom. Cold to the ankle, cold through the wrappings, cold up into the bone where it stayed. A boy at the end coughed once and swallowed it back. The wire out in front of them stood in the last dark like a hedge of thorns, and beyond the wire the ground fell away into shellholes full of black water, and beyond that was the other line where other men breathed the same wet breath and waited for the same seam of light. Someone was praying. It came in fragments, the tail of a sentence, a name repeated, and then it stopped, and the stopping was louder than the words.

Harker checked his rifle for the ninth time. He did not need to. His hands did it without him, working the bolt back and forward, feeling the oil and the grit, and he watched his hands do it as though they belonged to another man who had died some time ago and left the hands still moving. Down the line an officer moved crouched with the whistle already at his lips, saying nothing, only touching each man on the shoulder as he passed, and the touch went through them like current, one after another, and each man straightened and set his boot against the ladder rung and felt his own heart come up hard into his throat.

The barrage started at four. It came from behind them, from their own guns, a wall of noise that had no edges, that filled the sky and the ground and the chest all at once, and the earth jumped under their feet. Out ahead the enemy wire and the enemy line leapt in fountains of black and orange, dirt hurled up in slow columns that hung and fell, and the noise was so total that it became a kind of silence, a pressure with no sound left in it. Harker felt it in his teeth. He felt it in the water at his ankles, which shivered and would not be still. A man beside him was shouting something into his ear and Harker could see the mouth working and hear nothing, nothing at all, only the great flat roar that was the whole world now.

Five minutes of it. Ten. The light came up grey and sick behind the smoke. Harker's hands had stopped moving. His mouth was full of the taste of the barrage, cordite and iron and turned earth, and his bladder was tight, and he thought with a clarity that frightened him of a kitchen table and a window with rain on it and none of it was real, none of it had ever been real, there was only the ladder rung under his boot and the grey light and the whistle that had not yet come.

Then the guns lifted. The wall of noise walked forward, away from them, a hundred yards, two hundred, chasing something ahead into the smoke, and in the space it left there was a ringing hollow, and into that hollow the whistle went up shrill and small and terrible.

They climbed. Harker's body climbed before his mind agreed to it, hands on the parapet, boots kicking in the loose earth, up and over the sandbags slick with dew, and the air out of the trench was cold and wide and full of the smell of the whole broken country. He was standing on the top of the world. He was standing where no man was meant to stand. All down the line they were rising, dark shapes coming up out of the ground like the dead answering a call, and they went forward at the walk because that was the order, at the walk, dressed by the right, into the smoke.

The other line was not dead. It woke.

The first machine gun opened from the left and Harker heard it under the ringing, a stitching sound, quick and dry, and the man three down from him folded without a cry and went into the mud and did not move. Then another gun on the right. Then the whole front of the enemy line was chattering and the air was full of it, the air itself was made of it, a horizontal sleet you could not see and could only feel where it passed, and it passed close, it tugged at his sleeve, it plucked the cap from the head of the man in front. Harker walked. He did not know why he walked. His legs carried him at the ordered pace over ground that heaved with the fall of shells, past a crater with a man in it holding his own stomach in both hands and looking up with an expression of enormous surprise.

The wire. Their own barrage had not cut it. It stood in coils and Harker came to it at the walk and saw men caught in it, hung in it, jerking as the bullets went through them, and he veered right along its edge searching for the gap and there was no gap, there was only the wire and the guns finding the men who bunched against the wire. He threw himself down. Mud came up around his face, into his mouth, cold and full of the taste of iron, and above him the wire sang where the rounds struck it, a high thin plucking note, again and again.

A man crawled up beside him. It was the boy who had coughed. His helmet was gone and there was blood in his hair and he was laughing, or he was crying, the sound was the same, and he put his mouth to Harker's ear and said a word that was lost. Harker took his arm. They went along the wire together on their bellies, and the mud sucked at them, and shells came down among the coils and threw wire and men and earth into the grey air, and Harker felt something strike his pack and jolt him flat and did not stop.

There was a gap. A shell had cut it, a breach where the coils were flung apart, and Harker went through it on hands and knees with the boy behind him, and beyond it was the open slope up to the enemy parapet, thirty yards, twenty, and the ground was full of the fallen and full of the noise, and Harker got to his feet because you cannot cross open ground on your knees, and he ran. He ran with his rifle held low and his mouth open and no sound coming out of it that he could hear. The parapet grew. He could see the sandbags. He could see the black slot of the loophole and the flicker in it, the small quick flame, and it was pointed at him, and he ran toward it because there was nowhere left in the world to run but into it.

The boy was no longer behind him. The seam of light had opened all the way across the sky, and it was full morning now, grey and merciless, and the guns did not stop.
