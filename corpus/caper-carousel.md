The plan, as Desmond explained it in the parking lot of the Skylark Diner over the last of the coffee that the night waitress had stopped topping up an hour before out of a wordless refusal to participate in whatever the three of them were so obviously up to, had exactly four steps, each of which he had labeled with a letter of the alphabet and written on the back of a placemat, and each of which he had rehearsed aloud until Nora threatened to leave. It was foolproof. He said this twice.

Step A was the fence.

The fence around the old fairground warehouse was chain-link and eleven feet tall and topped with the kind of curled wire that exists chiefly to look discouraging, but the town, in the same fit of thrift that had led it to auction off the carousel in the first place, had never repaired the section behind the electrical shed where a delivery truck had backed into it in 1994 and simply bent it into a doorway shaped like a man walking, and so Desmond, who had known about this gap for thirty years and had been waiting his whole life for a reason to use it, ducked through sideways with the reverence of a pilgrim entering a shrine and immediately caught his belt loop on a barb and hung there. Suspended. Announcing, to the parking lot, that he was fine.

Nora went through next without incident because Nora did most things without incident, and Cliff went last, and Cliff, who was seventy-three and had replaced both knees and considered this a personal triumph of engineering that he mentioned in nearly every conversation regardless of relevance, lowered himself through the gap one titanium joint at a time while narrating the procedure like a man landing a plane, and then he was in, and he stood up, and he clapped his hands once, softly, the way you do when a thing you doubted has come to pass.

Step B was the window.

The warehouse had a row of clerestory windows that ran along the top of the north wall, and one of them, the fourth from the left, had a broken latch that Desmond's nephew, who did seasonal maintenance before the seasonal maintenance was itself eliminated, had described to Desmond in some detail at Thanksgiving under the impression that he was making small talk. To reach the fourth window from the left they needed height. For height they had brought, folded into the trunk of Nora's Buick and reassembled now against the wall in the dark with a great deal of pinched fingers and whispered blasphemy, an aluminum orchard ladder borrowed from Cliff's brother-in-law under a pretext so elaborate that Cliff had begun to believe it himself. The ladder was tall enough. The latch was broken exactly as promised. The window swung inward on a hinge that shrieked like a betrayed animal, held its note, and stopped.

They froze. A dog barked somewhere over on Merchant Street. It was talking to a different dog. It had nothing to do with them, and after a moment it stopped, and the silence came back and lay down where it had been.

Step C was the descent, and Step C was where the plan, having behaved so beautifully through A and B, began to reveal that it had opinions of its own. The window opened onto a maintenance catwalk that Desmond's nephew had mentioned and Desmond had forgotten to write down, so that when Nora, who was the smallest and the calmest and had therefore been elected, slid through the window fully braced for a heroic drop into darkness, she instead landed immediately and undramatically on solid grating eighteen inches down and stood there feeling cheated. She found the stairs. She descended them. She reached the floor and clicked on the little headlamp she had bought for exactly this and swept the beam across the great dim cavern of stored things, past the shrouded bumper cars huddled like sleeping cattle, past the funhouse mirrors stacked and reflecting nothing, past a wall of prize plush animals suffocating in plastic, and there, in the center of the room, up on a wooden pallet, draped in a canvas sheet with one carved hoof sticking out from underneath, was the horse.

Its name, painted on the saddle by whom nobody now living remembered, was Clementine.

They had all three of them ridden Clementine as children, on the outside ring, where the horses went up and down and also swung out slightly on their poles, which everyone agreed was superior to the inside ring where the horses merely sat there like furniture, and Nora had ridden Clementine the summer she was nine and had decided, on the strength of a single revolution, to marry a boy named Terrence, and Cliff had thrown up off the back of Clementine in 1961 in a manner still commemorated at reunions, and Desmond had simply loved the horse the way children love things without needing a reason, which is the only durable kind of loving there is. The town had sold Clementine to a collector in another state for four hundred and twelve dollars. The check had gone toward resurfacing a tennis court that no one used. It was, all three of them had agreed at the Skylark, an outrage that could not stand, and so here they were, standing.

Step D was the retrieval, and retrieval was the step Desmond had written on the placemat in the largest letters and thought about the least.

Clementine weighed a great deal. This they discovered the way you discover most fundamental truths, which is all at once and with your lower back. The three of them arranged themselves around the pallet and lifted on Desmond's count of three, and Clementine rose perhaps two inches, hung in the balance of their trembling collective effort like a decision the universe had not yet made, and then settled back down with the finality of a judge's gavel, and Cliff said a word he had learned in the Navy.

They regrouped. They deliberated. Nora, who did most things without incident because Nora thought first, walked the perimeter with her headlamp and returned wheeling a flat furniture dolly she had found parked under the funhouse mirrors, and onto this dolly, by means of a ramp improvised from a warped sheet of plywood and a shared willingness to risk everything, they tipped and rolled and coaxed the horse, and it went, and it stayed, and it sat on the dolly at last like a passenger in a taxi with somewhere to be.

Getting Clementine up the stairs and back out the fourth window from the left was, they realized in unison and without needing to say it, impossible.

So they went out the front. There was a loading door with a chain and a padlock, and the padlock was formidable, but the chain ran through two eyelets, and one of the eyelets was screwed into wood that had been rained on since the Eisenhower administration, and Cliff put his shoulder to it, briefly, experimentally, and the eyelet came out of the wall like a tooth out of a soft gum and the whole chain went slack and pooled on the floor, and the door rolled up, and the night air came in, cool and enormous and smelling of cut grass.

They wheeled the horse out under the stars.

Nobody stopped them. Nobody was awake. Somewhere the two dogs had made up. Desmond looked at the placemat, which said nothing about how you fit a carousel horse into a Buick, and he folded it, and he put it in his pocket, and he said, to the horse mostly, that they would figure it out.

They figured it out.
