The kettle had not yet begun to sing when Marta came into the kitchen, and she was glad of that. There was a stretch of morning she liked best, the part before anything asked to be done, when the light came in low and yellow across the floorboards and lay there like something that had been spilled and forgotten. She stood in the doorway a moment in her socks and watched it. The floor needed sweeping. She thought this without any urgency, the way you might note that a friend had let their hair grow long, with fondness and no intention to correct it.

Behind her the bedroom door was still ajar, and from it came the particular quiet of a person sleeping who is nearly awake. Daniel had that quiet. He surfaced from sleep slowly, in stages, the way tide leaves a beach, and she had learned over the years not to hurry him. If she pretended not to hear him stirring he would come to the kitchen ten minutes later of his own accord, warm and rumpled and pleased with himself as though waking were an accomplishment. If she called to him he would groan and burrow and stay another half hour out of principle. So she said nothing. She filled the kettle at the tap, holding it low so the water would not clatter, and set it on the flame.

The cat came and pressed itself against her ankles, describing a figure eight with its whole body, and she bent and touched the small warm knob of its head. "Yes," she said. "In a minute." The cat did not believe her, which was reasonable, and went to sit by its bowl with the wounded dignity of the perpetually neglected. Marta smiled at it. She got the bag of biscuits down from the cupboard and shook a few into the dish, and the cat ate as though it had been days, crunching loudly, its tail wrapped round its feet.

She liked the kitchen at this hour. In an hour it would be only a kitchen, a room where things got done, but now it held a kind of grace she could not have explained to anyone and did not try to. The two mugs waited on the counter where she had left them the night before, one white and one blue with a chip in the rim that Daniel refused to throw away because, he said, he had grown attached to the way it fit his thumb. There was a jar of honey with the lid not quite screwed on. A tea towel hung over the oven handle, printed with lemons faded almost to nothing from washing. These were small things, and she knew they were small things, and she loved them the more for it.

The kettle began to make its low sound, the sound before the whistle, a sort of gathering. She took it off before it could shriek and wake him properly. She warmed the pot with a little water swirled round and poured out, then measured the tea in with a spoon, two for the pot and one for luck, which was her mother's rule and made no sense and which she had never once broken. She poured the water in a slow steady stream and watched the leaves lift and turn and settle. There was time in it. That was what she liked, she thought. Not the tea itself so much as the time the tea required, the small stretch of minutes in which there was nothing to do but wait for a thing to be ready.

She heard him then, the creak of the floor in the hall, the soft heavy sound of a big man moving carefully so as not to disturb. He came in with his hair standing up on one side and the crease of the pillow still on his cheek, wearing the grey shirt he slept in that had a hole beginning at the shoulder. He looked at her and said nothing and she looked at him and said nothing, and this was its own kind of greeting, older and truer than words. He crossed the kitchen and put his arms around her from behind and rested his chin on the top of her head, and she leaned back into the warmth of him and felt his breath slow and even against her hair.

"Morning," he said finally, into her scalp.

"Morning."

"Is that tea I smell or am I dreaming."

"It's tea." She patted the arm crossed over her chest. "Sit down. It needs another minute."

He let her go and went to the table and sat heavily, and the chair complained the way it always did, and he yawned enormously with no shame at all. She loved that about him too, that he did nothing in half measures, that when he yawned he committed to it fully, jaw cracking, eyes streaming. He rubbed his face with both hands and blinked at the light on the floor.

"Nice morning," he said.

"It is."

"We should do something with it."

"We are doing something with it."

He considered this. "That's true," he said, and seemed satisfied, and folded his hands on the table and waited to be brought tea like a large contented child.

She poured. The blue mug for him, the white for her, milk in both, no sugar for her and a spoonful for him though he always said he was giving it up. She carried them to the table and set his down in front of him and he wrapped both hands around it before it had even settled, and she watched his thumb find the chip in the rim and fit itself there, and she thought, not for the first time, that a whole marriage could be measured in such small fittings, the way two people learned the shape of each other and the shape of their shared rooms until the fitting became invisible, became simply the way things were.

She sat across from him. Outside a bird was going through its whole repertoire in the pear tree, unhurried, taking its time between phrases as though it too had nowhere to be. The steam rose from the mugs and turned in the light. Daniel drank and made the small sound of contentment he made, deep in his chest, and set the mug down.

"I dreamed about my father," he said. "He was fixing the fence. The one at the old house. He kept telling me I was holding the post wrong."

"Were you?"

"Probably." He smiled. "I always was." He turned the mug a quarter turn on the table, an idle thing, and she watched his hands, which were broad and scarred across the knuckles and gentle in everything they did. "It was good to see him," he said. "Even about the fence."

"I'm glad," she said, and meant it, and reached across the table and put her hand over his, and he turned his hand up and held hers loosely, their fingers laced, and they sat like that and drank their tea and did not speak for a while.

The cat finished eating and came and jumped onto the empty chair between them and began to wash itself with great seriousness. The light moved a little across the floor as the sun climbed. Somewhere down the street a door opened and closed. There was the whole day ahead, and things in it that would need doing, and they both knew this and neither of them moved. The tea was hot and the morning was quiet and his hand was warm in hers, and that, for now, was enough. That was more than enough.
