The garlic hit the pan before anybody had properly lowered the lights, which was the first sign the surprise would not go according to plan.

"He can smell garlic from the truck bay," hissed Dolores, waving her spatula like a woman directing traffic at a four-car pileup. "He has a nose like a bloodhound with a grudge. Kill the burner."

"You can't kill the burner, the sauce'll seize," said Petey, who took every culinary decision as a matter of personal honor. "You want the man's send-off to taste like paste? On his last night? Is that the memory you want to give him, Dolores, paste?"

"I want to give him a surprise, Peter."

"He's been here thirty-one years. You think anything surprises Hollis anymore? The man watched a Buick fall out of a parking garage. Twice. Different Buicks."

They were still arguing the finer points of ambush when Captain Ruiz came in from the apparatus floor holding the streamers like she'd arrested them. "He's finishing the logbook," she announced, in the low, even voice she used for structure fires and for her children. "We have four minutes. Everybody down behind the island. Novak, that means you too."

Novak, the probie, was six foot four and folded himself behind the kitchen island with the grace of a deck chair. "This is my first surprise party at a firehouse," he whispered, delighted. "Is it always like this?"

"No," said everybody at once, and then, "yes," which somehow they also all agreed on.

They crouched. The refrigerator hummed. Somewhere a clock counted down that no one could see. And Petey, unable to bear the silence, murmured into the dark, "You remember the Delaney Street fire?"

"Oh, here we go," said Dolores.

"I'm just saying. Hollis carried that dog down three flights—"

"It was two flights and it was a cat," said Ruiz, without turning her head.

"It was a dog, Cap, I was there—"

"You were on vacation. You were in Myrtle Beach. You sent us a picture of a pelican."

"I heard about it in real time. On the phone. My cousin's a dispatcher, I got the play-by-play, and it was a dog, a big one, a shepherd mix, and Hollis came out of that smoke with it over his shoulders like a—like a scarf—"

"Like a scarf," Dolores repeated, to the ceiling, or to God.

The door opened. The lights, which no one had killed, revealed all of them squatting behind the island in various stages of collapse, and Hollis Aiken stood in the doorway with his reading glasses pushed up on his forehead and looked at them for a long, considering moment.

"Surprise," said Novak, gently, from the floor.

"You started the sauce," Hollis said. "I could smell it in the bay."

"I TOLD you," Dolores roared, rising like a phoenix with a spatula.

What followed was less a party than a slow, joyful flood. Someone found the good folding chairs, the ones without the wobble. Petey plated the pasta with the reverence of a man defusing a bomb. There was a cake from the grocery store on Vine that said HAPPY RETIREMENT HOLLI because the frosting had run out of room, and Hollis said he would treasure being called Holli, it felt fresh, it felt like a new beginning. Ruiz produced a bottle of something amber that she was not, strictly, supposed to have on the premises, and poured exactly one finger into everybody's coffee mug and said, "We were never here," and everybody said, "We were never here."

"To Delaney Street," said Petey, lifting his mug. "And the dog."

"Cat," said three voices.

"The animal," Petey amended, diplomatically. "To the animal."

But by then Big Marcus had wandered in off his rig check with his hat in his hand, and Big Marcus had a memory like a courthouse, and Big Marcus said, "Forget Delaney. You want to talk about Hollis, you talk about the Cranberry Bog Rescue."

"It wasn't a bog," Dolores said.

"It was a retention pond," said Ruiz.

"It was a bog in his heart," said Marcus, "and the kid had gone through the ice, and there was no ice, it was October, but the mother was screaming ice, ice, and Hollis just walks in, calm as a churchgoer, up to his belt in that freezing water, and he pulls the kid out and he says—" Marcus paused, savoring it, holding the whole kitchen in his palm. "He says, 'Next time swim in summer.'"

"I never said that," said Hollis, who was eating cake standing up.

"You said it. It's legend now, Holli, you can't take it back."

"He said," Ruiz corrected, "'Let's get you warm.' Which is what he says at every call. It's the only thing he says."

"'Let's get you warm,'" Novak repeated softly, as if memorizing a psalm.

"See, that's the thing about the kid," Hollis said, setting down his fork, and the room quieted the way it does when the one man who never talks starts to. "He wasn't even in the water. He was on the bank crying because his shoe was in the water. I went in for a sneaker. Light-up sneaker. Cost more than my boots."

"That's not the story," Marcus said, wounded.

"It's the true one."

"The true one's boring," said Dolores, "which is why we improved it. You're welcome."

Hollis laughed then, really laughed, the deep one they didn't hear often, and it set the whole table off, and Petey banged the table so the mugs jumped, and Novak laughed too even though he'd only understood about half of it, laughing the way you laugh when a room decides to be happy around you and you're just glad to be standing in it.

"Fine, fine, forget both of those," said Petey, wiping his eyes. "The only story that matters is the Thanksgiving turkey."

A groan went up, but it was an affectionate groan, the groan of people who wanted very badly to hear the turkey story again.

"Mrs. Pilcher," said Dolores, "deep-fried a frozen turkey."

"In her garage," said Marcus.

"With the door shut," said Ruiz.

"And Hollis," Petey continued, standing now, gesturing with his whole arm, "gets there, and the garage is a fireball, and Mrs. Pilcher is on the lawn in her robe, and she looks at Hollis, and she says—everybody—"

"'The gravy's still inside,'" the whole kitchen said together, and the laugh came up like a tide.

"And what did you do, Holli," Petey demanded. "Tell the probie what you did."

"I got the gravy," Hollis said.

"He got the gravy," Petey confirmed, sitting down heavily, satisfied, as though a great work were complete. "Ran in, came out, one pot of gravy, not a scratch on it. Best gravy that woman ever made, she said, and she never made it again 'cause she never fried another turkey, on account of the garage."

Novak looked around the table at all of them, glowing under the buzzing kitchen light, mugs raised, the cake half-gone, the sauce gone cold and nobody minding. "Are any of these true?" he asked.

"All of them," said Hollis.

"None of them," said Ruiz.

"The parts that matter," said Dolores, and she reached over and squeezed Hollis's shoulder, and did not let go right away.

And Hollis sat there with his light-up-sneaker legend and his frozen turkey and his cat that was a dog, thirty-one years of it warming the room, and he looked at each of them in turn, and he opened his mouth to say something, and the alarm did not go off, and the phone did not ring, and for once the night just let them have it. So he said the only thing he ever said.

"Let's get you warm," he told them, all of them, and raised his mug, and they came up around him laughing, louder, closer, until the little kitchen could barely hold the sound.
