In the days when the river still spoke, there lived a miller's daughter whose name was Ysolde. Her father ground the grain of the village, and the wheel of the mill turned by the water's grace. The village was small. It sat in the crook of the valley like an egg in a nest, and the river ran beside it, wide and slow and old.

The river had a voice. In the spring it laughed. In the summer it murmured. In the autumn it grew quiet, and in the winter it slept beneath the ice. The old people said the river was older than the hills, and that it remembered the shape of the land before there were fields. The young people did not believe this. But Ysolde believed it, for she had grown up beside the water, and she had heard it speak.

One year the rains did not come. The sky was blue and hard as a plate, day after day, and the sun beat down without mercy. The wells sank low. The wheat bent and yellowed in the fields. And the river, which had always been wide and slow, grew thin and thinner, until it was only a trickle among the stones. The mill wheel stopped. The bread grew scarce. The children cried in the night for water, and the mothers had none to give.

The villagers gathered in the square. They wrung their hands and looked to the sky, but the sky gave them nothing. They looked to the old priest, but he could only pray. They looked to the headman, but he could only shrug. And so, one by one, they went back to their houses, and the village grew quiet with despair.

But Ysolde did not go back to her house. She went down to the river.

She walked along the dry bed, past the stones that lay bare and white as bones, until she came to the deep place where the water still gathered in a green pool. There she knelt down at the edge and she said, "River, I have known you all my life. You have turned our wheel and given us bread. Now the village is dying. Will you not help us?"

For a long while there was no answer. The pool lay still. Then the water stirred, and a voice rose out of it, deep and cold and slow as the turning of stones.

"Miller's daughter," said the river, "I hear you. But I am dying too. The rains have forgotten me. My springs run dry in the far hills. I have only so much left to give, and if I give it, I will give all."

"Then give it," said Ysolde. "For the village will surely die without it."

"There is a price," said the river.

"Name it," said Ysolde.

The pool grew darker, and the voice grew deeper still. "I will pour out all my waters upon your fields. The wheat shall grow tall and the wells shall fill and the children shall drink and live. But when the harvest is gathered, and the bread is baked, you must come to me. You must walk into my waters and stay. For a river that gives all must be given something in return. Life for life. Water for water. This is the old law, older than your fields, older than the hills."

Ysolde was silent. The sun beat down. Behind her, far off, she could hear a child crying.

"I will come," she said.

Then the river rose.

It rose out of the green pool and it filled the dry bed and it swelled over the white stones. It ran laughing into the fields, and the wheat lifted its head and drank. The wells brimmed over. The mill wheel groaned and turned. And through the village a great glad shout went up, for the water had come, and no one knew how, and no one thought to ask.

The wheat grew tall and golden. The rain of the river fell upon it every night, though the sky stayed clear. When the harvest came, it was the greatest the village had ever known. The barns were full. The bread was warm on every table. The children laughed in the lanes. And the villagers said among themselves, "The river has blessed us," and they gave thanks, and they forgot how near they had come to death.

But Ysolde did not forget. Each day she went down to the river, and each day the water was full and living. And each night she lay awake and heard the water calling, low and patient, beneath her window.

When the last sheaf was gathered and the last loaf baked, Ysolde put on her white dress. She kissed her sleeping father upon the brow. She walked out of the mill and down through the sleeping village and along the bank to the deep green pool. The moon was up, and it lay upon the water like a coin.

"River," she said, "I have come. The harvest is gathered. The bread is baked. I keep my word."

The water stirred. The voice rose, deep and cold and slow.

"Miller's daughter," said the river, "you have come. Others would have hidden. Others would have fled to the far towns and let me take a stranger. But you have come freely, in your white dress, in the light of the moon."

"I made a bargain," said Ysolde. "A bargain must be kept."

Then the river was quiet for a long while. The moon shone. The reeds bent in a wind that was not there.

"Because you kept your word when you might have broken it," said the river at last, "I will change the price. Do not walk into my waters. Instead, each year, when the harvest is gathered, come to me and speak to me as you spoke today. That will be enough. For what I wanted was not your death. What I wanted was to be remembered. A river that is forgotten is a river that dies, and the fields die with it. Give me your memory, and I will give you my water, all your life long, and your children's lives, and their children's after them."

Ysolde wept then, for gladness. "I will come," she said. "Every year, until I am old. And I will teach my children to come, and they will teach theirs. You will not be forgotten while the mill wheel turns."

"That is a bargain," said the river, "and a better one."

So Ysolde went home, and she slept, and in the morning the water ran full and bright past the mill, and the wheel turned, and the bread was warm. And every year, when the harvest was gathered, she went down to the deep green pool and spoke to the river, and told it of the year that had passed — of the weddings and the births, of the storms and the frosts, of who had died and who was born. And the river listened, and gave its water, and the village prospered.

When Ysolde grew old, she took her daughter by the hand and led her to the water and taught her the words. And her daughter taught her own. And so the memory was kept, down all the years, from mouth to mouth, from mother to child.

And this is why, in that valley, the old people still say: A river that is remembered never runs dry. A word once given must be kept. And that which asks the highest price may, in the end, ask only that we do not forget.
