THE SEXTON, MARTIN VELE:

I had the only key, and that is the beginning and the end of it, whatever else they tell you. I locked the tower door at nine, same as forty years, and I heard the bell settle in its cradle the way it does when the wind drops, a little groan, a settling, like an old man lying down. Nobody came up those stairs but me. I would have heard the door. I would have heard the hinge, which I have oiled and not oiled and oiled again in my time, and I know its voice like I know my wife's. So when they say four men carried it out through the door, I tell them there is no door that opens without my hearing, and there was no hearing, and so there was no door. It went up, is what I think. I do not say who took it up. I say only that it did not come down through the ground, because I was the ground, I was the lock, I was the man on the stair.

WIDOW ASELIN:

Oh, it was the storm, plain as milk. You want it to be men, all of you, because men you can hang. But I stood at my window with the shutter banging and I saw the tower struck — not once, three times, three white teeth of it — and the last one so bright I had spots in my eyes for the saying of a whole rosary. And after that, no bell. You draw your line from the one to the other. Lightning has taken cattle and it has taken the top off the Herrin oak and it will surely take a bell that has been sitting up there fat and proud through every summer God sent. The metal ran, is what happened. It ran down inside the walls where you can't see it, and one day somebody'll knock a stone loose and find the bell poured out flat like a griddle cake, and then you'll all beg my pardon.

TOMAS THE CARTER:

I was on the north road with the empty cart, coming back from Dellin market, and I will tell you the hour because I had passed the milestone and the moon was where it is at eleven. And there were wheels ahead of me. Not my wheels — I know the song of my own axle. Another cart, heavy, going away from the village, and it groaned like it was carrying stone or iron. I could not catch it. Whoever it was drove without a lantern, which no honest man does, and when I came to the crossing there were ruts cut deep into the mud, deep, the way a cart cuts when it is loaded past its sense. I say the bell went east on those wheels. I say if you want it you should have gone to Dellin the next morning and looked in every barn, but nobody would listen to a carter, because a carter smells of the horse and not of the church.

THE PRIEST, FATHER OBRE:

Let me be careful, because in matters of this kind the careless word breeds a harvest. I was at prayer. I confess I was also asleep, for the two are near neighbours at that hour. I heard nothing that I would swear to. But in the morning the rope was gone — not cut, not frayed, gone entire, the whole bell-rope from the wheel to the floor — and a rope does not run off with a bell of its own accord. I have said from the pulpit and I say to you now that this was not weather and it was not thieves. A thief takes the silver, which was two arm-lengths from the tower stair and untouched. A thief does not take the one object in the parish too heavy to lift and too loud to move. No. It was taken as a sign is taken, to be a lack, so that we should feel the lack and mend ourselves. I do not say by whom. I say by Whom.

GRETA, WHO IS TWELVE:

I saw it and nobody believes me because I am small. It didn't go on a cart and it didn't melt. I woke up because it was too quiet — that's a real thing, being woken by quiet — and I went to the window and the tower had no top anymore, just sky where the little roof used to be, and the bell was going up. Slow. Like a bubble in the pond going up but backwards, going up into the black, and it was ringing but with no sound, I could see it swinging and no sound came, and then it was a star and then it was nothing. The next day the roof was back on the tower. That's the part nobody answers me. If men took it, who put the roof back? A cart can't put a roof back. Lightning can't put a roof back. I asked Martin Vele and he told me to go help my mother.

DELLIN GATEKEEPER, JORAM:

No cart came east that night and I will put my hand on the book. I keep the gate and I keep the toll and there is a ledger with every wheel that passes, and that night the ledger is empty from dusk to dawn, empty, because who travels in a storm? The carter says he heard wheels. The carter hears wheels the way a drunkard hears his own name in the wind. If a loaded cart came to my gate I would have had the toll off it or the hide off the man, and I have neither toll nor hide, and so there was no cart, and so the bell did not come to Dellin, and so all his ruts in the mud are the ruts of his own cart going and coming and telling itself stories.

OLD MOSSE, THE BELLFOUNDER'S SON:

My father cast that bell and I held the mould-string as a boy, so I will tell you a thing none of them know. That bell had a flaw. A hairline, up under the crown, that my father saw and swore me to silence on, for a cracked bell is a shamed founder. Sixty years it hung on a flaw. And a flaw grows. I say it cracked clean through in the cold of that night — cold snaps metal, ask any smith — cracked and the crown gave and the whole weight of it dropped straight down inside the tower and buried itself in the earth of the crypt floor, which is soft, which was always soft, my father complained of it. It is down there now under a foot of muck, whole, silent, waiting. No lightning. No wheels. No angels carrying it up to heaven for a child to watch. Metal and cold and a secret my father kept, that is all it ever was, and when I am dead there will be no one left who knows even that much, and you will all go on saying storm, saying thief, saying sign, saying star.
