The door I entered by was the same door I had already left through, though this time it opened inward onto the kitchen, and the kitchen was where I kept meaning to sleep. There was a bed in it now, or there had always been a bed, folded into the shape of a stove until I looked directly at the burners and understood they were buttons on a mattress. I lay down. The pillow smelled of my grandmother, who I did not have, who I had lost, who was in the next room writing my name on the windows in her own breath.

When I stood up the kitchen had become the hallway it always was, and the hallway leaned. I mean it tipped like a boat, and I walked its length with one hand on the wallpaper, which was warm and moved slightly, as if I were touching the flank of something asleep. At the end of the hallway there was a door I remembered from earlier, the door I entered by, except now behind it was a staircase going up, and I was certain I had already climbed those stairs and found nothing but the kitchen, so I climbed them again to be sure.

The stairs delivered me to the room with the piano. Someone I loved was in the room, sitting at the piano, and I could not see their face because faces in this house are the last thing to arrive, they come after the body has finished being recognized. This person played a note and the note was a smell, cut grass, and the grass grew up through the floorboards, and I understood that the room with the piano was becoming the garden, which is a room I have not yet reached but keep being promised. "You took the long way," the person said, and their voice was my own voice played backward, and I answered before they finished, which is how conversations work here, replies arriving ahead of their questions like water finding the low place first.

Then I was in the hallway again. It is important to say that I did not walk there. The house simply exchanged one room for another the way you exchange one thought for the thought beneath it, and I found myself already mid-step, already touching the warm wallpaper, already leaning with the leaning corridor, and I thought: I have done this. I have done this so many times that the doing has worn a groove, and I am the water in the groove.

At the far end there were now two doors where there had been one. The left door I knew opened onto the kitchen with the folding bed. The right door I had never seen, which meant it was the most familiar door of all, because in this house the things I have never seen are the things I miss most keenly. I opened the right door. Behind it was the kitchen. The bed was made. My grandmother, who I did not have, had finished writing on the windows, and the windows now said a word I could read only by not reading it, the way you see a faint star by looking beside it. I looked beside the word. The word was the name of the house. The house was called Again.

I want to describe the room with the water, because I passed through it, or it passed through me. The floor was an inch deep in still water that did not wet me, and the furniture stood in it up to the ankles of its legs, patient as cattle. A clock floated by, facedown. When I turned it over the hands were the legs of a small dark bird that flew up into the light fixture and became the light. In the water was a reflection of a room I had not entered, a room with a low ceiling and a made bed and a person waiting, and I knew that room was the room I was actually standing in, and that the room I thought I stood in was only the reflection, and I could not decide which side of the water I was the wet side of.

A voice said, from the doorway that was also a mirror, "You are looking for the room you started in." This was true and I had not known it until it was said, and the moment it was said it stopped being true, the way a name stops fitting once it is spoken too many times. I started in the kitchen. No. I started outside, at the door I entered by, which is the door I have not stopped standing at, one hand raised to knock, the knock still traveling toward the wood.

I climbed the stairs. They were the same stairs and they went down. Down led to the hallway, which is the only place all rooms agree to meet, a kind of throat the house swallows itself through. The wallpaper breathed under my hand, faster now, and I thought perhaps the house was frightened, or perhaps I was the thing it was afraid of, the small warm animal loose in its corridors, opening doors it had shut for good reasons. At the end of the hallway there was one door again, only one, and I was relieved, and the relief was a mistake, because relief is how the house knows where you are.

The one door opened onto the room with the piano, but the piano had become the folding bed, and the person I loved was lying on it with their face finally arriving, feature by feature, the way dawn arrives on a wall, and just before I could see the whole of it the room exchanged itself for the garden I had been promised. The garden was indoors. It had a ceiling of grass and a floor of sky and I walked upside down among the roots, which hung like chandeliers, and my grandmother, who I now had, who had always been mine, sat in a chair of soil writing my name on the air with her breath, and each time she wrote it the letters fell as rain into the room below, which was the kitchen, where a version of me lay on the folding bed listening to rain that was my own name and thinking: I should get up, I should find the room I started in.

I have been trying to leave by the door I entered. But every door I open is that door, and every threshold sets me down further inside, and the house folds its rooms shut behind me and deals them again in a different order, kitchen hallway stairs piano water garden, hallway stairs kitchen, and I am always mid-step, always leaning, always about to see the face. Somewhere the knock I began with is still crossing the last inch toward the wood. When it lands I think the house will open. Or the house is the sound of the knock arriving, over and over, and I am the space inside the sound, walking, and the rooms are the echoes deciding where to fall.

I open the door. It is the kitchen. The bed is warm as if I have only just left it. Someone is writing my name on the window in breath, and this time, when I look beside the word to read it, the word is not Again.

It is Still.

And I lie down, and the stove becomes the bed, and the hallway leans, and I enter by the door I have not yet reached.
