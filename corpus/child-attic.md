The attic was not a place I was allowed to go. Mama said the stairs were bad and the wood was old and I could fall through and break my whole self. But Mama was at the store buying eggs and flour, and Papa was asleep on the couch with his mouth open, making the sound like a slow train, and nobody was watching me at all. So I went up.

The door at the top of the little stairs was white but the white was peeling off like skin when you have a sunburn. I pushed it and it made a long crying sound. Inside it smelled like the inside of an old book and like the dust that floats in the sun when you look at it just right. There was one round window and the light came through it yellow and thick like honey.

There were boxes everywhere. Big brown boxes and little boxes and a box that used to have shoes in it but I did not think it had shoes in it anymore. I stepped very careful because Mama said the wood was bad, but the wood felt fine to me. It did not break. Maybe the wood was only bad when grown-ups walked on it because they were heavy.

The first thing I found was a wedding dress. I knew it was a wedding dress because it was white and long and puffy at the bottom like a cloud that fell down. It was hanging on a nail and there was a clear plastic bag over it but the plastic was ripped. I touched the bottom of it. It felt like the petals of a flower but colder. I wondered whose it was. Maybe it was Mama's, from before she was Mama and was only a lady. It was hard to think about Mama being only a lady. It made my stomach feel funny, like when you swing too high and for a second you are up in the air and not connected to anything.

Next to the dress there was a box with pictures in it. The pictures were not in the phone like our pictures. They were real pictures you could hold, and some of them were not even in color, they were gray and white like the world was before color got invented. There was a picture of a baby in a lady's arms and the lady looked a little bit like Grandma but young, so young that she was not Grandma at all, she was somebody else who would become Grandma later. I looked at the baby a long time. I did not know who the baby was. Maybe it was Papa. Papa was a baby once but I do not believe it, really, because his hands are so big.

Then I found the thing that scared me.

It was in the far corner where the light did not reach so good. It was a big shape with a white sheet over it, and under the sheet I could see it had a face. The face was pushing against the sheet from underneath like it wanted to breathe. I stood very still. My heart went fast. I thought maybe it was a person who had died and they put them up here because there was no more room in the ground. I knew that people got put in the ground because Grandpa got put in the ground and everybody cried and Mama wore black and held my hand too tight.

But this person was up here, in the attic, above everyone's heads. And nobody talked about it. That was the scary part. That there was a dead person right above where we ate our dinner and slept in our beds and nobody said a single word.

I did not want to look but I wanted to look. Both of those wanting things were fighting inside me. In the end the looking one won, because it always does. I went over slow and I lifted the corner of the sheet just a little with two fingers.

Underneath was a face, but it was not a real face. It was made of something hard and shiny and cracked, like a plate. The eyes were open and painted blue but they did not blink and they did not look at me even though they were looking right at me. It was a big doll. The biggest doll I ever saw, almost as big as me, sitting in a little chair. Her hair was gone in one spot so you could see the round head underneath, smooth and bald like an egg. One of her hands was broke off and it was sitting in her lap like she was holding her own hand, being her own friend.

I pulled the sheet off all the way. The dust flew up and I coughed. She had a dress on too, a small blue one, and little shoes that buttoned. She just sat there. I felt sorry for her. Somebody put a sheet over her face and left her up here in the dark all alone with the dead person feeling all around, and she could not even close her eyes to go to sleep because her eyes were painted open forever. I thought that was the saddest thing. To be up here and awake the whole time.

"It's okay," I told her, even though she was not real. "I'm here now."

I sat down next to her on the floor. The floor did not break. I told her about the eggs and the flour Mama was buying and about how Papa made the train sound when he slept. I told her my name. She did not tell me hers so I made one up. I called her June because it was almost June and it was a pretty word.

That is when I saw the little papers under the chair. They were tied with a ribbon, a bunch of them, folded up. I opened one. It had handwriting on it, the swirly kind that grown-ups from a long time ago did, and I could not read most of it because I only knew my regular letters and these were dressed up too fancy. But I saw a name at the bottom. The name was Mama's name. Mama's real name that only Papa calls her when they think I am not listening.

I did not understand. I thought maybe these were letters Mama wrote to the doll. Maybe when Mama was little like me she came up here too and found June and talked to her the same way, and wrote her letters, and then Mama grew up and forgot all about her and left her in the dark. That made me want to cry a little. That you could love something so much and write it letters and then just forget. I did not want to grow up if growing up made you forget the things you loved.

I heard the front door then, and Mama's voice calling my name, and it had the scared sound in it that meant I was somewhere I should not be.

I put the sheet back over June's face very gentle so she could go to sleep. "I'll come back," I whispered. I did not know yet that I would not be allowed. I went down the crying stairs into the light where Mama grabbed me and shook me and then held me, and I did not tell her about June, because June was mine now, and some things you keep, so you do not forget them later.
