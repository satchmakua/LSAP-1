We had always come to the pool in the last week of May, when the fence gate stuck on its hinge and had to be lifted and shoved, when the water was still cold enough to make us gasp, when the lifeguards were teenagers we had known as babies and now watched apply zinc to their noses with the solemnity of surgeons. Every year we came like this, and every year the pool received us the same way, with its blue rectangle and its rope of white floats and its drain at the deep end that we told our children would suck them down if they lingered, which was a lie, but a useful one, a lie we passed to each other across decades like a casserole dish returned clean.

This year they told us it was the last. The city sent a letter, and the letter used the word *decommission*, which none of us had ever said out loud about a place we loved.

"It means they're closing it," said Marguerite, who read the letter first because she was the one who checked the community board.

"It means it's cheaper to fill a hole with dirt than to keep it full of water," said Dev, who was not wrong.

So we decided, without deciding, the way we decided most things — by the accretion of nods at the mailboxes and the murmur that traveled porch to porch — that we would spend the whole last season saying goodbye. Not all at once. That would have been unbearable, and also we were not the kind of people who could organize a single unbearable thing. Instead we took turns. We made a schedule, though no one wrote it down, and we honored it the way we honored the unwritten schedule of whose turn it was to bring folding chairs to the block party, which is to say imperfectly and with feeling.

We came to say goodbye and we found ourselves saying hello to the pool as we had first known it, and to each other as we had first known each other, and to the versions of ourselves that had been thin or unwrinkled or unmarried or unafraid, all of whom seemed to be doing laps in the shallow end just under the surface, just out of reach.

Mrs. Okafor went first, on a Tuesday, in her flowered cap, and she swam twenty lengths without stopping, and when she climbed out she patted the ladder like the flank of a horse.

We watched her do it and we did not say anything, because there was nothing to say that the patting had not already said.

Then it was the turn of the men who came at dawn, the ones who had jobs that started at seven and so had earned the water when it was flat and silvered and belonged to no one, and they came together that whole final summer and stood at the edge with their coffee going cold in paper cups, and they talked about the transmission on a truck and the price of lumber and the daughter who was moving to a city they could not pronounce, and eventually one of them would put down his cup and dive, and then the others, and for a few minutes the surface would break and re-form and break, and the ripples would fan out and touch every wall at once, which is the only kind of goodbye a body of water understands.

We would bring our children even now, even knowing, because we did not want the children to think that a thing worth loving was a thing you abandoned once it was doomed. We wanted them to see us stay. We wanted them to learn to say goodbye slowly, over a season, the way you should, and not all at once at the end when it is too late to mean it.

"Why are they closing it," asked one of the smaller children, a girl named Priya who had learned to float here the summer before.

"Because grown-ups run out of money for the good things before they run out of it for the dull ones," said her mother, and then, seeing the girl's face, added, "and because nothing lasts, sweetheart, but that's not the same as nothing mattering."

We remembered the year the filter broke and the water turned green and they closed it for two weeks and we were beside ourselves, we called the city, we wrote petitions, we treated a fortnight like a bereavement, and now we would have taken a hundred green fortnights, we would have swum in a swamp, we would have swum in tears. We remembered the boy who cannonballed off the high dive on a dare and split his chin and came up laughing with blood in the water and that was the summer we all learned the word *stitches* had a plural. We remembered the widower who came only to sit, never to swim, in the same white plastic chair, doing the crossword, until one August he wasn't there and we understood without being told, and someone left a flower on the chair, and the chair stayed empty in a way that other empty chairs are not empty.

On the last actual day, the very last one, a specific Sunday in the specific gold of September, we did not schedule anything and so of course everyone came. The gate stuck on its hinge and we lifted and shoved it one final time. The teenagers who were lifeguards sat in the high chair together, two of them where there should have been one, because none of them wanted the last watch alone. Someone brought a cake, which was ridiculous, you cannot bring a cake to a pool, the frosting sweated in the sun, and we ate it anyway with our wet hands.

We went in by ages, though no one announced it, the oldest first and then down the years, and the water held each of us and let each of us go. Mrs. Okafor did her twenty lengths, slower now than in June. The dawn men came at dusk for once, in the wrong light, and dove anyway. Priya floated on her back with her ears underwater so that all the goodbyes came to her as muffled thunder.

"Say something," someone said. "Somebody should say something."

But we found we could not, or would not, elect a single voice for it, because it had never been a single-voice place, it had been a chorus, it had been all of us at once, overlapping and interrupting and holding each other up in water over our heads.

So instead we did the only thing that made sense, which was to get in all together, at the same time, on a count we shouted in unison, and the pool rose against its own walls as it had never risen, displaced by every body in the neighborhood, and the water climbed and slopped over the tiled lip and ran onto the concrete and toward the drain, and for one moment the pool was too full of us to hold us, and that, we decided, was the goodbye.

Then we climbed out, dripping, and we did not look back, and we lifted the gate on its hinge, and we let it swing shut.
