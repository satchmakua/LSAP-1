**blanket-tax** (noun). The toll a person must pay to enter another person's made bed, usually one arm and both feet. *Dad tried to sit on the fresh bed and Mum said the blanket-tax is very high today, sir, and he lay down flat and pretended to be arrested.*

**breffnik** (noun). The specific breakfast eaten standing up at the counter because someone is already late; never as good as sitting breakfast, always eaten faster. *Nobody remembers what we ate the morning of the fire drill because it was breffnik and breffnik doesn't stay in your head.*

**churg** (verb). To pretend to be asleep so that you get carried. *I am too big to be churged now, but I still churg on the last day of any holiday, and Dad still carries me and calls me a sack of wet sand, and I keep my eyes shut all the way up the stairs.*

**dworth** (adjective). Describing a mood that looks like sulking from the outside but is actually thinking, and must not be poked. *Leave your sister, she's dworth, said Mum, and I was dworth for forty minutes about whether the dog could tell we were sad.*

**embery** (adjective). Of the state of the living room after everyone has gone to bed but one lamp is still warm and one person is still up on purpose. *Gran said the best conversations are embery ones, when the day has burned down and there's only the two of you left glowing.*

**frim** (noun). The good tiredness after swimming, or after crying, or after laughing very hard — the kind where your bones feel emptied out and clean. *We drove home full of frim and didn't talk and it was one of the best hours of my whole life.*

**garrow** (verb). To arrange food on a plate so that the best bit is saved for last, and to defend that bit with your whole body if anyone reaches. *Dad garrows his roast potatoes like a dragon and once bit Uncle Rob with a fork, which is a family story now.*

**gleeble** (noun). The small ridiculous dance a person does when they have found the thing they were looking for. *Mum did the biggest gleeble of her life the day she found the car keys inside a wellington boot, and we made her do it again so we could film it.*

**hoosh** (interjection). The word said to a pet, a small child, or a grown-up, meaning *out of the kitchen, I love you, but out.* *You could hear Gran hooshing all afternoon on Christmas Eve and it meant everything was going right.*

**jandle** (noun). A word invented by mistake and then kept, usually a mispronunciation from when someone was very small. *"Jandle" was originally my word for "candle" and now it is the only word we use, and if a stranger says candle at our table it sounds cold and wrong.*

**klomming** (verb, continuous). The specific footfall of Dad coming up the stairs pretending to be a monster, heavier than his real feet, always slightly too slow. *I used to lie awake happy just listening to the klomming get closer, knowing exactly whose monster it was.*

**lorb** (noun). A gift that is obviously something the giver wanted for themselves. *The fishing rod Dad gave Mum for her birthday was a classic lorb, and she gave him a spa day the next year that she used entirely herself, and honour was even.*

**mimbly** (adjective). Describing the feeling of a house right before someone comes home who has been away a long time — everything cleaner than usual and too quiet. *The kitchen was mimbly all morning before Toby came back from university, and none of us could sit down.*

**murr** (noun). The low sound Mum makes reading in the other room that means she is fine and you can go to sleep. *I don't remember Dad ever murring, but I remember Mum's murr like I remember my own name, and when I can't sleep now I try to make it myself.*

**nixet** (noun). The last five minutes before we have to leave, when everyone suddenly needs the toilet, a jumper, and a snack. *We have never once left the house without a nixet, and Dad says on his gravestone it will read HE WAITED IN THE CAR.*

**ockle** (verb). To hold a hot drink with both hands purely for the warmth, not to drink it. *Gran ockled the same cup of tea for two hours the winter after Grandad and nobody said anything about it going cold.*

**pledge-bun** (noun). The bun, biscuit, or last chip that you say you don't want so someone else will eat it and be happy. *Mum's whole life is pledge-buns, she never wanted the pledge-bun, she wanted us to have the pledge-bun, and I only understood this recently and it made me cry a bit.*

**quilm** (noun). A worry that only exists at night and dissolves completely by breakfast. *Dad taught me to write my quilms on a bit of paper and put them in the drawer, and in the morning they were always the wrong size, too small for the paper.*

**rennet** (verb). To reheat an argument that was supposed to be over, usually days later, usually in the car. *Please do not rennet the thing about the map, said Mum, we agreed we were finished with the map, and Dad said one more thing about the map.*

**scrimmet** (noun). The exact amount of a shared dessert that counts as "just having a taste," which is legally enormous. *Toby's scrimmet took half my cake and he said that's a taste, that's a normal taste, and we have never resolved this.*

**settle-down** (noun, one word in our house). The precise ten minutes at the end of a birthday party when everyone is too tired and too happy and slightly about to cry. *The best photo we have of all four of us is a settle-down photo, wonky and yellow, nobody quite looking, everybody home.*

**snuck** (noun). The particular smell of Dad's coat, which is rain and the outside and something else that is only him. *When I was little I used to put my face in the snuck when he came in from work, and he'd stand very still and let me, still wearing his boots.*

**tarrow** (verb). To leave a light on in an empty room for someone who is not home yet, so the house looks like it's expecting them. *Even after Toby moved out Mum tarrowed his window every night for a term, and none of us mentioned it, and then one night she stopped and that was its own kind of sad.*

**underneath-voice** (noun). The quiet voice grown-ups use when they think you're asleep, which carries the real news of the house. *I learned most of what I know about my family in the underneath-voice, lying on the stairs where the carpet is worn from all of us lying there.*

**vurl** (noun). The pause before someone says something kind, when you can see them deciding to be brave about their own feelings. *Dad had a long vurl at the airport and then he said I was proud of you before you were even born, which is not a normal thing Dad says.*

**wemmer** (noun). A day so ordinary that it turns out, years later, to have been one of the happy ones. *I didn't know at the time that the Tuesday with the burnt toast and the flat tyre was a wemmer, but I know now, I'd give a lot to have that Tuesday.*

**yandle** (verb). To keep something broken because throwing it away would throw away the person it belonged to. *We yandle Grandad's radio even though it only gets one station, and once a year Gran turns it on and we all pretend it works.*

**zoss** (interjection). The sound the whole family makes together when they collapse onto the sofa at the same moment, meaning nothing, meaning everything, meaning we made it to the end of another one. *We still say zoss, all of us, even now that we're scattered, even on the phone, so that wherever we are we're sitting down together.*
