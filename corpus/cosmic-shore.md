The shore at this hour belonged to no one, least of all to me, and I had come to it as one comes to a confession without a listener, believing the dark would hold whatever I set down and ask nothing back. The tide was low and going lower, retreating with a patience that seemed less like the ordinary arithmetic of moon and water than like a withdrawal of breath, as though the sea were gathering itself for some utterance it had rehearsed since the first cold hour of the world. I stood where the sand went from dry to damp to that slick mirror-black which holds the sky upside down, and I looked at the reflected stars trembling there, and I felt the first faint wrongness the way one feels a fever before the thermometer confirms it — a subtle disagreement between what the body knows and what the eye is willing to report.

The stars in the wet sand did not match the stars above. I told myself this was refraction, the honest distortion of water and grief, for I had come here grieving, though of what I could no longer say. But the longer I looked the more certain I became that the constellations underfoot were arranged according to some other sky, an alien geometry that had never wheeled over any land I had walked, and that this second heaven lay not above me reflected downward but below me, deep, unspeakably deep, shining up through the thin skin of the shore as through the lid of an eye not yet fully closed.

I raised my head to the true sky for reassurance and found it diminished, the familiar lamps of the night grown faint and provisional, as if they were the painted scenery of a theater and something behind them had begun, very gently, to lean against the canvas. The moon hung swollen and pale and it seemed to me that it was not the moon's light that fell but the moon's attention, and that this attention was itself a borrowed thing, a lesser regard standing in for a greater which had not yet troubled to turn my way. A tightness gathered at the root of my thoughts. I understood, without wishing to understand, that I was being permitted to notice — that the wrongness had always been there, patient beneath the everyday, and that some hinge in me had merely swung open tonight to let the knowledge in.

The water, where it lay beyond the exposed flats, was too still. Not calm, for calm is a quality of surfaces at rest, and this was the stillness of a surface pressed flat from beneath, held taut over a volume that strained upward against it. I could not see the far edge of the sea. The horizon had dissolved into a band of undifferentiated dark where the categories of air and water and distance had ceased to be distinct, where up and out and down had entered into a treaty I was not party to and did not survive the reading of. And from that band, or from beneath it, or from within the very substance of the wet ground I stood upon, there rose a pressure — not a sound, for sound would have been a mercy, a thing with edges and a source. This was pressure only, the felt nearness of magnitude, the sensation one might have standing blindfolded beside a mountain and knowing by the displacement of the air how much of the world it occupied and how little of it was you.

There was something beneath the water. I use the word beneath but it is a lie of convenience, for the thing did not have a location so much as it had a claim, a jurisdiction, and the water was only the topmost membrane of its dominion. I felt it the way I might feel that I am being watched, save that watching implies eyes and separation and the courtesy of two things regarding each other across a gap; here there was no gap. The thing did not watch me. It contained the possibility of me, as the sea contains the salt already dissolved in it, and my small terror was a chemistry occurring inside its indifferent body.

I wanted to run and found that running had become an abstraction I could no longer perform, a verb belonging to a grammar the night had suspended. My legs were present but the ground they would carry me across had grown suspect; the sand beneath my feet no longer felt like the reliable floor of the reliable earth but like the tautened hide of something breathing very slowly, and to run would be to run across the flank of the thing itself, to announce my minuteness to the whole extent of it. So I stood, and the tide went on withdrawing, and as it withdrew it uncovered shapes upon the flats that I did not permit myself to consider, wet mounded shapes that had the symmetry of intention without the mercy of resemblance to anything named.

The pressure grew. It ascended through registers I had no organs to measure, and I felt my thoughts begin to loosen from their fastenings, the way objects in a foundering ship slide first slowly and then all at once. Concepts I had trusted my whole life — that the world was made of separate things, that I was one of them, that tomorrow would proceed from tonight in the accustomed order — these did not so much break as reveal themselves to have always been decorative, a lace laid over an abyss to spare the eye. Beneath the lace was the true face of things, and the true face was vast, and old past the reach of the word old, and it was rising.

The reflected alien stars underfoot brightened. The sea drew back further, impossibly far, uncovering a plain of glistening dark that ran out beyond sight, and I understood at last that the tide had never been water leaving but a lid being lifted, and that what lay revealed was not a seabed but an interior, the inner surface of an eyelid, or the pale expanse of a countenance turning upward through fathoms toward the little smudge of warmth that was me. I felt the whole shore tilt without moving. I felt my name detach from me and drift, a small unclaimed sound, into the general roar of a silence too total to be called silence. My heartbeat became a rumor I could not verify. The night was no longer around me; it was inside the aperture that I had been, and through that aperture something regarded the world for the first time in an age, and found it there, and found me there, incidental, a mote caught in the beam of an attention that had no beginning and would concede no end.

I opened my mouth. Whether to scream or to worship I do not know, for the two had ceased to differ. The water was gone. The stars beneath were the only stars. And the vastness, having been noticed, having at long last permitted itself to be known, began — with a slowness that was itself the most terrible thing, a slowness like the turning of a year, like the cooling of a sun — to come up out of the deep and toward the place where I was standing, or where I had been standing, or where the shape of my standing still hung in the dark like a held note, waiting to be let go.
