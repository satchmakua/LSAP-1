"Buoy off the starboard bow!" Teo hollered from the rail, arm flung out.

"Got it, got it." Marsh swung the boat's nose over. "Hooked yet?"

"Reaching." Danny leaned with the throw hook, missed, spat, tried again. "There. Line's coming."

"On the block!"

"On the block, aye."

The hydraulics whined and the line came up out of the gray water dripping, snapping tight, and the whole deck tilted as a swell shouldered under them.

"Hold your feet," Marsh said.

"I'm holding, I'm holding, quit mothering."

"First pot," Teo called. "Up she comes."

"How's she look?"

"Ugly. Full of ugly."

"Ugly's money, count 'em."

The pot broke the surface streaming and swung and Danny caught it, muscled it onto the launcher rail while Teo popped the door.

"Dump."

They shook it and the crabs came out clattering, a slap and skitter of them across the sorting table.

"Count."

"One keeper, two, three — no, that one's soft, toss it."

"Soft goes back, aye."

"Four, five. Female, over the side."

"Bye, sweetheart," Danny said.

"Six keeper. Seven."

"Seven's a beauty. Look at the arms on that."

"Looks like Danny," Marsh said from the wheelhouse door.

"Danny wishes."

"Eight, nine. Short one, measure it."

Teo held the gauge. "Short. Back you go."

"Ten keeper."

"Rebait it, keep the string moving," Marsh said. "We got light for maybe two more hours and forty pots to run."

"Forty?"

"Thirty-eight now. Bait."

Danny slapped a fresh hanging bait bar into the pot, wired the jar shut, kicked the door. "Baited."

"Baited, aye. Clear the rail."

"Clear."

"Launch!"

The pot slid, splashed, gone, the line hissing off the coil after it.

"Line's running clean," Teo said.

"Watch your feet in the coil, that's how Pauly lost his — "

"Pauly still got both his feet."

"He got both his feet 'cause he was lucky, not 'cause he was smart," Marsh said. "Next buoy. Where's my buoy."

"There, there, two o'clock."

"I see it. Danny, hook."

"Reaching." A swell lifted the bow and dropped it. Danny went down on one knee, up again, hooked it on the fall. "Got him. Line."

"On the block."

"On the block, aye."

Up it came again, water sheeting off, and the second pot swung heavy.

"Heavier'n the first."

"Told you the deep water. Set 'em deep, that's what I said."

"Nobody said you were right, don't get married to it."

"Dump."

The clatter again, the skitter, the smell of them and of the bait and of the cold coming down off the north where the sky was going the color of a bruise.

"Count."

"One, two, three keepers. Female. Female. Bye now."

"Four. Five. That's a full one, would you look."

"Six, seven, eight."

"Eight! Now we're fishing."

"Nine keeper. Soft, toss it, ten. Eleven."

"Eleven off one pot, boys, write that down."

"Nobody's writing anything, keep counting," Marsh said.

"Twelve. That's the pot."

"Twelve, aye. Bait it."

"Baiting."

"Faster, the light."

"I got hands, boss, not wings."

"Grow some."

Teo laughed and the swell hit and everybody grabbed the rail without looking, the way you learn to, the deck sliding out from under and coming back like a thing breathing.

"Big set coming," Marsh said, eyeing off the stern. "Three of 'em. Hold up on the launch."

"Holding."

The first swell came green and high and the bow climbed it and the water broke over the rail knee-deep and rushed foaming across the deck and out the scuppers.

"Everybody good?"

"Good."

"Good."

"Wet," Danny said. "But good."

"You were already wet, quit whining."

"Second one."

The boat climbed, hung, dropped, the crab in the tanks below sloshing and scrabbling.

"Third."

"Here she is."

It broke and passed and the deck came level enough.

"Now launch."

"Launch, aye — clear the rail — clear — gone."

"Line's clean."

"Buoy?"

"Working on it, working on it. There. Danny."

"I see it, I'm on it." Hook out, hook down, and this time first try. "Got him."

"Show-off."

"Somebody's got to be good at something on this boat."

"On the block."

"On the block, aye."

The block screamed and the line jerked and stuttered.

"She's fouled," Teo said. "Snag on the bottom."

"Ease it, ease it, don't part the line."

"Easing."

"There she goes. There. Free."

"Free, aye. Up."

Third pot, and it came up light, mostly empty, a tangle of eelgrass and one small crab riding in the corner of it.

"Water haul."

"Not a water haul, there's one."

"One's a water haul with a hat on."

"Measure it anyway."

"Short. Back over."

"So — nothing."

"Nothing on that one. Rebait, launch, move on. Can't win 'em all," Marsh said. "That one was set shallow. Somebody set it shallow."

"That somebody was you, chief."

"Was not."

"Was so, I coiled that one, I remember the mark."

"Bait it and shut up, both of you."

"Baited."

"Launch."

"Gone."

They worked down the string that way, buoy and hook and block, dump and count and toss, bait and launch, the light leaking out of the sky a little more with every pot, the swells long and gray and rolling under them like the backs of something asleep and enormous.

"How many so far?" Danny asked.

"You want a number?"

"I want a number."

"Ninety, ninety-five keepers. Round it up. We been at it."

"Ninety-five! That's a good afternoon."

"That's a good afternoon if the last twenty don't skunk us. Buoy."

"Where."

"Dead ahead. Right where I put it."

"You put nothing dead ahead in your life," Teo said.

"I put you off this boat if you don't hook that line."

"Reaching. Swell — hold — got it."

"On the block."

"On the block, aye."

And the pot came up so heavy the block groaned and the line trembled and Danny had to put his shoulder into it and Teo got a hand on the bridle and the two of them hauled it swinging onto the rail.

"Look at this."

"Full to the mesh."

"Careful, careful, don't dump 'em in the drink."

"Door."

"Door's open — dump — "

They came out in a living heap, a crawling, clacking pile of them.

"Count fast, we're losing the light."

"One two three four keepers, five, six, female, female, seven, soft, back, eight, nine, ten — "

"Slow down, you'll miscount."

"He's not miscounting, he counts good, keep going."

"Eleven, twelve, short, back, thirteen, fourteen — "

"Fourteen!"

"Fifteen keeper, sixteen, and that's the table clear."

"Sixteen off one pot."

"That's the honey hole, boys. That's where the money lives."

"Mark that spot."

"It's marked, it's up here." Marsh tapped his temple through the wheelhouse window.

"We're doomed."

"Bait it, put it right back where it came from, right in the honey hole, easy — "

"Baiting. Wired. Door."

"Clear the rail."

"Clear."

"Launch."

"Gone. Right back home."

"That's the way. How many pots left?"

"Six. Then we run for the bay."

"Six and dark," Teo said, looking west at the last thin band of light under the cloud.

"Six and dark, we'll make it. Buoy."

"Got a buoy. Two of 'em, real close, watch it — "

"Take the near one, leave the far, we'll come round."

"Near one, aye. Danny."

"Reaching. Swell — hold — hold — got him."

"On the block."

"On the block, aye," Danny said, bracing his boots wide as the deck heaved and the line came up shining out of the dark water, and somebody laughed, and somebody said count 'em, and the block began again to scream.
