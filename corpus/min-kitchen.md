The guests left at ten. The door closed. The hallway light was on.

Mara stacked the plates. There were six plates. Two were clean. Four had food on them. She scraped the food into the bin. The bin was under the sink. She opened the cupboard with her knee.

Daniel carried the glasses. He held them by the stems. He held four glasses in one hand and two in the other. He set them on the counter. One glass had lipstick on the rim. It was not Mara's color.

The table had a cloth on it. The cloth was white. There was a red stain near the center. The stain was wine. There was another stain near the edge. That stain was smaller.

Mara ran the tap. The water was cold. She waited. The water got warm. She put the plug in the sink. She added soap. The soap made bubbles. She turned off the tap.

Daniel opened the window. Cold air came in. There was smoke from the candles. The candles were on the table. Two of them were still lit. He blew them out. The smoke rose and bent toward the window.

"Leave the window," Mara said.

He left the window open.

She washed the plates. She washed them one at a time. She set them in the rack. The rack was on the left of the sink. Water ran off the plates and pooled at the bottom of the rack. There was a hole for the water to drain.

Daniel took the cork from the bottle. The bottle was empty. He put the cork on the windowsill. There were three corks on the windowsill already. He looked at the bottle. He put it by the door with the other bottles. There were five bottles by the door.

The chairs were pushed back from the table. Mara counted them without meaning to. Six chairs. Two of them were turned sideways. One had a napkin on the seat. The napkin was folded. It had not been used.

She dried her hands on the towel. The towel hung on the oven handle. It was damp already.

"Do you want tea," she said.

"No," Daniel said.

He picked up the napkin from the chair. He shook it out. He folded it again. He put it in the drawer with the other napkins. The drawer stuck. He pushed it. It closed.

The clock on the wall said ten fifteen. It was three minutes fast. Neither of them had fixed it. The second hand moved. It made no sound.

Mara wiped the counter. She used a sponge. The sponge was blue. It had a green side for scrubbing. She used the soft side. She wiped in one direction. She rinsed the sponge. She wiped again.

There was a plate on the stove. It was the serving plate. There was still meat on it. The meat had gone cold. The fat had turned white. She looked at it. She covered it with foil. She put it in the fridge. The fridge light came on and went off.

Daniel carried the chairs. He carried them one at a time. He set them under the table. He pushed them in. Each chair scraped the floor. He did this six times. The last chair was heavier. It had an arm broken and taped. He set it against the wall instead.

"That chair," Mara said.

"I'll fix it," he said.

"You said that."

"I'll fix it."

She rinsed the wine glasses. She held each one to the light. The one with lipstick took two rinses. The lipstick was on the inside too. She used her thumb. It came off. She set the glass in the rack. The stems pointed down.

The salad bowl was on the table. There was oil in the bottom. There were seeds stuck to the wood. She brought it to the sink. She wiped it first. Then she washed it. The oil made the water cloudy.

Daniel stood by the window. He looked at the street. The street was empty. A car passed. Its lights moved across the ceiling. Then it was gone. He did not turn around.

"They left early," he said.

Mara did not answer. She washed the salad bowl. She set it upside down on the towel. It did not fit in the rack.

"It wasn't early," she said.

"It felt early."

She pulled the plug. The water went down. There was a sound. She rinsed the sink. She wiped the rim. She squeezed the sponge and set it on its edge behind the tap.

The cloth was still on the table. Mara went to it. She lifted the plates that were left. There were two side plates. There was a bread board. There was a knife on the board. The knife had butter on it. She carried these to the counter.

She took the corners of the cloth. She lifted them. Crumbs slid to the center. She gathered the cloth into a bundle. She carried it to the window. She shook it out over the street. Crumbs fell into the dark. She brought it back in. She folded it. The stains were on the inside now.

"That won't come out," Daniel said.

"I know."

"The red one."

"I know," she said.

She put the cloth on the chair with the broken arm. The table was bare. There were two rings on the wood. One was from a glass. One was from the bottle. She wiped them with the sponge. One ring came off. The other was in the varnish. It stayed.

Daniel closed the window. The room was quiet. The smell of smoke was still there. It was fainter now.

He picked up the two side plates. He put them in the sink. There was no water in the sink. He ran the tap. He washed them. He washed the knife. He washed the bread board. He was slower than she had been. She watched him. She did not say anything.

The candles were on the table still. Mara took them out of the holders. The wax was soft at the base. She scraped the holders with the knife handle. Wax came off in flakes. She put the flakes in the bin. She put the holders in the cupboard above the fridge.

"There's cake," Daniel said.

"They didn't want it."

"There's still cake."

"Put it away," she said.

He put the cake in a container. He pressed the lid down. It clicked at the corners. He put it in the fridge. The fridge light came on and went off.

The kitchen was clean. The rack was full. The counter was wet in one place. Mara wiped it. She hung the towel back on the oven handle.

Daniel turned off the tap. It had been dripping. Now it stopped.

They stood in the kitchen. The clock said ten forty. The floor had a mark where the chairs had scraped. Neither of them looked at it.

"Go up," Mara said. "I'll get the light."

Daniel went to the door. He stopped.

"It was fine," he said. "The dinner."

Mara turned off the light over the counter. The room went dark except for the hallway.

"Go up," she said.

He went up. She heard the stairs. She stood in the dark kitchen. She could see the rack. She could see the shapes of the glasses. She turned off the hallway light. Then the whole house was dark. She went up the stairs.
