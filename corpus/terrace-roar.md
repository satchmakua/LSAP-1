Ten minutes. Somebody says it and then everybody says it, ten minutes, ten minutes lads, the number passing down the terrace like a bucket of water at a fire, hand to hand, mouth to mouth, ten minutes and one goal, one goal and up we go.

We are on our feet. We have been on our feet since the second half started but now we are more on our feet than that, we are up on the concrete lip of it, we are leaning, we are a single body of two thousand leaning at the pitch as if we could tilt the whole town forward, tilt the grass, roll the ball toward their net by wanting it hard enough. We want it hard enough. We have wanted it since August, since the rain came sideways off the estuary in the first away game and eleven men and forty of us in a shed stood shivering and believing, we have wanted it through the dead middle of winter when nobody wanted anything, and now here it is, the wanting come round to meet us, ten minutes wide.

Nine.

The whistle in a mouth we cannot see. A free kick out wide. Come on. Come on now. Load it in. Load the box, put every red shirt in there, put the goalkeeper in there if you have to, put the manager, put us. We would go in ourselves. We would fill the six-yard box with our own soft bodies and let the ball come off our foreheads and our chests and our terrible hopeful hearts.

The chant starts at the back, low, a drum of feet on the old boards, and it climbs. We built this club.
