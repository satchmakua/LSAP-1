The lamps too bright, someone had said, turn one off, but no one moved and the light stayed on the faces like varnish, and Marian, holding her glass at the height of her collarbone as if it might steady her, thought: he is not coming, he said he might but he will not, and if he does I will be by the window and I will not turn. The window held the street in it, wet and doubled, cars smearing their reds along the black. Behind her a man was laughing at his own remark before it ended, the laugh climbing over the words, and she felt it go through the room like a draught.

—Twelve years, he was saying, twelve years and they still send the wrong invoice, you'd think, and here Peter lost the sentence because a woman had come in wearing green and he had loved a woman in green once, or the green had loved itself onto her, on a lawn, a striped chair, her mother calling from a doorway that was painted a color he could no longer summon, only the fact of paint, and he heard himself say twelve years again to fill the gap where the lawn had been.

Green. The green woman was called Estelle and she did not know she was called anything by anyone here, she was thinking of the taxi she had not paid enough for, the driver's eyes in the mirror, how he had not looked at her at all, and this—the not looking—had followed her up the stairs and into the room and stood beside her now while a man told her about invoices. She smiled the smile that required nothing of her. Somewhere a spoon rang against a bowl. If I leave at ten, she thought, if I say the sitter, but there was no sitter and no child, only the wanting to have somewhere urgent to be, the shape of urgency without its weight.

The clock on the mantel had stopped, she saw, at some hour that was not now and had perhaps never been now for this room. Marian saw it too from across the crush of shoulders and thought: even the clock has given up pretending. Give up pretending. Who had said that. He had said that, on the last night, lightly, cruelly, give up pretending you don't understand me, and she had understood him perfectly, that was the trouble, she had understood him down to the marrow and there was nothing there worth the understanding.

—You'll have another, said a voice at her elbow, not asking, and the bottle tilted its neck toward her glass and she watched the wine come without her permission, dark then bright as it thinned against the crystal. Thank you, she said to whoever. The whoever was Douglas and Douglas was full of the pleasant heat of being useful, of moving through the room with the bottle like a small mercy he could dispense, here, and here, and to the woman in green who covered her glass with her palm and to the laughing man who did not cover his and never would. Douglas thought: this is what I am good at. To be the one who pours. His wife across the room was telling the story of the ferry again and he could tell by the tilt of her head which part she had reached, the part where the gulls, and he had heard the gulls so many times they had become his own memory, though he had not been on the ferry, he had been at home with a fever, but the gulls belonged to him now, wheeling and screaming over a rail he had never gripped.

Screaming. There had been screaming once in a house at the end of a road and Marian was seven and the wine was doing that thing wine did, opening the little doors low down in her that stayed shut in daylight, and out came the road, the end of it, the way the house had leaned. Why now. The green woman had laughed—Estelle had laughed at nothing, at the invoice man's persistence, a laugh like a coin dropped—and the laugh unlatched the door, that was how it worked, one sound calling to another across years, the child's ear and the woman's ear the same ear after all.

—but the point is, Peter was saying, having recovered the lawn and lost it again, the point is that nobody reads them, the invoices, they go into a drawer, and I sometimes think—and he did think, genuinely, for a moment the whole party thinned to paper, everything a document filed and unread, himself a document, Estelle a document, the green a stamp of ink—I sometimes think we're all just—but he did not finish because finishing was not the custom here, one left the ends loose so that another could pick them up or not.

The room breathed. It took them all in and gave them back changed, each mouth borrowing a phrase from the mouth beside it, laughter propagating along the walls, and above the heads the smoke went up in slow considered spirals as though it alone had a plan. Estelle set her glass on the mantel beside the stopped clock and thought: I will remember this and not know why, this exact arrangement, the clock, the smoke, the man's twelve years, and in ten years it will come back to me at some traffic light and I will weep and not know why. She was often visited by the future's grief in advance. It was her particular gift, to mourn a thing while standing in it.

Marian by the window. If he comes I will not turn. But the door, when it opened, opened only on a couple leaving, their coats already on, the woman doing up a button as she stepped into the corridor's cooler light, and Marian felt the not-turning go slack in her, the great preparation wasted, and she almost laughed, and the almost-laugh was so like Estelle's that Estelle looked up sharply as at an echo of herself thrown back from the far side of the room, two women who did not know each other briefly wearing the same expression, the recognition without acquaintance, the sisterhood of the unarrived-for.

—Gulls, the wife was saying now, wheeling, and Douglas mouthed the word a half-second behind her, screaming, and the bottle in his hand was empty and he held it up to the light to be sure and through its green glass the whole room went underwater, wavering, the faces bending, the lamps too bright dimmed to a submarine gold, and for one held breath Douglas saw them all as swimmers, mouths open, moving slowly through a medium that was not air, reaching for each other and never quite, and then he lowered the bottle and the room stood up straight again and someone said his name.

The lamps too bright. Turn one off. No one moved. The light lay on the faces like varnish, preserving them, this hour that the clock refused to keep, and outside the cars unspooled their reds along the wet black street, and Marian drank, and Estelle mourned in advance, and Peter did not finish, and the green went on being green in the corner of everyone's eye like a field they had all once crossed, separately, on their way to becoming these particular strangers gathered in the yellow light, each thinking the room was theirs to think.
