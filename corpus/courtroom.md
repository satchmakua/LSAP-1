Members of the jury, the case before you does not depend on interpretation. It depends on sequence. The events of that night occurred in a fixed order, and once that order is established, the conclusion follows without room for alternative. I will now reconstruct that order for you, step by step, using only the evidence entered into the record.

Begin with the time. At 9:47 that evening, the defendant's mobile device connected to the cellular tower located at the intersection of Delaney and Fourth. This is not disputed. The carrier's records, entered as Exhibit 14, establish the connection to a precision of one minute. The tower has an effective radius of 0.8 kilometers. The victim's residence lies within that radius, at a distance of 0.4 kilometers. The defendant's own residence lies 3.6 kilometers away, well outside the range of that tower. Therefore, at 9:47, the defendant was not at home. He was near the victim's residence. That is the first fixed point.

Consider next the physical evidence at the point of entry. The rear door of the residence bore tool marks consistent with a flat pry instrument. The forensic examiner, whose qualifications the defense did not contest, testified that the marks were fresh, that the paint fracture had not oxidized, and that the wood fibers were displaced outward at the latch and inward at the hinge. This pattern indicates force applied from the exterior. The door was not opened from within. It was forced from without. That is the second fixed point.

Now observe how these two points connect. The connection is not one of speculation but of exclusion. Someone forced that door from the outside. The defendant was within 0.4 kilometers of that door at 9:47. I do not yet assert that these are the same act by the same person. I ask only that you hold both facts in place while I add the third.

The third point concerns the recovered instrument. A flat crowbar was found in a storm drain 120 meters from the residence, along the most direct route from the rear door to the parking area where the defendant's vehicle was later identified on camera. The crowbar bore two categories of evidence. First, its working edge matched the tool marks on the door to the exclusion of the tolerance tested by the examiner. Second, the shaft carried a partial fingerprint. That fingerprint, entered as Exhibit 22, matched the defendant's right index finger at twelve points of comparison. The defense offered no witness to dispute the match. They offered instead an argument that the print could have been transferred earlier. I will address that argument by sequence, because sequence answers it.

For the transfer theory to hold, the defendant must have touched that crowbar at some prior time, and the crowbar must then have traveled to the door, forced it, and come to rest in the drain, all without the defendant's involvement. This requires a chain of custody for the object that the defense has not supplied and cannot supply. It requires that the tool the defendant handled be the same tool that forced the door, which places the tool in the hands of whoever forced the door. The defense cannot have it both ways. If the print is old, the tool is still the tool, and the tool was still used. The print does not become innocent by aging. It becomes evidence that the defendant handled the instrument of entry. The sequence closes the exit the defense attempted to open.

Return now to the timeline inside the residence. The medical examiner fixed the time of the victim's death within a window from 9:30 to 10:15, based on liver temperature corrected for ambient conditions and on the progression of lividity. The window is wide because forensic science does not pretend to precision it lacks. I do not need the window to be narrow. I need only to establish that the defendant's presence at 9:47 falls inside it. It does. The death occurred while the defendant stood within 0.4 kilometers of the body, having handled the tool that forced the only door showing signs of entry.

Consider the interior scene. There was no sign of a struggle beyond the immediate area of the body. Drawers remained closed. The victim's wallet lay on the counter, containing cash. Nothing was taken. This is significant not for what it shows but for what it excludes. It excludes robbery as motive. A burglar who forces a door does so to acquire property. This intruder acquired nothing. The forced door was therefore not the door of a thief. It was the door of someone who came for the person inside. This narrows the field of persons with reason to be there, and it narrows it toward those with a relationship to the victim. The defendant had such a relationship. It is documented. Exhibit 7, the series of messages sent between the parties over the preceding eleven days, establishes a dispute that escalated in tone. The final message, sent by the defendant at 6:12 that evening, reads in full: "This ends tonight." Note the word "tonight." Note that the death occurred that night. The defendant selected the timeframe himself, in writing, hours in advance.

Let me now assemble the sequence in full, in the order the evidence compels. At 6:12, the defendant announced in writing that the matter would end that night. At some point before 9:47, he traveled from his residence toward the victim's, a journey inconsistent with his ordinary movements as reflected in the two prior weeks of location data. At 9:47, his device registered within the tower serving the victim's residence. Within the death window that includes 9:47, the victim died. The rear door was forced from the exterior by a flat pry instrument. That instrument was recovered along the direct exit route, bearing the defendant's fingerprint and matching the door marks. The interior showed no theft, excluding the motive that would point elsewhere. Every element points in a single direction, and no element points away.

The defense will ask you to consider the possibility of an unknown third party. I ask you to apply the same discipline of sequence to that possibility. For an unknown party to be responsible, that party would have to have forced the door with the defendant's crowbar, committed the act during the precise window when the defendant happened to be nearby, taken nothing, and departed, all while the defendant, having announced the ending of the matter that very night, arrived and then left without acting. This requires not one coincidence but a chain of them, each improbable, multiplied together. The law does not require you to disprove every conceivable fiction. It requires you to follow the evidence to the conclusion it supports. The evidence supports one conclusion, and the coincidences required to reach any other exceed the bounds of reason.

I have offered you no feelings tonight. I have offered you a sequence. Time, place, instrument, entry, absence of theft, stated intention. Each fact is fixed by the record. Each connects to the next. The order of these facts admits one actor at the center of them, and that actor sits before you. Follow the sequence, and it will bring you where it brought the evidence: to a single, unavoidable conclusion.
