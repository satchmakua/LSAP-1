The alley smelled like wet cardboard and old grease from the kitchen fan two doors down. Rain came off the fire escape in a thin steady line and hit the lid of a garbage can with a sound like somebody tapping a spoon against a glass, over and over, waiting to make a toast that never came. I stood in the doorway of a shuttered laundry and turned my collar up and watched the mouth of the alley where the streetlight made a yellow cone on the pavement.

She was late. They usually are. Being late is how they tell you they can afford to keep you waiting, or how they tell you they nearly didn't come. Both come out of the same wallet.

I'd worked twenty hours out of the last thirty. My eyes felt like they'd been dipped in sand and set out to dry. There was a flask in my coat, mostly for the smell of it now, and I didn't reach for it because reaching for it in an alley is how a man loses his footing and his gun in one motion. I just stood and smoked and let the ash go soft in the damp.

She came in from the wrong end. That told me something. Anybody honest comes in off the main street where the light is. She came the back way, from the parking court behind the tenements, heels quick and careful on the broken concrete, a raincoat belted tight and a scarf over her hair like she was somebody's idea of a woman in a movie about somebody like her.

"Mr. Calder," she said. Not a question.

"You're late."

"I couldn't get away."

"From what?"

She didn't answer that. She stopped a few feet off, in the half-dark between the streetlamp and the doorway, close enough that I could see the pale of her face and not much else. Rain beaded on the scarf. Somewhere back on the avenue a bus sighed and pulled away from a curb.

"You have it," she said.

"I have something." I flicked the cigarette into a puddle where it died with a small hiss. "Whether it's the something you're paying for depends on how straight you feel like being tonight. Last time we talked you told me your husband was in Cleveland."

"He was."

"He was in a motel on Route 9 with a woman named Doreen who does the books at his brother's dealership. Cleveland's a nice town but it's not that close to Route 9."

She was quiet. The garbage lid kept up its tapping. Somewhere a dog started and gave it up.

"You had someone follow him," she said.

"You hired me to. Or somebody did. You want to tell me who I've been working for, now we're both standing in the rain like a couple of fools?"

She looked back over her shoulder at the mouth of the alley. Her hands were in her coat pockets. I marked that and didn't like it, so I put my own hand where I could reach my own pocket and kept it there, easy, like a man warming his fingers.

"I told you the truth about the important part," she said.

"Sister, in my experience there's no such thing as the important part. There's just the part people leave out because it costs them, and the part they hand over free because it's already worthless."

"You're a hard man."

"I'm a tired one. Hard's for when I've had sleep."

She took a step closer and now the light caught her enough that I saw she was younger than her voice, and that she'd been crying at some point that day, or wanted me to think she had. The two look the same in an alley. That's the trouble with alleys.

"The photographs," she said.

I patted the flat shape inside my coat. "Right here. Eight of them. He's not a photogenic man. Neither's Doreen. But you'll know it's them."

"How much."

"We agreed on three hundred. You've paid one. That leaves two."

She brought a hand out of her pocket. Just an envelope. My shoulders came down a notch I hadn't known they were up. She held it out and I didn't take it right away. Taking things too fast is another way to lose your footing.

"Count it if you want," she said.

"I trust you. That's a joke. Stand still while I count."

I opened the envelope one-handed, the other hand still where it was, and thumbed the bills against the doorframe out of the wet. They were real, or real enough for the light I had. Two hundred, mostly twenties, one of them torn at the corner and taped. People who tape twenties have run out of choices further back than they let on.

"It's short forty," I said.

"I'll get you the forty."

"You'll get me the forty." I put the money away. "And I'll hold the pictures forty dollars' worth, which is one. You pick which one you want to be short."

"Don't," she said. Her voice cracked on it. Might've been real. "Please. I need all of them. He can't — if he keeps even one he'll say I made them up. He'll say I hired someone to fake them. He does that. He turns it around."

I looked at her a while. The rain filled the space where I wasn't talking.

"Who are you," I said. "Straight, now. You're not his wife. His wife's in a chair in a house on Linden with a nurse and a view of the river. I met her Tuesday selling encyclopedias. She doesn't wear a scarf and she doesn't come to alleys."

The woman's face did a thing then, a small collapse and a quick shoring-up, the way a sandbank goes when the tide takes the bottom out. "I'm Doreen," she said.

"There it is."

"He's going to leave me holding it. All of it. The books. He's been moving money through the dealership for two years and if his wife's people start pulling threads it lands on me. I keep the books. My signature's on everything." She wiped rain or something off her cheek with the back of her hand. "I need those pictures to prove there was more than money between us. That I wasn't just an employee cooking numbers. That there was — a reason."

"A reason," I said.

"You think that's nothing."

"I think a jury's warmer to a woman who loved a crook than to a woman who only counted for him. You're not wrong. You're just not the person who hired me, and the person who hired me is going to want to know why his pictures went to the mistress in a wet alley for a hundred and sixty dollars."

"How much," she said again, and this time there was no crack in it, and I understood we'd finally gotten down to the true floor of the thing, which is always money and never love, even when love is in the room.

"More than you've got tonight," I said. I took the envelope back out and put it in her wet hand and closed her fingers over it. "Keep it. Go home. Come see me tomorrow at the office where there's light and a chair, and bring whoever you really are, and we'll talk about what these pictures are worth to which people."

She stood holding the money like it might bite. The garbage lid tapped. The bus came back around, or another one did.

"You're letting me go," she said, like she didn't believe it.

"I'm going to sleep," I said, and stepped past her into the rain.
