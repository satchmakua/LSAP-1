To our valued shareholders, partners, and the wider community of stakeholders whose faith in us has been, this year, so thoroughly tested and, I am pleased to report, so richly rewarded:

It has been a transformational twelve months. I use the word deliberately, because transformation, as any student of the natural sciences will tell you, is rarely a gentle process. The caterpillar does not consult the butterfly about whether it wishes to be dissolved. And so it is with real innovation, which announces itself not through the tidy incrementalism our competitors mistake for progress, but through a series of what the timid might call setbacks and what we, here, have learned to call opportunities to demonstrate resilience.

Let me begin with the recall, since I know it is uppermost in your minds, and since candor is one of the four core values we adopted this year, replacing, at some cost, the previous three. It is true that we recalled every unit of our flagship product. It is true that we did so twice, the second recall being necessitated by a flaw in the remedy applied during the first. What is not true, and what I must gently correct, is the characterization of these events as failures. A failure is something one does not learn from. We learned a great deal. We learned, for instance, that our customers were using the product in precisely the manner our advertisements had encouraged, which produced results our engineers had frankly not anticipated at that scale. Is this not the very definition of discovery? Did the pioneers of aviation not fall repeatedly from the sky before they mastered it? We fell from the sky this year, shareholders, and I would ask you to consider how much we must now know about the ground.

The financial impact of the recalls has been, in the language of the analysts, significant. I prefer to think of it as an investment in trust, deferred. We have spent a considerable portion of our reserves demonstrating to the market that we are a company willing to spend a considerable portion of its reserves, and this signaling, in a world starved of authentic commitment, is worth more than any figure a spreadsheet can contain. The spreadsheets, I should add, are being reviewed. The gentleman responsible for the spreadsheets is, at the time of writing, pursuing other opportunities, as are several of his colleagues, and it is to these departures that I now, with genuine gratitude, wish to turn.

You will have read that our Chief Executive resigned in the spring. You will also have read that our Chief Financial Officer resigned in the summer, that our Chief Operating Officer resigned some weeks later, and that three members of the board resigned in what the press insisted on describing as an exodus, a word I find needlessly biblical. What the press declined to report, because it does not fit the narrative of decline they had already drafted, is that these were not losses but a deliberate and rigorous streamlining of leadership. We had, frankly, too many people who knew how things had always been done. An organization cannot disrupt itself while burdened by the memory of what worked. Each resignation, therefore, should be understood as a courageous act of self-subtraction, a voluntary clearing of ground so that the new may flourish. I am reminded of the gardener who prunes the rose not because he hates the rose but because he loves the bloom to come. We have pruned vigorously. We are, at present, mostly stem.

I myself was appointed to this role in October, following the departure of my three immediate predecessors, and I want to assure you that the rapidity of the turnover at the top reflects not instability but an admirable metabolic rate. A company that changes its leadership four times in a year is a company that refuses to grow comfortable, that resists the sedation of continuity, that keeps itself, if I may, on its toes. Our competitors have retained the same executives for a decade. I invite you to ask yourselves what such loyalty is concealing.

I would be doing you a disservice if I did not address the fire. I address it now with the seriousness it deserves and the perspective it demands. On the evening of the fourteenth, a small fire broke out in a supply room on the fourth floor of our headquarters. The fire was contained. No one was seriously hurt, a fact for which we are all profoundly and quietly thankful, and which I mention first because I know that a certain kind of reader has been waiting for me to bury it in the fourth paragraph of a section, which I have, though not for the reasons that reader supposes. The material damage was limited to a single room and its contents, those contents being, as it happens, the physical archive of our internal communications from the relevant period, which had not yet been digitized and which are, regrettably, no longer available for the review that certain parties had requested. I want to be absolutely clear that the timing of this fire, however unfortunate its optics, is best understood as a coincidence, and that coincidences, like recalls and resignations, are simply the texture of an ambitious year. We have since installed additional fire suppression systems, and I would point to this as evidence of a culture that responds to challenge with capital expenditure, which is, after all, what a culture is.

Some of you will ask, and you are entitled to ask, whether all of this amounts to a strategy or merely to a sequence of things that happened to us. I understand the question. I welcome it, in the way one welcomes a difficult guest, warmly and with a clear sense of when they might leave. But I would submit that the distinction you are drawing is a false one, a relic of the old thinking that separates intention from outcome, that clings to the quaint belief that a company should have meant to do the things it did. We have moved beyond that. We have embraced a mode of operation in which the events of the year are not judged against a plan but are themselves understood, retrospectively, as the plan. This is not evasion. This is the highest form of adaptability. To have foreseen none of it and survived most of it is a testament to a flexibility our rivals, with their forecasts and their contingencies, can only envy.

And so I look to the year ahead with an optimism I want to describe as earned. We have cleared out the old leadership, cleared out the old inventory, cleared out, in one localized instance, the old paperwork, and we stand before you lean, unburdened, and free. Our balance sheet is a canvas. Our reputation, once a heavy and fragile thing, is now light enough to be reimagined entirely. We are, in every sense that matters, a startup again, with the added advantage of decades of institutional experience, most of which has recently and conveniently departed.

I thank you for your continued investment, by which I mean your continued patience, by which I mean your continued presence, which we do not take for granted, and which we intend to reward in a manner and on a timeline to be announced. The future belongs to those brave enough to have already lost this much. We have lost it. The future, therefore, is ours.

With enduring confidence and sincere regard, on behalf of a leadership team assembled this morning.
