By nine o'clock the water had climbed to the third mark on the gauge below the Front Street bridge, and Ora Belliveau, who had watched that gauge for forty years and had buried two husbands within earshot of it, called down the line that they would need every hand and every sack the town could muster before dawn, and the word went out the way such words go, from porch to porch and phone to phone, until the whole low grid of the settlement was awake and moving under the sodium lamps.

Down at the sand pile behind the co-op, Denny Fowler backed the loader into the mound and swung the bucket high and let the wet sand pour into the truck bed while the Hutchins boys, Cole and Levi and the youngest one they all called Sparrow, held the burlap open two at a time and cinched the necks with baling twine and threw them up onto the flatbed until their arms shook, and beside them old Rennert counted aloud, one hundred, two hundred, three, keeping the tally in a spiral notebook he wet through in the first hour and kept writing in anyway with a carpenter's pencil that would not quit.

The trucks ran the sacks down River Road to the weak place below the tannery, where the levee had always seeped in the spring and where Marguerite Sosa now stood in hip boots directing the drop, pointing here and here and no, higher, stack them stepped like a stair, and behind her Tomas Okafor and the two Vandenberg sisters and a boy from the Lutheran youth group whose name nobody yet knew formed a bucket line of bodies instead of buckets, passing each fifty-pound sack hand to hand up the wet grass slope, grunting, laughing once when Sparrow slipped and sat down hard in the mud and came up grinning, and the line did not break, the line only shifted to close the gap and kept the sacks moving.

Meanwhile the water rose. It rose behind the willows at the bend and it rose against the pilings of the old ferry landing and it rose in the storm drains so that black water welled up cold through the gratings of Second Street, and Chief Halloran walked the length of the levee with a flashlight in one hand and a radio in the other and read off the numbers as they came, a foot below the top at the tannery, ten inches at the school field, holding at the bridge, and his voice on the radio was not afraid, it was only steady, the way a man's voice is steady when he has decided there will be time enough to be afraid later or never.

At the school field the churches had opened the gym and set the long tables end to end, and there Ruthie Pell and her daughter and three women from the quilting circle poured coffee into paper cups and ladled chili into bowls and sent them out into the dark on trays carried by teenagers who had never before been trusted with anything, and the coffee went down the line at the tannery and down the line at the bridge and warmed men who had stopped feeling their hands, and Ruthie kept the urns filling and the pot on the burner and did not sit down once, not once, between nine and the gray of morning.

By midnight the crest was said to be at the county line upriver and coming, and the pace changed, and everyone felt it change; Denny drove the loader harder and the bucket clanged and the diesel roared and the Hutchins boys stopped talking and only filled and cinched and threw, filled and cinched and threw, and Marguerite Sosa climbed onto the growing wall of sacks herself and stamped them down flat with her boots so they would knit together and not shift when the pressure came, and Tomas Okafor waded out into the shallow flooding at the base and found with his feet the boil where the river was pushing up under the levee and called for the ring of sacks that would hold it, and they built the ring around the boil the way she told them, a low round crown of burlap to press back against the pressing water, and it worked, the boil settled, the water in the ring rose to its own level and stopped, and Tomas came up out of the dark grinning with mud to his collarbone.

The rain, which had held off all evening as if out of some courtesy, began now at one in the morning, first as a fine mist and then as a hard slanting downpour that turned the beams of the trucks to solid pillars of falling water, and still the line held and still the sacks went up, and now Father Amadi came down from the gym in his shirtsleeves and took a place in the line without a word and the boy from the Lutheran youth group made room, and now the widow Belliveau, who had started the whole night with a phone call, walked the top of the wall with a lantern and told each of them by name that they were doing well, Levi, you are doing well, Cole, hold there, Sparrow, dear, drink the coffee before it's cold, and something in the naming kept them upright past the hour when their bodies had wanted to lie down.

At two the water reached the second-to-last mark and at three it touched the last, and Chief Halloran stood at the bridge and watched the black river slide by inches below the lip of the barrier they had made, watched it slide and press and search along the seam of sandbags for a way through, and he said into the radio only, this is it, this is the crest, hold what you've got, and all down the levee they stopped adding and only held, standing on the wall, leaning their weight into it, watching the water find the top and stop there, quivering, held.

And it held. For an hour the river stood at that terrible even height and did not rise, the way a held breath does not rise, and the rain slackened and then quit, and somewhere behind the clouds the sky began the slow business of going from black to charcoal to the dirty pewter of a river dawn, and Marguerite Sosa, stamping one last sack into place, felt under her boots that the pressure had eased, and she straightened up and looked at the gauge and saw what she had prayed all night to see, a hair of dark wet wood showing above the waterline where before there had been none, and she said it quietly first, it's dropping, and then she said it loud, it's dropping, it's going down, and the word went back up the line the way the first word had come down, tired mouth to tired mouth.

Then they sat where they stood. Denny cut the loader and the great silence rushed in behind the diesel. The Hutchins boys lay back on the wet grass with their ruined hands open to the lightening sky and Sparrow laughed once and could not stop. Tomas Okafor sat down in the mud beside his boil and put his arm around the shoulders of the boy whose name he now knew was Peter. Ruthie Pell carried out the last of the coffee and this time she sat. And Ora Belliveau stood on the top of the wall they had built out of nothing but sand and burlap and one long night of ordinary people, and she looked at the river going down, and she did not say anything at all, because there was nothing left that needed saying.
