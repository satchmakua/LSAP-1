The refrigerator stopped humming at 3:14, and that was the first thing.

I know it was 3:14 because I looked, and I looked because the silence arrived the way a held breath arrives, all at once and with intention. Refrigerators cycle. This is a known fact about them, a thing I have understood my whole life without ever once thinking about it, and the not-thinking is precisely what troubles me now, because when a machine you have never noticed suddenly becomes noticeable, the noticing is not neutral. Something wants to be seen. Or something wants me to see that it can be seen, which is different, which is worse. I sat very still on the edge of the couch with my hands flat on my knees and waited for the hum to return, and when it did, forty seconds later, the return felt like a correction. Like a thing that had revealed too much and was now pretending.

I want to be reasonable. I keep telling myself I want to be reasonable. But reasonableness is a posture, I've realized, a way of holding your face, and I don't trust my face anymore because I can't see it and it's doing things without me.

The mail came at the wrong time. Ordinarily the mail comes between one and two; today it came at four-forty, and I heard the little metal mouth of the box downstairs snap shut, and I thought: why late. And then I thought: because they waited. Because there was a decision about when, and the decision was four-forty, and four-forty is after 3:14 by exactly the interval it would take a person to receive a report about the refrigerator and act on it. I did not go down for the mail. I understood that going down was what I was supposed to do, that the whole arrangement of the afternoon was a kind of funnel narrowing toward the stairwell, and that if I descended I would be somewhere they knew I would be, and being where you're expected is the entire mechanism of the trap. So I stayed. But staying is also a choice, and if they anticipated the staying then the staying is also compliance, and there is no move I can make that is not a move they already have a card for. This is the thing about a well-designed situation. It closes around you like weather.

The neighbor above me walked to the east wall, stopped, and walked back. Three times. I counted. There is no reason to walk to a wall and stop, unless the wall is where you can be heard best, unless the stopping is a signal to whoever is below listening, and I was below, and I was listening, so who else could it be for. I pressed my ear to the ceiling — I stood on the arm of the couch, which wobbled, and I steadied myself against the light fixture, which was warm, which it should not have been because I had the light off, I had had the light off for hours precisely so that no one could read the shadow of me against the curtain — and through the plaster I heard nothing, which is not the same as nothing being there. Silence is the most convincing evidence of all because it is what a careful person produces. A careless person makes noise. A careful person, a person with a purpose, moves like water in a glass.

I should call someone. I keep arriving at that thought and it keeps dissolving before I can hold it, because to call someone I would have to use the phone, and the phone is the most compromised object in the apartment, it is a microphone I pay for, it is a small bright rectangle that knows where I am and has always known and reports it the way a fever reports an infection. When I picked it up an hour ago the screen showed a notification I do not remember enabling, a little gray banner that said "Your week in review," and I stared at it until the words came apart. My week. In review. Reviewed by whom. Compiled by whom. There is a whom, that's what the passive voice hides, that's what it's for, that's why they built the whole language to have a place where the doer disappears. Your week in review. My week has been reviewed and found — what. Found worth reviewing. That's the part. You don't review what doesn't matter.

The light through the curtain has been changing, and I've been tracking it, and I want to say it's just the sun going down but the quality of it is wrong, it has a graininess, like it's being fed to me at a slightly lower resolution than the world used to have, and I think about how you'd never notice if the fidelity of everything dropped by two percent, you'd just feel vaguely that things weren't quite, and you'd blame yourself, you'd say I'm tired, I'm anxious, I need to eat, and the beauty of it, the awful economy of it, is that the explanation is always waiting inside you, pre-installed, so that you never look outward, so that every alarm bell gets muffled in the padding of your own supposed dysfunction. That's the trick. Make the victim the diagnosis.

Someone flushed a toilet and the pipes in the wall spoke, a long shuddering vowel, and I felt it in my teeth, and I thought: they're moving the water to hear where I am by how I react to the sound. I held completely still. I did not react. I want it on the record, if there is a record, and there is always a record, that I did not react, that I gave them nothing, that I sat in the dark with my hands on my knees and my breath shallow and even and betrayed them by being unreadable. But then I thought: stillness is data too. A guilty man is still. A man with nothing to hide fixes himself a sandwich. And I hadn't fixed myself a sandwich in — I couldn't remember, and the not-remembering opened under me like a stair that isn't there in the dark, that sick drop, because a person who can't account for his own hours is a person who has been somewhere, has done something, and doesn't have it, and where does a lost hour go, who takes it, what is it used for while it's gone.

The refrigerator stopped again. 6:02 now. I checked. The interval was two hours and forty-eight minutes, and I did the arithmetic three times because arithmetic is the last honest thing, numbers can't smile at you, and two hours and forty-eight minutes is one hundred sixty-eight minutes, and one hundred sixty-eight is the number of hours in a week, and my week, they said, was in review, and here it was again folded into the silence of a machine that has decided, after a lifetime of anonymity, to start speaking to me in the only language it has, which is the language of stopping, of the withheld hum, of the held breath, and I am sitting here in the grain of the failing light understanding, finally, that the ordinary was never ordinary, that it was only ever waiting for me to be alone enough to hear it, and now I am, and now I do.
