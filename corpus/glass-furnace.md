The furnace is already breathing when I arrive. I open the door and the heat leans out against my chest. Inside the glass sits pooled in the crucible, orange and slow, waiting. I set my tools along the bench in the order I will need them. The jacks go on the left, the shears beside them, the wet paper folded in a shallow dish of water. I check the marver and wipe it clean with my palm. I lay the blowpipes on the pipe warmer and let them take the first heat. This is how each shift begins, the same arrangement, the same small readiness.

I take the first pipe and turn it in the glory hole until the end glows. I bring it to the crucible and dip it into the molten glass. I turn the pipe and the glass climbs onto the steel in a thick clear bead. I lift it out and carry it to the marver and roll it flat and even. The gather cools at the edges and I feel the weight settle on the pipe. I blow a short breath into the pipe and cap the end with my thumb. The trapped air makes a small bubble at the center of the gather. This first bubble will become the body of the goblet.

I return to the glory hole and reheat the gather until it softens. I roll the pipe against the arm of the bench and keep the glass centered. I blow again, longer this time, and the bubble grows. I close the shape with the jacks, pressing a line near the pipe to mark the neck. The glass answers the tool and holds the line. I turn and blow and turn, letting the form open under its own weight. When the body is the size I want, I stop and let it stiffen. The rhythm of turning never leaves my hands.

I gather a second time over the first to build the wall. I dip and turn and lift and marver, and the shape grows heavier and rounder. I reheat and shape and reheat and shape, and each cycle adds a little glass and takes a little heat. I work the profile with the jacks until the curve is smooth from neck to base. I run the wet paper along the surface to true the wall and cool the skin. The paper hisses and smokes and I smell the char of it. The goblet body is ready for its stem.

I take a second gather on a punty and let it cool to a working softness. I bring the small gather to the base of the body and press the two together. I turn the pipe and pull the stem down with the jacks in a slow steady draw. The glass stretches thin and clean between the tools. I let it lengthen and then close my hands to set the width. The stem cools straight and I release the pull. The proportion of stem to bowl decides the balance of the whole cup.

I make the foot last, before the transfer. I take a small gather and lay it at the end of the stem and open it with the jacks. I turn the pipe faster and the foot spins wide and flat. I true the edge with the paper and check the level by eye. The foot must sit flat or the goblet will rock. I turn and watch the rim of the foot come even. When it holds level I stop and let it firm.

Now the transfer. I bring the punty and touch a small hot gather to the center of the foot. I press and turn and the two steels are joined through the glass. I score the neck at the pipe with a wet tool and tap the pipe once. The goblet parts from the blowpipe and stays on the punty. I carry it to the glory hole with the open bowl now facing out. This is the turning point of every cup, the moment the top becomes the work.

I reheat the open neck until it flushes orange. I open the bowl with the jacks, spreading the lip outward as I turn. The rim widens and the bowl deepens under the steady pull of rotation. I keep the pipe level and let centrifugal motion do the shaping. I check the diameter against my thumb and finger held to the light. When the bowl reaches the width I want I let it slow and cool. I trim the lip with the shears to a clean even circle. The rim comes off crisp and I roll it once more to soften the edge.

I knock the goblet free of the punty over a gloved hand. I carry it to the annealer and set it upright among the others. The oven holds the finished cups at a warm even heat. I close the door and let the glass rest and slowly cool. Sudden cold would crack it, so the annealer holds the night. I return to the bench and clear the scrap into the cullet bin. I wipe the jacks and lay them back in their place. One goblet stands in the oven and the run has begun.

I take a fresh pipe and warm it at the pipe warmer. I dip into the crucible and lift the second gather. I marver and blow and shape and the second body grows. The motions are the same and my hands know them without thought. I build the wall with a second gather and pull the stem and spread the foot. I transfer and open the bowl and trim the rim and knock it free. I set the second goblet beside the first in the annealer. The two cups stand together and the shift takes on its measure.

The hours pass in gathers and I count the work by cups. I dip and turn and blow and jack and shear and set, and dip again. The furnace holds its steady roar and the glass keeps its steady glow. My arms find the weight and my breath finds the bubble and the shapes repeat. Cup by cup the annealer fills and the bench stays clean. I stop between goblets only to drink water and stretch my back. The heat is constant and the light is orange and the day is measured in glass.

The seventh cup comes off the punty a little heavier than the sixth. I set it in the oven and adjust the next gather to match. The eighth and ninth run true and I take a small quiet pleasure in their sameness. A run of goblets should look like one goblet made many times. I check the row through the annealer window and see the shapes agree. The stems stand at the same height and the bowls hold the same curve. This is the whole of the satisfaction, the line of them alike.

I make the twelfth and last in the same unhurried order. I dip and marver and blow and shape and pull and spread. I transfer and open and trim and knock it free and set it down. Twelve goblets stand warm in the annealer in an even row. I close the oven door and set it to cool through the coming hours. I bank the furnace and lay the tools clean along the bench for tomorrow. The glass will be ready when I return, and I will lift the first one out.
