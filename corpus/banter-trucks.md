"That cable is mine."

"That cable is communal. Says so on the permit."

"The permit says shared generator. It does not say your truck gets to hum like a dying refrigerator all night while mine flickers."

"My truck sounds like ambition. Yours sounds like a printer out of ink."

She plugged in anyway. The lights over her window came up gold, and the ones over his dropped to a sad amber, and somewhere a fryer sighed.

"You just stole four amps from me," he said.

"I redistributed them. To a worthier cause."

"Worthier. You sell corn."

"I sell elote with three chiles and a lime cream that has made grown men weep at this festival, and you sell—what is it again? Fusion?"

"It's not fusion."

"It has kimchi on a taco."

"That's not fusion, that's Los Angeles."

"It's a hate crime against two cuisines at once."

He wiped his hands on the towel at his belt and leaned out his window toward hers, six feet across the humming little valley of the generator. "Try it before you insult it. I dare you."

"I don't take food from strangers."

"We've shared a generator for three hours. We're practically married."

"That is the least appealing proposal I have ever received, and I once got proposed to at a funeral."

But she took the taco. He watched her not want to like it. He watched her jaw do the small betraying thing, the little downward settle of surprise, before she got her face back under control.

"It's fine," she said.

"It's fine."

"It's edible."

"You closed your eyes."

"I blinked. Human beings blink. It's involuntary, like my contempt for you."

The line at her window was longer. It was always longer; he'd stopped pretending not to count. Four people at his, eleven at hers, a kid on a scooter idling in between like a referee.

"You should send me your overflow," he said.

"My overflow would riot if I sent them to Kimchi Man."

"That's not my name."

"It's what the group chat calls you."

"There's a group chat?"

"There's a vendor group chat, and you're not in it, and there's a reason for that."

"Because you're all afraid of me."

"Because you microwaved fish at the vendor meeting and Denny still hasn't recovered."

"That was branzino and it was the best thing that ever happened to that parking lot."

She turned back to her window, and he watched the back of her, the quick clean economy of her hands, corn and char and the little squeeze of lime, the way she called every customer *mi amor* like she meant it, which was the thing that got him, actually, that she said it like she meant it and probably didn't and he couldn't tell the difference and hated that he wanted to.

"You're staring," she said, without turning.

"I'm supervising the theft of my electricity."

"Supervise from your own window."

Nine o'clock. The band down at the corner started something with too much bass and the generator coughed and both trucks went dark at once. A groan went up from both lines, a shared human sound, the festival's collective disappointment.

"You overloaded it," they said, together, and then, "I did not," together, which was worse.

He was out and around the front of his truck before he decided to be. She was out too, both of them crouched over the machine in the dark like surgeons, phones out for flashlights.

"Move your light."

"You move yours, you're blinding me."

"Good. Then you can't see how right I am about this being your fault."

"It reset. It's a breaker. Here." He flipped it. The trucks came back, hers gold, his amber, the eternal injustice. She didn't move. He didn't either. They were very close over the warm machine, and it was loud, and she smelled like lime and smoke and he thought, with the small horror of a man watching himself fall down stairs, *oh no.*

"You have char on your face," she said.

"So do you."

"I'm a professional. I wear it on purpose."

"Right here." He almost reached up. He put his hand back in his pocket like it had misbehaved.

They stood. The line at her window had started to grumble.

"Go," he said. "Your people need you."

"They're not my people, they're customers."

"You call them *mi amor.*"

"I call everyone that."

"I noticed."

Something crossed her face and she went back to her window fast, and he went back to his, and for twenty minutes neither of them said anything, just the sizzle and the calling out of orders, and he told himself he was relieved.

Then a paper boat came sailing over the generator and landed on his prep counter. Elote. One ear, dressed, still steaming.

"What's this," he called.

"Charity. You look thin. Emotionally."

He ate it. He didn't want to like it either and that made two of them, liars over a machine. The lime cream was doing something he'd been trying to do to a crema for two years and failing. It made him angry. It made him want to know how.

"It's fine," he called.

"It's fine."

"You put cotija in the cream, not just on top."

Her head came up. "How did you—"

"I have a palate. It's the only thing about me you respect."

"I don't respect your palate. I respect that you finally admitted mine is better."

"I said it was fine."

"You ate it in four bites and you licked the boat, I watched you, it was disgusting, I'm going to marry you."

"You said that was the worst proposal you'd ever—"

"It's growing on me. Like mold. Send me a taco."

He sent her a taco. He watched her eat it in the dark of her truck between customers, standing, one hand on the counter, and she didn't perform this time, didn't make the face for him, just closed her eyes for real, for herself, and he had to look away because it felt like reading something over her shoulder.

By eleven the lines thinned to nothing and the band packed up and the festival began the slow business of becoming an empty street again. They sat on the generator itself, back to back almost, on the flat top of it, sharing its warmth because the night had gone cold.

"Slow now," she said.

"Mm."

"You didn't sell out."

"Neither did you."

"I did, actually. Twenty minutes ago. I've been giving the last ones away."

"Then what did you send me?"

"The last one."

He didn't say anything for a second. "You gave me your last ear of corn."

"Don't make it a thing."

"It's a thing."

"It's a vegetable."

"Marry me," he said, and felt his heart do something undignified.

"That's not funny."

"I wasn't being funny. I'm never funny. I'm the fish guy, remember. I'm not in the group chat."

She turned around on the generator to look at him and the warm metal creaked and her knee bumped his and neither of them moved it away.

"I'll add you to the group chat," she said. Quietly. Like it cost something.

"That's the most romantic thing anyone's ever said to me."

"Your bar is on the floor."

"It's underground. It's in the parking lot. Next to your truck. Every weekend, apparently, since the schedule came out and I saw your name on it and requested the spot next to you."

"You—" She stopped. "That was you? I thought that was a coincidence."

"There are no coincidences. There's only me, and bad decisions, and your lime cream."

She was quiet. Then she leaned, the small distance, and wiped the char off his cheek with her thumb, unhurried, and left her hand there a second longer than the char required.

"There," she said. "Now you look almost edible."

"Was that a compliment?"

"It was fine," she said, and didn't take her hand away, and the generator hummed on between the two dark trucks like something that had gotten exactly what it wanted.
