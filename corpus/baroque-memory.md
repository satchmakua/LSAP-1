The thimble sat in the cup of his palm the way a small bell sits before it is struck, holding within its dented silver skin all the silences it had ever gathered, and he turned it slowly, not because he wished to examine it, for he knew every scratch upon it as one knows the particular weather of a room slept in for decades, but because the turning was itself a kind of listening, a way of pressing his old ear against the thin partition that separates the present, which is always so scantly furnished, so provisional and drafty a place, from the vast overstuffed chambers of what has already been lived and lost and, he understood now with the peculiar clarity that arrives only when the hands begin to tremble, never truly relinquished, only misfiled, only laid aside in some drawer of the self whose location one forgets until an object like this, small enough to swallow, heavy as a continent, insists upon being found.

It had been his mother's, or so he had always been told, though the telling and the thing had long ago fused into a single artifact of belief, and as the light from the window slid across its rim he felt the first of the memories arrive not as a picture but as a temperature, the specific overwarm closeness of a kitchen in a season that no calendar could any longer name, a warmth that carried within it the smell of scorched cotton and boiled milk and the faint mineral tang of the sewing needles she kept clustered like a small silver reed-bed in a pincushion shaped, absurdly, tenderly, like a tomato, and he was there, he was small, he was beneath the table where the light came in bars and the legs of chairs were the trunks of a forest and his mother's feet in their worn slippers pressed the treadle of the machine in a rhythm that was the first music he had ever loved, before he had words for love or music or loss, when the world was only the underside of things and the certainty that above him, forever, someone was making something whole out of something torn.

From that warmth the next memory did not so much follow as bleed, in the way that watercolors laid too wet upon the page will find each other and marry against the painter's intention, and he was older, he was standing in a doorway he could not have described a moment before and now could see down to the grain of its wood, watching his mother thread a needle by holding it up against the pale square of the window and squinting, her mouth pursed, her whole face gathered into that one act of aiming the frayed thread through the impossibly small eye, and he remembered—no, he did not remember, he simply was again inside—the enormous patience of it, the way she would fail and fail and fail and moisten the thread's end between her lips and try again without complaint, as though the difficulty were not an obstacle but the very substance of the task, as though everything worth doing were finally a matter of coaxing something reluctant through an opening too narrow for it, and he wondered, standing now in his actual body in his actual room with the thimble grown warm in his fist, whether he had learned from that patience anything at all or whether he had spent the long middle of his life doing precisely the opposite, forcing, tearing, dragging the thread by main strength through eyes that were never meant to hold it, and calling the resulting damage a life.

And then, because memory obeys no chronology but only the secret gravities of feeling, he was falling backward and forward at once into a winter, a particular winter whose cold he could feel in the small bones of his feet, when he had been sick with something that kept him in bed for what seemed to the child he had been the entire duration of the world, and she had sat beside him mending in the lamplight, the thimble on her finger catching the flame so that each stitch was accompanied by a tiny flare, a minute recurrent dawn, and he had watched from the fever's floating distance the small silver cap rising and falling, rising and falling, pushing the needle through the resistant cloth, and had believed, with the total unironic faith of the very young or the very ill, that this was what held the night together, that her stitching was somehow the same labor by which the dark outside was kept from coming apart at its seams, and he had fallen asleep to it, safe, so wholly and unrepeatably safe that the memory of that safety now arrived less as comfort than as accusation, a grief so pure it had no object, only the fact of its own vanishing.

He closed his hand entirely around the thimble then, as if to keep the cascade from carrying him past the point of return, but the closing only pressed the metal deeper into the meaning of his skin, and there rose in him, unbidden, the last time he had seen her use it, when her hands had already begun their own long forgetting and the thread wandered and the stitches came out crooked as a child's first fence, and he had wanted, God how he had wanted, to take the work from her and finish it himself, to spare them both the spectacle of the diminishment, and had not, had sat instead in a cowardice he now recognized as its own bruised form of respect, letting her labor at the ruined seam because to stop her would have been to say aloud the thing that neither of them would say, and she had looked up at him at last with the thimble still on her finger and smiled the smile of someone who knows she is losing the map but has decided to be kind about it, and had said something ordinary, something about the weather or the tea, and the ordinariness of it was the whole of the tragedy, was the very shape of how the ones we love depart, not in grand renunciations but in the slow surrender of small competences, the buttons no longer managed, the names beginning to slide.

Now the room had gone dim without his noticing, the light having drained westward while he stood inside the amber of all that had been, and he understood that this was what remained to him, this was the whole inheritance, not the silver itself, which was worth nothing, which any stranger would sweep into a box with the other unclaimed residue of a life, but the capacity of a worthless thing to become a door, to open onto rooms otherwise sealed, and he thought that perhaps this was the final and most secret function of objects, to serve as the small dense knots at which the loose thread of a life is tied so that when we tug, however feebly, however late, the whole garment gathers toward us for one last instant of nearly unbearable wholeness before it slips again, inevitably, out of the needle's narrow eye and into the dark that no stitching, not his mother's, not his own, not anyone's, has ever finally been able to hold together.
