"The ring was in the cake."

"There's no cake."

"There was a cake, Margaret, at the rehearsal, and I put the ring in it for safekeeping."

"You put the ring in a cake."

"For safekeeping!"

Door 412 opened and a man in a half-buttoned tuxedo shirt leaned out. "Is somebody yelling about a cake? Because we ordered a cake."

"That's not our cake," said Margaret.

"Whose cake is it?"

"There is no cake!" said the first man, whose name was Douglas, and who was the father of a bride he could no longer locate. "The point is the ring, the point is my son needs the ring in eleven minutes, and it is not in my pocket, and it is not in the cake, because there is no cake."

"You just said there was a cake."

"Down there, the cart," Margaret said, pointing at a room-service cart abandoned midway along the corridor, a silver dome gleaming under a covered plate. "Maybe somebody moved the cake to the cart."

"Nobody moved a cake to a cart."

Door 410 opened. A woman in a lilac bridesmaid dress stepped out with a shoe in each hand. "Has anyone seen a groom? Ours has escaped."

"Escaped which way?" said Douglas.

"That way." She pointed at the cart.

"We're looking at the cart," said the tuxedo-shirt man from 412.

"Why is everyone looking at the cart," said the bridesmaid, and she looked at the cart, and lifted the silver dome, and there was no cake, there was a club sandwich, and she said, "This is a club sandwich."

"That's ours," said 412. "That's my club sandwich."

"Then where is your groom," said Margaret, "if your sandwich is here and your groom is not."

"He's not my groom, he's her groom—" and 412 gestured backward into the room, where a woman in an enormous white dress now appeared, holding a champagne flute at a dangerous angle.

"Have they found Kevin?" said the bride in the enormous dress.

"Who's Kevin?"

"Kevin is the groom!"

"I thought your groom escaped," Douglas said to the bridesmaid.

"Different groom," said the bridesmaid. "Ours is Patrick. Their Kevin is nothing to do with our Patrick."

"There are two grooms loose in this hallway," said Margaret, sitting down abruptly on the cart, which rolled.

"Don't sit on the sandwich!"

The cart, with Margaret on it, gathered speed toward door 408, which opened precisely then, and a bellhop stepped through with a stack of towels, and Margaret and the cart and the club sandwich went into the bellhop, and the towels went up into the air like startled birds, and the sandwich achieved flight, and door 408 slammed behind the bellhop who was now on the floor beneath Margaret saying, "Ma'am, ma'am, the cart is a moving vehicle, ma'am."

"The ring!" screamed Douglas, because the sandwich, in disassembling itself midair, had ejected a small bright circle that pinged off the wall and rolled.

"That was in the sandwich!"

"How was the ring in the sandwich!"

"Somebody moved it from the cake!"

"THERE IS NO CAKE."

The ring rolled. Everyone watched the ring roll, which it did with the malice of a small round thing that knows exactly where the drain is. It rolled past 408, past 410, and door 410 opened again and Patrick the escaped groom stepped out with lipstick on his collar and said, "Am I late?" and stepped directly on the ring, which shot out from under his heel like a watermelon seed and disappeared under door 406.

"It went under 406," said the bridesmaid.

"Whose room is 406?"

Nobody knew whose room was 406. Douglas knocked. The bride in the enormous dress finished her champagne. The bellhop crawled out from under Margaret. Door 406 did not open, but door 414, all the way at the far end, did, and a small furious woman in a bathrobe with a mud mask on called down the hall, "It is MIDNIGHT."

"We know!" everyone said.

"My son is being married at midnight," Douglas explained, "which was a very romantic idea nine minutes ago."

"Knock on 406 again," said Margaret.

Douglas knocked on 406. The door opened. It was Kevin. It was, apparently, Kevin, wearing a groom's boutonnière and holding the ring between two fingers with the expression of a man who has found a ring under his door and does not know it is anybody's business.

"That's my ring," said Douglas.

"This is my room," said Kevin.

"You're the other groom," said the bridesmaid. "You're Kevin."

"How does everyone know my name?"

"Your bride is looking for you," said 412, "she's right there," and pointed, and the bride in the enormous dress raised her empty flute and said, "Kevin!" with joy, and Kevin said, "Deborah, I can explain the ring," and Deborah said, "Why are you explaining a ring, we lost our rings hours ago, we've been using a curtain ring," and Douglas said, "THAT is not a curtain ring, that is a two-carat—" and lunged, and Kevin, startled, flung the ring reflexively upward, and it arced, and everyone's head went back in unison to follow it, and it came down and went straight into the champagne flute Deborah was holding, plink, and Deborah looked into her flute and said, "Oh, there's a ring in my drink."

"THAT'S THE RING," said Douglas.

"Give the man his ring, Deborah," said Kevin.

"There's no drink to swallow it with, it's fine," said Deborah, and tipped the flute, and the ring slid toward her mouth, and Margaret dove, and the cart rolled again, and the bellhop said "the cart is a moving vehicle" from the floor, and Margaret caught Deborah's wrist and the flute went sideways and the ring left the flute and this time nobody saw where it went, because at that exact moment door 408 opened and out came a bride—a third bride, a bride nobody had budgeted for—holding a bouquet and blinking.

"Is this the wedding?" said the third bride.

"WHICH wedding," said everyone.

"The midnight one. On the roof. I'm Susan."

"You're on the wrong floor, Susan," said the bellhop, still down, "the roof access is by the ice machine."

"Then what floor is this?"

"This is the fourth floor," said the bellhop, "and everyone's wedding is on the roof, all three, they double-booked the roof, that's why there's so many of you."

There was a silence in the corridor, the first one, holy.

"All three weddings are on the roof," said Douglas slowly.

"At midnight," said the bellhop.

"Then where is the ring," said Margaret, and looked down, and the ring was sitting neatly in the cuff of the bellhop's rolled-up sleeve where it had landed when he raised his arm to say the cart was a moving vehicle, and it winked up at all of them, and the bellhop said, "Oh. There it is," and handed it up to Douglas, who took it, and held it, and pressed it to his chest, and began to laugh.

And then Patrick laughed, and Deborah laughed, and the bridesmaid with the two shoes laughed, and Susan the extra bride laughed, and the woman in the mud mask at 414 shouted "I HATE ALL OF YOU" and that made everyone laugh harder, and Kevin helped the bellhop up, and the towels lay everywhere like snow, and the club sandwich adorned the wall, and Margaret climbed off the cart and gave it a shove and it rolled the whole length of the hall and gently, apologetically, bumped door 406 shut.

"To the roof," said Douglas, ring aloft. "All of us. Everyone marry everyone. There's no time."

And they went, laughing, up the stairs by the ice machine, in a herd of white, leaving the corridor to its identical doors, all of them, at last, agreeably closed.
