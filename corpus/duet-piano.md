You will want to know about the piano, of course, everyone always does, for a piano in the earth is precisely the sort of thing that lodges in the mind like a fishbone, and I shall tell you, if my sister will only permit me to arrange the events in their proper sequence, that it began — the whole magnificent catastrophe of it began — on the wettest evening of that autumn, an evening so drenched and glistening that the very hedges seemed varnished, when our father, God keep his enormous and impossible soul, stood in the doorway and announced —

It didn't rain.

I beg your pardon.

It didn't rain that night, Cecil, and it wasn't autumn, it was March, and you've been telling people about that varnished hedge for forty years and there was no varnished hedge because the hedge was a fence. We had a fence. Wooden. Father put it up himself and did it badly.

She does this. You will notice, over the course of whatever I am able to salvage of this account, that my sister possesses a temperament calibrated exclusively toward demolition; she cannot bear a thing to stand a moment longer than its bare utility requires, and so the hedge — which I maintain existed, which I remember with the tactile vividness of a man who scratched his hand upon it — the hedge must be pulled down and made a fence, the rain must be stopped, the season reversed, all in the name of what she calls accuracy and I call the systematic starving of a story.

Fine. The story. Father owed money. There it is. That's the whole of it before Cecil dresses it up.

That is not the whole of it. That is the skeleton with all the meat cut away and the marrow sucked out and the bone thrown to a dog. Father did not merely owe money, he owed money the way a cathedral owes the sky — as a matter of scale, of architecture, of ambition monstrously overreaching itself. He had borrowed against the house and borrowed against the borrowing, and the piano, you see, the piano was the last thing of value that anyone could point to and name a figure over, and there were men — there were men in coats, Margaret, you cannot dispute the coats —

The coats I'll give you.

— men in coats who were going to come and take it. And Father, who loved that piano more than he loved the roof, more I sometimes think than he loved us —

Oh, here we go.

— Father decided that if he could not keep the piano, then no man in a coat should sit at it either, and that is why, my dear reader, that is the beating heart of the reason, we took it into the garden.

We didn't take it. You make it sound like we carried it out on a cushion. It weighed as much as a horse and Father was fifty-eight and had a bad back and I was nine.

You were eleven.

I was nine. You were fourteen and you did nothing.

I organised.

He stood at the top of the garden and pointed.

I supervised the descent of the instrument, which is a form of labour your generation has never understood, being the intellectual and coordinative labour, the labour of the mind, and I will have you know that pointing, when done with authority, is worth ten men with ropes. And there were ropes. I want that in the record. There were ropes and there was the wheelbarrow which collapsed —

The wheelbarrow was true.

Thank you.

The wheelbarrow collapsed under the leg and Father sat down in the mud and cried and then laughed, and that was the only part of the whole night that I still think about, if you want the truth. The rest of it was just digging.

She reduces it, you understand. "Just digging." As though the hole were nothing. That hole, reader, was an undertaking of near-geological proportion; we opened the earth as one opens a great dark book, and the smell of it came up at us, the smell of everything that had ever rotted quietly beneath a respectable English lawn, and we dug for what felt to my young imagination like the better part of a week —

Two nights.

— for the better part of two nights, then, if she insists on the mean and clockmaker's version, and the neighbours' lights went out one by one, and there was a moment, a single silvered moment when the moon —

There was no moon, you said it rained.

I said it rained on the first evening. The burial was later. The sky may perfectly well have cleared. You cannot have it both ways, sister — you cannot pin me for a moon on a rainy night and then deny me the rain in the first place. Choose your weapon.

I'm saying you don't remember. I'm saying neither of us remembers and you've decided to remember beautifully and I've decided to remember at all.

That is the difference between us in a single sentence, and I could not have phrased it better myself, and I resent that you phrased it, because it was mine to phrase.

Here's what happened. Father owed money. The piano was worth something. He couldn't stand to sell it and he couldn't stand to lose it to the men so he decided nobody would have it, which was, when you say it plainly, an act of pure spite dressed up as feeling, and he made us help him bury it over two nights in the back garden, and Mother watched from the kitchen window and never said one word, not one, and that silence was the loudest thing in the house for months afterward.

She's right about Mother.

I'm right about most of it.

She is right about Mother, and I concede it without embellishment, which for me is the highest form of grief I can offer. Mother at the window. The lamp behind her. Her not coming out. I have spent portions of forty years constructing hedges and moons precisely so that I would not have to arrive at Mother at the window, and my sister, who builds nothing, walks straight up to it every time because she has no scaffolding to slow her down.

The men came the next week and there was nothing to take. Father told them it had been sold. He had no receipt. They looked in the house. They didn't think to dig.

Nobody thinks to dig. That is the whole secret of the earth. It keeps what you give it and asks no questions and grows a lawn over the top and children play upon the very spot and never feel the great cold chord waiting under their feet.

It rotted, Cecil. Whatever you think is down there, it's not a piano anymore. It's a shape in the soil. Strings, maybe. The iron frame. The wood went years ago.

I know what it is. I have always known what it is. I simply prefer to speak of it as it was on the night it went down, whole and gleaming and briefly, gloriously ours in the way a thing is only ours at the moment we are burying it.

We never dug it up. People ask that too. Why didn't you dig it up when things got better. Things didn't get better, that's why. Father died still owing. We sold the house with the piano in the garden and never told the buyers and I've felt bad about it exactly once, at his funeral, for about a minute.

And I have felt bad about it, reader, continuously and elaborately, every single day, which is either the more moral position or merely the more decorative one, and after forty years of arguing with this woman I have finally stopped being certain which, and that, I think, if you will let an old man have the last word for once —

I won't.

— is precisely as it should be.
