Dear Marguerite,

I have started this letter eleven times, and each time I have stopped at your name, because your name is the easiest part, and everything after it is the confession. You will notice I have not written *my dear sister* the way our mother used to at the top of her letters, in that looping hand that took up half a page. I do not think I have earned the *my* anymore. Seven years is a long time to hold a thing at arm's length, and I have held you there, and myself there too, and I am tired of the distance I built.

So I will simply tell you. I have to tell you now, before I lose the nerve I have been storing up like coins, because if I keep it any longer it will calcify into the kind of silence that outlives its keepers, and I refuse to be buried with this. You deserve better than a graveside version of the truth, whispered by someone else, softened by the fact that I am no longer here to be angry at.

It was me who took the letters.

I can feel you frowning already. *What letters,* you're thinking, and I do not blame you, because I have spent seven years making sure you would never think of them at all. I mean the letters from Papa. The ones that came the spring after he left, the ones you waited for on the front step, the ones that never came. I told you they never came. I stood at the door with my hands empty and told you, again and again, that the post had brought nothing, that he had simply gone quiet the way he went quiet, and I watched your face fold up a little more each week, and I let it.

They came, Marguerite. There were four of them. They were addressed to you. I took them from the box before you woke, and I read them, and then I kept them, and one of them I burned in the kitchen sink and washed the ash down with the breakfast dishes so that even the smell would be gone by the time you came downstairs.

I need you to understand that I am not writing this to be forgiven. I want to be very careful here, because I know how confession can dress itself up as a gift when it is really only a way of setting down a weight, and I do not want to hand you my weight and call it a present. If you read this and feel nothing but the old cold anger, that is yours to feel, and it is right, and I will not argue with it.

But I will tell you why, because I think you are owed the why even if you are owed nothing else.

You were fourteen. You worshipped him. You had his laugh and his terrible patience for card games and his way of looking at a room as though it might improve if he stared at it long enough. And I was seventeen, and I had watched him take everything soft in our mother and press it flat, and I had watched her stop singing in the mornings, and I had decided, with all the terrible certainty of a girl who thinks she is the only adult in the house, that he did not get to have you too. That was the shape of it in my mind. He had taken enough. I would not let him take the one clean love you had left.

The letters were kind, Marguerite. That is the part I have never been able to say out loud, the part that has sat in me like a stone in a shoe for seven years. They were not the letters of a man making excuses. He asked after your drawings. He remembered that you liked the color of the sky before a storm and not after. He wrote that he was sorry, in plain words, and that he wanted to come for you in the summer, and that he understood if you did not want to see him but that he would keep writing until you told him to stop.

And I made you the one who told him to stop. Without a single word from you, I told him for you. When the fourth letter went unanswered — because I had answered none of them — he stopped. And you grew up believing your father had abandoned you a second time, when in fact it was your sister who closed the door and told you the wind had blown it shut.

He died in March. I don't know if the news reached you where you are. I found out from a cousin, in the offhand way one finds out these things now, a message among other messages, and I sat in my kitchen for a long time afterward and understood that the last chance to make any of this right had passed while I was making tea. That is when I began to write to you. Not out of guilt only. Out of something I have no cleaner name for than the simple wish that you should not go through your life not knowing you were loved by him. You were. He said so, four times, and I let you believe the opposite because I thought I was protecting you, and because — and this is the truer, uglier thing — because I was jealous of the tenderness he still had for you when he had spent all of his on me long ago and I had watched it curdle. I did not want you to have what I had lost. I dressed that up as protection. It was not protection. It was theft, and I was the thief.

I have the three remaining letters. I have kept them in a tin, the blue one with the dented lid that you will remember, and they are yours, they have always been yours, and I will send them the moment you tell me you want them, or I will bring them to your door and leave them and go, or I will do whatever you ask, including nothing. I mean that. If your answer is silence I will accept your silence the way you should have been given the choice to accept his letters. It is the least symmetry I owe you.

I do not expect this to reach across seven years and undo them. I know that a wound tended late is not the same as a wound tended in time. But I could not carry it into my own quiet without at least handing you the truth of it, so that if you ever wondered — and I think you must have wondered — why he stopped, you would know it was never you. It was never anything you did or failed to do. It was your sister, standing at a door, lying with an empty hand.

I love you. I have no right to end on those words, but they are also true, and I am done keeping true things from you.

Write to me if you can. Don't, if you can't.

Either way, the tin is yours.

Élise
