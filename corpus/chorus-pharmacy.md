We had watched the corner pharmacy die slowly, the way we watched most things on our street go, which is to say sideways and with a kind of patient disbelief, first the pharmacist's wife stopped sweeping the step, then the neon mortar-and-pestle in the window went half-dark so it read only ORT, then the papered-over glass, then the padlock that rusted through two winters until it wore a little beard of orange, and every year we told ourselves someone would take it, and every year no one did, and we grew fond of the emptiness the way you grow fond of a missing tooth, running our tongues over it out of habit.

So when the truck came in June we came out onto our stoops to see.

"Furniture," said Mrs. Ojeda, who read a truck the way other people read tea leaves.

"Bar," said the Petersen boy, hopeful, always hopeful.

"Church," said someone, and we laughed, because you never can tell.

They were a family, we saw that much — a mother with paint already in her hair on the first day, as if she'd come pre-committed, and a father who carried things wrong, gripping the middle of long boards so they seesawed, and two children who were immediately absorbed into our children the way a drop of dye is absorbed into a glass of water, no seam, no ceremony, and a grandmother who set a folding chair on the sidewalk and supervised the entire summer from it without once, that we saw, offering an opinion aloud.

We took to calling it the project, and then we took to calling it ours.

Because here is what a street does when a shuttered thing begins to open: it slows down passing it. We slowed. We found reasons to be out with the recycling at the hour the dumpster filled, and we peered, and we made our inventories, and the inventories were glorious — out came the old apothecary drawers with their tiny brass handles and their labels in a language of abbreviation none of us could read, and out came the ceiling tiles stained the color of weak tea, and out came a scale so ornate it looked like it had weighed the souls of the neighborhood for a century, and out came linoleum in great curling scrolls like something being unmade, and out came a smell, released all at once on the third or fourth day, a smell of menthol and dust and old sugar that stopped Mr. Achterberg on the sidewalk and made him say, without meaning to, that his mother used to send him here for cough drops, and we all had a version of that, we discovered, we all had been sent here as children for something, and so the gutting was also a kind of exhumation, and we stood at the lip of it and looked down into our own small histories being carried out to the curb.

"They're taking out the good drawers," Mrs. Ojeda mourned.

"They're keeping the drawers," her husband corrected. "Look, they stacked them under the tarp."

We were relieved about the drawers. We didn't know yet why we cared.

All summer the guessing was the sweetest part, sweeter maybe than the answer, and we don't mind admitting it, because a street that is guessing is a street that is talking to itself, leaning over its fences, and we hadn't done that in a while. Every evening the theories came home from work with us. It would be a coffee place, obviously, with the exposed brick they were so carefully cleaning, brick by brick, the mother on a ladder with a wire brush and a look of ferocious happiness. It would be one of those stores that sold nothing you needed. It would be a restaurant, because a dishwasher went in, we saw it go in, on its back on a hand truck like a patient. It would fail, said the pessimists among us, who are always with us and whom we love, in six months it would fail, this street couldn't hold a thing like that. It would be beautiful and doomed, we agreed, and we half hoped for that, because beautiful and doomed is a story we know how to tell.

They painted the front a green we didn't have a name for.

"Sage," said the Petersen boy's mother.

"Money," said Mr. Achterberg, meaning it would cost too much, whatever it was.

We watched the children of the new family teach our children to skateboard down the disabled ramp they'd poured fresh, and we watched the grandmother in her folding chair reach out one afternoon and steady a wobbling six-year-old without appearing to move at all, and we understood then that they meant to stay, that this was not a flip, not a scheme, that the slowness of their working was the slowness of people who intended to be old here, and something in us unclenched that we hadn't known was clenched.

Then came the specific day, the one we still date things from.

It was the second Saturday in August, hot in the flat exhausted way of late summer, and they took the paper off the windows all at once, and we came out — we did not pretend to be doing anything else, we simply came out — and we stood on the far curb in a loose crescent and read the sign, which was hand-painted, gold on the green, and it did not say COFFEE and it did not say BAR and it did not say any of the things we had bet on.

It was a library. A lending library, free, with the old apothecary drawers set into the wall behind the counter, each little brass-handled drawer now holding index cards, and the great soul-weighing scale polished and standing in the window with a stack of books on one pan, and the brick clean and glowing, and shelves, floor to that tea-stained ceiling they had kept and only wiped, shelves already half full because — we learned this slowly, over the following weeks, the way you learn the good things — they had been asking, all summer, every neighbor who lingered, what did you used to come here for, and then, what do you read now, and writing it down.

"A library," said Mrs. Ojeda, and for once she had predicted nothing and did not seem to mind.

"Who does that," said Mr. Achterberg, and it was not a question, it was a kind of praise.

Nobody does that. That was the whole point. We had guessed at every profitable thing and none of us had guessed at the thing that gave itself away, and we were ashamed of that for about the length of one breath, and then we weren't, because they had left the door propped open with the old iron doorstop shaped like a dog, and the smell that came out now was menthol and dust and paper, the old smell and a new smell braided together, and the grandmother had folded up her chair at last and carried it inside and set it by the window in the light.

We went in. Of course we went in. We had been standing outside that corner our whole lives, running our tongues over the gap, and now there was somewhere to stand inside it. We took books we did not need. We brought books we could not part with and parted with them anyway, sliding them onto the shelves between strangers, and every year now we do this, we bring and we take and we linger, and the pessimists come too, defeated and glad, and we still call it the project, and we still call it ours, though it was never ours, though it was only ever theirs, given to us open-handed, brick by patient brick, all one long green summer we thought we were only watching.
