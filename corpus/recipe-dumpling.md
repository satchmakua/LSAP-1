1. Soak the dried mushrooms.

Do this the night before, in a bowl too large for the amount of water you will use, because the bowl matters, and I will explain the bowl later, or my sister will, if she is here, which she will not be, having declared thirteen years ago at the funeral that she was finished with dumplings and everyone attached to them, a category into which she had by then folded most of the living relatives and at least two of the dead. The mushrooms need the whole night. This is not negotiable, though everything else in this recipe has been negotiated, litigated, wept over, and once — memorably — thrown, in its entirety, filling and all, off the third-floor balcony onto the awning of the tailor below, who returned it in a covered dish the following morning with a note that read, in careful characters, *I believe this belongs to your family,* which it did, and which we ate anyway, because Auntie Bo said a fall of one story per generation was, if anything, an improvement.

2. Mince the mushrooms fine.

Finer. Auntie Second was the one who insisted on this, and Auntie Second was wrong about many things — she believed televisions could overhear you, she believed her husband had been a handsome man, she believed the number eight was watching her — but she was right about the mushrooms, which must be minced until they surrender their little dark selves entirely into the pork, so that no bite announces *here is mushroom* but every bite is haunted by the having-been of it. She minced with a cleaver older than the building. She wore a cardigan the color of weak tea in all seasons. When she died we found forty-one identical cardigans folded in a trunk, and I have one still, and I wear it when I make these, and it smells of nothing now, which is its own kind of grief.

3. Add ginger, scallion, one egg white.

4. Stir in one direction only.

This is the part where the argument lives. My grandmother stirred clockwise because that is how her mother stirred, and her mother stirred clockwise because — nobody knew, the reason had fallen out of the recipe somewhere in the migration, the way a button falls off a coat in a crowd and you feel the small cold spot for years — but Auntie Bo, who came into the family by marriage and therefore believed she could see the whole thing more clearly than those born inside it, argued that clockwise was mere superstition and that the meat did not care which way it was flogged. So Auntie Bo stirred counterclockwise, once, at a New Year when my grandmother was in the hospital and could not enforce the tradition, and the dumplings that year were, by universal and slightly frightened agreement, exactly the same. This settled nothing. It made everything worse. My grandmother, recovering, was told, and she said: *Of course they tasted the same. She stirred with spite. Spite is very binding.* And she stirred clockwise for the rest of her life, and so do I, and so, I have noticed, does Auntie Bo, now, when she thinks no one is watching, her old wrist going round and round, patient, apostate, defeated, at peace.

5. Make the dough. Flour, water, patience.

The patience is the third ingredient and you cannot buy it and you cannot rush it and it goes off if you are angry. Rest the dough covered with a damp cloth for thirty minutes. Rest yourself in the same interval. My grandmother used this half hour to conduct her feuds by telephone, holding the receiver with her shoulder, walking the length of the cord, which was long, she had specifically requested a long cord so that she could pace while she forgave people slowly and unforgave them faster. The dough listened to all of it. I am half convinced the dough is why the dumplings taste of our family in particular — that it has absorbed, over three generations, every grievance aired within earshot of its rising, so that to eat one is to eat a small edible archive of who insulted whom at which wedding.

6. Roll thin rounds. Thick at nothing, thin at nothing, even everywhere.

That is my grandmother's phrasing and I have never improved on it and I have never fully understood it. Even everywhere. She would take the wrapper I had rolled, hold it to the window, and if light came through unevenly she would hand it back without a word, and the not-word was the whole lesson. She taught the way a river teaches a stone: by continuous unhurried unbearable friction, until one day you are the right shape and you cannot remember being any other.

7. Fill.

A spoonful, no more. Here is the sin of the young: overfilling. You want abundance, you want the dumpling stuffed to bursting because you love the people you are feeding and love wishes to be enormous. Resist. A dumpling that bursts in the pot has failed everyone at the table and shamed the cook and, worse, allowed the broth to go cloudy, and a cloudy broth at the festival is a thing the aunts will discuss for a calendar year. Love, in this cuisine, is restraint. This took me until I was forty to learn and I learned it from Auntie Second's cardigan, which is to say I learned it from a smell that was no longer there, which is to say I learned it too late for her to see, which is the general condition of learning anything worth knowing.

8. Pleat. Seven folds.

Or nine. My grandmother said seven, Auntie Bo said nine, Auntie Second folded a chaotic and beautiful number that changed with her mood and her arthritis and whether the number eight was watching, and my sister, before she quit us all, folded three enormous pleats out of pure provocation and dared anyone to say the dumplings were worse for it, and no one could, and that was the trouble with my sister, she was so often not worse and it enraged everyone. Fold as many times as it takes to close it. Close it completely. An open dumpling drowns.

9. Boil. When they rise, add cold water. When they rise again, add cold water. When they rise a third time, they are done.

Three risings. Three generations. I did not arrange this rhyme; the dumpling arranged it, or the aunts did, retroactively, the way we all go back into our stories and tidy them into meaning after the fact. My grandmother rising in her mother's kitchen. My mother — I have not mentioned my mother; my mother is the second rising, the quiet one, the one who kept the recipe alive simply by cooking it every year and never once claiming she had improved it, which was its own radical act in a family of improvers. And me, the third rising, standing here in a dead woman's tea-colored cardigan, stirring clockwise out of love and habit and the fear of being wrong, adding cold water to calm the water down, three times, the way you calm anything down, patiently, again, again.

10. Serve at once. Say nothing about the recipe. Let them argue.
