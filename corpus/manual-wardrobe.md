1. Remove all components from packaging and lay them on a flat surface. Confirm the following: two side panels (A), one base panel (B), one top panel (C), one rear panel (D), four dowels, sixteen cam locks, one bag of assorted screws.

2. Do not discard packaging until assembly is complete.

3. Attach side panel A to base panel B using two dowels. Insert dowels into the pre-drilled holes marked with the corresponding letter. Apply firm, even pressure until flush.

4. Repeat step 3 with the second side panel. The base should now stand upright without support. If it does not stand without support, check that dowels are fully seated and that the base has not been inverted.

5. Secure each joint with a cam lock. Insert the metal disc into the circular recess and rotate clockwise one half-turn using the provided key. You will feel resistance, then release. That release is the mechanism engaging. Listen for it.

6. Attach the top panel C. The frame is now rigid. Test it by pressing gently at the corners. There should be no movement. If there is movement, you have missed a lock, and you will need to go back through the joints one at a time, pressing your palm to each until you find the one that gives.

7. Slide the rear panel D into the grooves along the inner edge. It will only fit one way. Do not force it. If it resists, turn it around and try the other orientation, and if it still resists, set it down and step back and look at the whole assembly for a moment before you continue, because sometimes the problem is not the panel but something you did four steps ago that you have not yet noticed.

8. Fix the rear panel with the small nails provided, spacing them evenly. Even spacing is not strictly necessary for structural integrity, but you will know, later, whether you did it well, and you will be the only one who knows.

9. Fit the door. Align the hinges with the marked positions. Adjust the tension screws until the door hangs level and closes without catching. This may take longer than you expect. Doors rarely hang level on the first attempt; they swing a little wide, or drag along the base, and you find yourself opening and closing them, opening and closing, adjusting a quarter-turn each time, until the afternoon has gone and the light in the room has changed and you did not notice it change.

10. The wardrobe is now assembled. Position it against the wall of your choosing. It is best placed against the longest unbroken wall, away from the window, so that the morning does not fade the finish. Consider where the light falls before you commit. Consider that once it is standing, filled, it will not easily be moved again, and that the wall you choose is likely the wall it will stand against for as long as you live in this room.

11. Level the wardrobe using the adjustable feet. Turn each foot until the unit no longer rocks. A wardrobe that rocks will, over months, work its own joints loose, and you will hear it settle at night — a small tick from the corner of the room — and you will tell yourself it is nothing, the way you tell yourself many things are nothing at the hour when the house has gone quiet and every small sound arrives without competition.

12. Once level, secure the wardrobe to the wall using the anti-tip bracket. This is the most important step and the one most often skipped. It is skipped because it requires drilling into the wall, into a fixed and permanent thing, and there is a reluctance in most people to make a permanent mark, to admit that the wardrobe is staying, that you are staying, that this room with its particular corner and its particular slant of light is now, whether you decided it or not, the place where your clothes hang and your mornings begin.

13. Drill the bracket into a stud where possible. Feel along the wall with your knuckle until the sound goes solid. The house was built by people you will never meet, who left their studs and their wiring behind the plaster like a language you must knock against to read, and you are only the latest to press your ear to it and listen for what holds.

14. Fill the wardrobe. Begin with the things you wear most and work inward toward the things you keep but never use — the coat for the wedding you may not be invited to again, the shirt that no longer fits but that you cannot give away because someone once told you it suited you and you have not been told that since. Arrange these however you like. There is no correct order. There is only the order you will come to expect, so that your hand reaches without your eyes, in the half-dark, for the sleeve it knows.

15. Close the door. Notice, now, the room around the wardrobe. Notice that it, too, was assembled, once, panel by panel, by hands that measured and cut and hoped it would hold, and that you moved into it the way you move into most things, provisionally, meaning to stay only a while, and that the while has become the shape of your days.

16. Check the room for level, if you can. You cannot, of course; there is no key that turns the room a half-turn until it stops rocking. But you know where it tilts. You know which floorboard announces you, which door will not stay shut, which window admits the cold along its lower edge no matter how you seal it, and you have stopped trying to seal it, and this stopping is a kind of settling too, and you should not be ashamed of it.

17. Consider the house around the room, the street around the house, the ways you came to be standing here holding a small metal key and a leftover screw you cannot place, a screw that came with the wardrobe and belongs to nothing you have built, and that you will put in a drawer, because you cannot throw away a thing that might yet be needed, and you have a whole drawer of such things, and perhaps that drawer is the truest inventory of your life — not the clothes hung neatly behind the door, but the loose parts you kept in case.

18. Sit down on the floor if you are tired. You are allowed to be tired. It has taken longer than the box suggested; it always takes longer than the box suggests, and the estimate on the box was made by someone who has never assembled anything in a real room, with real interruptions, with the phone ringing and the light going and the particular weight of a particular afternoon pressing on the back of your neck.

19. Look at the thing you have made. It stands. It holds. It is, against the odds and the instructions and your own uncertain hands, level and secure and fixed to the wall of the room of the house where you live, and you did that, and no one will praise you for it, and that is all right, because the standing of it is its own quiet answer, and you can hear it now in the settled silence — not a tick, not a loose joint, but the sound of something staying up.

20. Retain these instructions for future reference. You may need them again. You will move, one day, or you will not, and either way there will come a morning when you stand before this same door in the changed light and try to remember how you ever got it all to hold.
