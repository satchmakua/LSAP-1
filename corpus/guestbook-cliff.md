*3rd April 1961.* First guests of the season, and I daresay the coldest. Room 4 recommended by Mrs Prowse, who keeps this book. Wind like a knife off the water but the tea hot. We shall come again.
— H. & E. Cartwright

*17th September 1961.* Room 4 is a coffin with a view. Take Room 2, over the porch, where the chimney breast keeps you warm and you needn't hear the sea gnawing all night as though it meant to have you.
— R. Salter, commercial traveller

*Whitsun, 1962.* Salter is wrong. One comes to a place like this precisely to hear the sea gnaw. A man who wants a warm chimney should stop in Birmingham.
— A. Cartwright (son of the above H. & E.)

*3rd August 1963.* We are honeymooners and took whichever room was going, which was 5, at the back, looking at the hill. We saw no sea at all and were entirely happy. I record this only so that some future reader knows the sea is not the whole of it.
— Mr & Mrs J. Ferris

*3rd August 1963, later.* Bless the Ferrises. Mrs Prowse gave us their leftover cake.
— the two Miss Everards, Room 1

*June 1964.* Rain seven days of seven. Room 2 as advertised by Salter: warm, snug, and blind to everything worth seeing. I looked out at grey and heard nothing and might as well have been dead. Give me the coffin.
— R. Salter (yes, the same; a man may change his mind)

*11th October 1966.* A gale took three slates off the roof over Room 4 and Mrs Prowse moved us to 3. Room 3 is the secret of this house. It faces the little cove and at low tide you can watch the seals. No one writes of Room 3 because those who find it keep quiet.
— D. Okafor, schoolmaster, Lagos and Penzance

*Easter 1968.* We have taken Room 3 on the strength of Mr Okafor's entry and found the seals exactly where he promised, seven of them, fat as bishops. We are converts. Room 3 for evermore.
— the Hollands

*2nd September 1969.* Mrs Prowse buried this June. Her niece keeps the house now and keeps this book, and asks that guests be gentle in their opinions, as she is learning. I write only to say Room 4 is mine as long as I draw breath, slates or no slates.
— A. Cartwright

*2nd September 1969.* And the tea is still hot. That is the important thing and the girl has got it right.
— A. Cartwright (a postscript)

*July 1971.* Brought the children. Room 5, the honeymoon room of the Ferrises, holds a family of four if two of them are small. No sea, as promised, but the hill has rabbits and that is a better bargain at their age than any amount of drowning water.
— The Ferrises again, now with Tom and Susan

*July 1971.* Susan wishis to say she saw a rabbit. She wrote this herself.
— S. Ferris, age 6

*May 1973.* I am old enough now to disagree with my late parents. Room 4 is not a coffin. Room 4 is the prow of a ship. My father, who I see has scrawled his loyalty up and down these pages like a boy carving a tree, had it right for once. The sea does gnaw. Let it. Something ought to.
— A. Cartwright — no; I am the son, and my father is the *A.* above. I sign myself Arthur to end the confusion he began.

*October 1975.* Weather foul. Room 3, seals absent, tide wrong all week. Do not trust a house by its best day.
— G. Trewin

*October 1975.* Do not trust a man who comes in October and blames the sea for being the sea.
— Arthur Cartwright

*Midsummer 1978.* We are the new people. We have bought the house from Mrs Prowse's niece, who is off to marry in Canada, and we mean to keep this book going as she kept it, because a stranger showed us these pages before we signed and we understood at once that we were buying a conversation and not merely a roof. We shall not take sides on the rooms. We shall only say the chimney in Room 2 still draws, the slates over Room 4 are mended at last, and the seals in the cove below Room 3 numbered nine this morning.
— Ben and Iris Quick, proprietors

*4th August 1979.* Sixteen years to the day since our wedding and Room 5 was free. The rabbits are gone — a fox, Mr Quick thinks — but the hill is the same green nothing it always was and we would not swap it. Tom is at university. Susan is engaged. We came back to see whether the place had shrunk, as places do, and it had not.
— The Ferrises

*April 1982.* My father is dead. Alfred Cartwright, whose hand is all over the early pages, the man who called Room 4 a prow. I have taken Room 4 and sat in it three nights and I understand him now, which is a thing sons only manage when it is too late to say so. The sea gnaws. He was not being poetic; he was being accurate. I leave this here so he is not forgotten by the house he loved better than he could say to any of us.
— Arthur Cartwright

*April 1982.* We remember him. — B. & I. Quick

*September 1984.* Room 3, seals, sun, the tide obliging for once. Whoever G. Trewin was, coming in the wrong month and grumbling, he did the rest of us a service, for it is the grumblers who teach a place its patience.
— D. Okafor, returned at last, older, the same schoolmaster

*July 1990.* I am Susan Ferris that was, now Susan Lyle, and I have brought my own daughter to Room 5 and shown her the entry I made when I was six, about the rabbit. She did not believe a rabbit could matter so much to anyone. She is nine. She will learn.
— S. Lyle

*July 1990.* i saw NO rabbit but i saw a fox. its better.
— Ellie Lyle, age 9

*October 1996.* Iris died in the spring. I keep the book alone now. I have moved myself into Room 2, Salter's warm blind room, because at seventy a man wants the chimney more than he wants the view, and I find Salter was right the first time and right the second time too, which is the trick of long life — everything is true if you wait.
— Ben Quick

*August 2003.* We found this book in a drawer, dusty, the house shut up and for sale, and the agent let us read it while we waited. Strangers, all of you, and yet we have taken sides already — Room 4, without question, whatever the coffin-people say. We are buying the house. We promise to keep the book.
— The Amenta family

*August 2003.* Bless you. Keep the tea hot. That was always the one rule.
— Ben Quick, who came back one last afternoon to sign off, being too old now to stay the night

*June 2011.* Room 4. The sea still gnaws. Someone wrote that decades ago and I have read every page waiting for the tide and I say to all of them, the Cartwrights and Salters and Ferrises and Quicks, wherever you have gone: it gnaws yet, and we hear it, and we are not afraid.
— A. Cartwright — great-granddaughter of the Alfred who started it, come to find her name already in the house before her.
