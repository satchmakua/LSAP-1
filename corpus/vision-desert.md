The wanderer came down out of the last hard light of afternoon into a country the color of old bone, and there she stopped, because the ground itself seemed to be turning toward something. All day she had walked without direction, or so she had told herself, following only the pressure of her own thirst and the slow revolution of the sun. But now, as the day began to fold inward and the heat lifted off the sand in long trembling sheets, she understood that she had not been wandering at all. She had been carried. Some current beneath the visible had been drawing her east and then south and then east again, and she had mistaken its patience for aimlessness. The desert had been leading her the way a river leads a leaf, and she had thought herself free.

She knelt without deciding to kneel. Below her the land dropped away into a shallow basin, a dry lake bed cracked into a mosaic of pale plates, and beyond it the horizon burned. The sun had begun its descent into that far edge, and the whole sky above the basin was catching fire in slow, deliberate stages, as though light were a thing that ripened rather than merely fell. First the low gold, thick as honey along the rim of the world. Then, higher, a rose that deepened as she watched into something that had no name she knew, a color that seemed to be the memory of every warmth she had ever been given and forgotten. She felt her breath go out of her and not come back for a long moment, and in that emptied space something entered that was not breath.

She had been thirsty for three days. Her lips were split and her tongue lay in her mouth like a stone, and her feet were a country of small ruined nations. None of this mattered now. The suffering had not vanished; rather it had been taken up into a larger thing and given a place, the way a single held note is taken up into a chord and made to mean what alone it could never mean. She understood, kneeling there, that her thirst had been necessary. That without it she would have walked past this basin as she had walked past a thousand ordinary sunsets in her life before the desert, glancing and moving on, protected by her comfort from the terrible openness of the world. The thirst had scoured her. It had made of her a hollow reed, and now the wind of the evening moved through her and she rang.

The light thickened. Across the basin the cracked plates of the lake bed began to glow, each one holding the fire in its upturned face, so that the whole dead lake seemed to remember that it had once been water and was water still, water made of light, a lake of standing flame lying perfectly still under the sky. And she saw then that nothing in this place was dead. She had walked for days believing herself in a land of absence, of erasure, of the great indifferent nothing that the desert wears like a mask. But the mask was falling away in the failing light, and behind it was not nothing. Behind it was attention. The desert was watching her. Not with eyes, not with anything so small as eyes, but with the whole weight of its silence bent toward the single point where she knelt, as though her arrival had been awaited since before the mountains wore down into this sand.

She wept, though her body had no water left to spend, and the weeping was dry and shaking and did not diminish her but enlarged her. She understood that she was being addressed. Not in words. Words were too particular, too small, snagged as they were on the hooks of meaning; this was addressed to her beneath the level where words could reach, spoken directly into the marrow, into the old animal knowledge that lives below the mind. And what it said, insofar as it could be said at all, was this: that she was seen, that she had always been seen, that not one of her days had fallen unwitnessed into the dark. That the long apparent randomness of her life, its detours and its griefs, its loves that failed and its labors that came to nothing, had been not the absence of a pattern but a pattern too vast for her to have stood inside and seen. She had been living inside a design the way a single thread lives inside a tapestry, seeing only the tangle of its own small passage, blind to the figure it helped to make. And now, for one dilating moment, she was lifted out and shown the whole.

She could not have described the figure. That was the mercy of it and the terror of it both. But she felt its rightness the way one feels the rightness of a key turning in a lock long thought jammed forever. Everything belonged. The child she had been belonged, and the woman she had become, and this kneeling body with its ruined feet, all of it belonged, and even her wandering belonged, even the days she had cursed as wasted, for they had brought her here, to this rim, at this hour, when the light was doing this. There is a grace that undoes and there is a grace that gathers, and this was the gathering kind. It reached back through her whole life and touched each abandoned thing and said: come, you also, come into the meaning. Nothing would be left behind. Nothing had ever truly been lost.

The sun was half-swallowed now by the burning rim, and the sky above had gone to a violet so deep it seemed to press down upon the crown of her head like a laid-on hand. A single planet had pierced the upper dark, steady and white and unbearably tender. She lifted her face to it. She felt the whole vault of the evening turning slowly above her, the stars beginning their patient emergence one by one, each one a word in a sentence too long for any life to read, and yet the sentence was being spoken, had always been being spoken, and its subject, she now knew, was not indifferent to her at all. The universe was not cold. That had been the great lie of her comfort and her fear. The cold was only the distance she had kept. Come close enough, be scoured enough, kneel low enough, and you would feel it: the immense, deliberate warmth turned toward you, the whole of creation leaning in the direction of a love it was still, even now, in the act of becoming.

The last edge of the sun slipped under, and the fire on the lake bed dimmed to embers and then to ash-grey, and the cool came up out of the ground like a tide. But the wanderer did not feel abandoned by the going of the light. She understood that the light had not shown her a thing that was ending but a thing that was beginning, a door held open a single hand's-breadth so that she might know a door was there. She rose. Her thirst was still her thirst, her feet still their small ruined nations, but she was walking now toward, and not merely away. She could not see the destination. She did not need to. She had felt the whole world tilt toward it, and she would go where the tilting led, downward into the darkening basin, eastward under the emerging stars, forward into the long and deliberate unfolding that had, she now knew past all unknowing, her name folded somewhere inside it, waiting to be spoken aloud.
