You will wake before the alarm, though you will have set one anyway, because you will not trust the years to have taught you calm. The light will be the thin gray of early September, and it will come through the gap in the curtains we still have not replaced, the ones with the small blue anchors on them. You will lie there for a moment and listen. Down the hall, in a room that is now the nursery but will by then be a proper bedroom with a real bed and a lamp shaped like a rocket, your daughter will still be sleeping. She will sleep the way she sleeps now, one arm flung above her head, her mouth slightly open, her breath a small steady tide. You will get up quietly. You will not want to wake her yet. You will want, first, a few minutes in which nothing has begun.

You will go downstairs and the third step will still creak, because we will never have fixed it, and you will smile at the creak the way you smile at an old friend's bad joke. In the kitchen you will fill the kettle. You will stand at the window while it boils and you will watch the street come slowly awake — a jogger, a cat crossing the road with great seriousness, the neighbor's recycling waiting at the curb. You will drink your tea too hot and burn the roof of your mouth, because you will not be able to wait, because your hands will want something to do.

At seven you will go up and open her door and say her name, softly, the way you say it now when she has fallen asleep in the car and you must carry her in. She will stir. She will be five years old and her feet will reach much further down the bed than you will be prepared for. She will rub her eyes with two fists and she will look at you and remember, all at once, what day it is, and she will sit up very straight. "Is it school," she will say, not quite a question, and you will say yes, it is school, and something enormous and quiet will move through the room.

You will dress her in the clothes you laid out the night before, the ones she chose herself weeks ago and then guarded fiercely: a red sweater, dark trousers with an elastic waist, and shoes with a strap she can fasten alone, because she will insist on fastening it alone. You will let her. You will watch her tongue poke out of the corner of her mouth as she concentrates. She will get it on the second try and look up at you triumphant, and you will say, that's it, that's exactly it, and you will mean far more than the shoe.

Breakfast will be toast with the crusts cut off, which we will have promised ourselves we would stop doing and will not have stopped. She will eat only the middle. She will drink her milk in one long draught and set the cup down with a small gasp. You will pack her lunch into a box with her name on it, written in your handwriting, and you will fold the napkin twice the way she likes, and you will slip in, at the last moment, when she is not looking, a note that says nothing important, only that she is loved, in case she needs it at some hour of the day when you cannot be there to say it.

At eight you will help her into her coat and she will stand in the hallway looking impossibly small under the weight of the backpack, which will be nearly as tall as she is and empty of everything except a spare set of clothes and that lunch and a stuffed rabbit she will have decided, at the door, that she cannot possibly leave behind. You will not argue. You will let the rabbit come. You will take a photograph on the front step and she will not smile the way you ask her to; she will smile the other way, the real way, the crooked one, and it will be the best picture you have ever taken.

The walk will take nine minutes. It takes nine minutes now, though there will then be a low fence around the corner house that is not there yet, and the sycamore at the end of our road will be taller and its roots will have lifted the pavement into a little hill she will want to climb. You will hold her hand the whole way and she will chatter, or she will go silent, and you will not know until the morning comes which it will be. You will point out the things you always point out — the letterbox, the dog behind the blue gate, the church clock that is always four minutes fast. She will hold your hand tighter as the school gates come into view. She will not say why. You will not ask.

At the gate there will be other parents and other children, and a teacher in a cardigan who will crouch down to your daughter's height and say good morning with such practiced gentleness that you will love her instantly and forever. Your daughter will hide behind your leg. Then she will peek out. Then, in a moment you will not be able to slow down no matter how you try, she will let go of your hand. She will take one step, and then another, and she will walk toward the door with the rabbit tucked under her arm, and she will not look back.

You will want her to look back. You will stand there wanting it. And then you will understand that her not looking back is the whole point, that it is the thing you have been working toward for five years without knowing it, that a child who does not look back is a child who trusts that the world behind her will hold. So you will not call out. You will let her go through the door. You will see the red of her sweater for one more second and then you will not.

You will walk home the long way. You will do this on purpose. You will pass the coffee shop and you will not go in. You will notice that the morning has become ordinary again around you, that the jogger has finished and the cat has gone wherever cats go, that the world has not paused to acknowledge what has happened, and you will find this both cruel and comforting. You will let yourself into the house and the third step will creak and there will be nobody to hear it but you.

You will pick up the stuffed rabbit's twin from where it fell under the coat rack. You will hold it for a moment. You will put the kettle on again and you will not drink the tea. You will look at the clock, the one that is right, and you will calculate the hours until three, and they will seem both endless and nothing at all.

And at three you will be at the gate early, far too early, standing in the same place. The door will open. You will scan the small bright river of children and you will find the red sweater, and she will find you, and she will run — she will run — and you will kneel down and open your arms and she will crash into them and begin at once to tell you everything, and you will hold her and you will listen, and you will know that you can do this, that you will do this, every single day.
