1. To offer bread is easy; to offer it without a ledger is the whole of hospitality.

2. The host who counts the guests has already lost them.

3. An invitation is a small confession: it names whom we wish to become.

4. Every threshold is an argument about the world, settled without words.

5. The generous man fears only one thing: that his generosity be noticed.

6. To be welcomed is to be forgiven in advance for a fault not yet committed.

7. The table is the one court where the accused presides.

8. He who apologizes for the meal insults the appetite of the guest.

9. Hospitality is the art of pretending abundance where there is only enough.

10. The stranger at the door is a mirror; we entertain our own reflection when we let him in.

11. To arrive uninvited is a sin; to be turned away is a greater one against the host.

12. The perfect guest leaves the room as he found it, and the host altered forever.

13. We open our houses to keep from opening our hearts, and are undone when both give way at once.

14. Wine loosens the tongue that salt has already bound.

15. Bread shared is bread multiplied; bread hoarded is bread already stale.

16. The host commands the meal but obeys the guest; this reversal is the whole of civilization.

17. A closed door is an honest statement; a grudging welcome is a lie in three courses.

18. To feed a man you dislike is the beginning of liking him.

19. The measure of a house is not its rooms but its willingness to shrink them.

20. Every guest carries an invisible gift: the chance to be seen behaving well.

21. The miser sets a fine table and dines alone at it forever.

22. Courtesy is the tax the fortunate pay for the illusion of being loved.

23. He who eats and does not thank has stolen twice, once from the plate and once from the pleasure of giving.

24. The uninvited guest tests the host; the invited one tests the friendship.

25. To feast is to declare, briefly, that death has been postponed.

26. A host who tires of his guests has forgotten that he was once one himself.

27. The threshold belongs to neither party; it is the only democracy the house allows.

28. We remember the room where we were fed and forget the room where we were praised.

29. Hospitality withheld from the poor is hospitality that has understood nothing.

30. The best meal is the one whose recipe no one asks for, because no one thought to leave.

31. To seat a man is to assign him a truth about his worth; the wise host lies upward.

32. The guest who overstays converts a gift into a debt and never repays it.

33. Salt at the table is a promise; to break it is to break something older than the house.

34. He who serves last serves best, for he has watched everyone else be filled.

35. A cold hearth is not the absence of fire but the presence of refusal.

36. The invitation we decline reveals us; the one we accept only decorates us.

37. Politeness is hospitality performed for an audience; kindness is hospitality performed for no one.

38. To pour for another before oneself is to admit that thirst can be shared.

39. The host's true wealth is the willingness to be temporarily poorer.

40. A guest is a verdict rendered on the host's whole life, delivered over dinner.

41. The empty chair at the table is the most crowded seat in the house.

42. He who feeds only his equals has not yet learned to eat.

43. The finest hospitality asks no questions and remembers no answers.

44. To be a good guest is to praise without flattery and to leave without relief.

45. The host who exhausts himself has confused love with sacrifice, and both suffer.

46. Every feast is a rehearsal for the one none of us will attend.

47. The door that opens too eagerly is as suspect as the one that will not open at all.

48. Bread offered in silence says more than the whole grammar of welcome.

49. We do not entertain to be seen; we entertain to see, and lie about which.

50. The guest owes the host a story; the host owes the guest a silence in which to tell it.

51. To refuse food from an enemy is prudence; to accept it is the first act of peace.

52. A crowded table forgives what an empty one cannot conceal.

53. The stranger fed becomes the friend remembered; the friend refused becomes the stranger forever.

54. Hospitality is memory practiced forward, against the day one is oneself the traveler.

55. The host apologizes for nothing; the guest forgives everything; between them the evening is saved.

56. To set a place for a man is to insist, against all evidence, that he was expected.

57. The meal is a sentence; the seating is its grammar; the conversation, its only meaning.

58. He who counts his wine has already drunk the worse of it.

59. A welcome given from fear is worse than a door slammed from courage.

60. The guest's laughter is the host's true payment; all else is receipt.

61. To share a table is to consent, for an hour, to be human in front of witnesses.

62. The invitation is a promise; the arrival, its interest; the departure, its principal returned with thanks.

63. He who feeds the hungry earns nothing; he only stops owing.

64. The host who watches the clock has already told his guests the hour of their exile.

65. A gift brought to the table should be small enough to forget and generous enough to remember.

66. To be welcomed poorly is to learn what wealth cannot buy; to be welcomed richly, to learn what it cannot excuse.

67. The good host makes the guest forget he is a guest; the great host makes him forget he was ever anything else.

68. Every table is a border, and every meal a treaty signed in appetite.

69. He who eats alone by choice is fed; he who eats alone by circumstance is only filled.

70. The threshold crossed in trust is worth more than the room entered in triumph.

71. To offer the last portion is not generosity but faith that there will be more.

72. The host's fatigue is his secret and his gift; the guest who notices it has repaid him.

73. A house without guests is a museum of intentions.

74. Welcome is the only currency that grows by being spent.

75. To decline hospitality is sometimes the kindest gift a guest can give a weary host.

76. The meal ends; the debt of having been fed does not.

77. He who insists on repayment has served a bill, not a supper.

78. The guest brings the outside in; the host makes the inside larger to hold it.

79. Bread eaten in fear tastes of the fear and nothing else.

80. To share a table with one's enemy is to disarm him with a fork.

81. The perfect welcome leaves no evidence, only the strange conviction that one has come home.

82. Hospitality is the last religion the faithless keep, and the first the cruel abandon.

83. The guest departs, and the host discovers he was the one being hosted all along.

84. To open a door is a small thing; to leave it open is the whole difficult art.
