The board lights up before I finish the question, three lines at once, little green stars in the dark of the booth. I turn the studio light down low the way I do this hour, and I say it again for whoever's just tuning in: tell me about a thing you lost and never got around to replacing. Not something you upgraded. Something with a hole where it used to be. Line one, you're on the air.

"Oh. Am I? Hello. Sorry, I had the radio on and now it's on a delay, that's odd, I'll turn it — there. Right. It was a thimble. My mother's. Silver, worn down thin on one side where her finger went, and she never sewed, that's the funny part, she couldn't sew a button, but she kept it in the sugar tin, and when I was small I'd fish in the sugar for it and she'd smack my wrist, gently, you know, not hard. I don't know where it went. When she died we cleared the house in a weekend, which was too fast, everybody says that afterwards, they say it too fast. I've thought about buying one. But then it would just be a thimble. It wouldn't be the one from the sugar."

Thank you. That's — yeah. That's the thing exactly, isn't it, it wouldn't be the one from the sugar. Line two.

"Yeah hiya, can you hear me, I'm driving, I've got the hands-free. I lost a lighter. Zippo, proper one, brushed steel, and I'm not even a smoker anymore, gave up nine years, but that lighter I had since I was seventeen and it had this dent in it from when I dropped it off a roof in Magaluf and it still worked, still sparked, and that was the thing, wasn't it, you'd flip it open and it'd go first time every time. I could do the trick, roll it up my arm. Left it in a taxi. And you'd think, get another, they're twenty quid, but a new one doesn't have the dent, does it, and it's not been to Magaluf, it's not been anywhere, it's just a shop lighter. So I don't. I click my thumb sometimes like I've still got it. Anyway. Green light. Cheers, drive safe yourself, love the show."

Drive safe. Click your thumb for us. Line three, hello, you're through.

"...Hello?"

You're on the air, take your time.

"I lost my accent. Is that — can that be a thing? I know you said an object. But I keep thinking about it since you asked. When I came over I had it thick, my mum's, my nan's, the whole valley in it, and people'd ask me to say certain words for a laugh at work and I'd say them and everyone laughed and I laughed, and then one day someone new started and they said, oh, whereabouts are you from, and I said the place, and they said, oh, you can't tell. You can't tell at all. And I felt — proud, for a second. Proud. And then in the car park after I tried to say the words to myself, the way I used to, and I couldn't get it back. It wouldn't come. It's not lost like you can't find it in a drawer. It's lost like it wore off. And I never noticed it going, that's what gets me. Sorry. That's not an object."

It's an object to me. Thank you for that. Stay with us, all of you, we've got a full board, it's twenty past the small hour and nobody's sleeping. Line four.

"Right, mine's daft. It's a spoon. Wooden spoon. My gran's, she baked, and the spoon had gone kind of — soft? At the edge, from the years, the wood went velvety, and it smelled of every cake she ever made, all of them at once, and I took it when she moved into the home because I thought, I'll bake, I'll be the one that bakes now. Never baked. Put it in a box. Moved four times. Somewhere between the third flat and this one the box didn't come. I've bought spoons. New wood's got a smell but it's a wrong smell, it's sawmill, it's shop. Hers was — it was butter and vanilla and a hundred birthdays. You can't buy that, you'd have to bake for forty years to get a spoon like that and I don't have forty years of anybody's birthdays in me. Sorry, I've gone on."

You go on as long as you like. That's what we're here for. Line five, you're on the air.

"Good evening. I'll be brief. I lost a fountain pen given to me by a man I should not have accepted a pen from, if you follow me. It was a long time ago and we were both married elsewhere. I used it for twelve years to sign nothing of importance — shopping lists, crosswords, the backs of envelopes — because I could not use it for anything that mattered without feeling I owed something. It leaked, latterly. It stained a coat. And when the nib finally went I did not have it repaired, and I did not buy another fountain pen, because I understood that I had been keeping the man in the pen, do you see, and that when the pen stopped I had let him go at last, and it was a relief, and it was also — the other thing. It was the other thing too. Both. Thank you for letting me say that out loud. I've never said it out loud."

Thank you. Both. I think that's most of them, if we're honest. Both at once. We'll take a short piece of music and then more of you. Stay warm out there.

...

And we're back, and the board's still full, it doesn't empty this hour, it only turns over. Line one again, new caller, you're through.

"Hello, yes, mine is a marble. A single glass marble, blue with a white twist in the middle like weather, and I lost it when I was six down a drain outside the newsagent and I cried and my father said don't be soft it's a marble, and he was right, it was a marble. I'm seventy-four. I still know exactly which drain. I could take you there. I have owned a house, I have owned two cars, I have raised three children and buried a wife I loved more than the language has words for, and I do not grieve any of those things the way I grieved that marble, cleanly, do you understand, the way a child grieves, with your whole body and then it's over. That's what I lost. Not the marble. The way of losing where it ends. Everything since has been the other kind."

The other kind. Yes. I don't have anything to add to that, sir, so I won't. Thank you for calling.

It's late now, later than the clock admits. The lines are lit and I'm going to keep taking them, one after another, all these small holes in all these dark rooms, until the birds start or the board goes quiet, whichever comes first. Line two. Tell me what you lost. I'm listening. There's nobody here but us and the hour, and I'm listening.
