Before the sun had properly crested the eastern ridge, the valley lay under a hush so complete that even the grass seemed to withhold its breathing, each blade bent beneath its beading of dew as though kneeling to something not yet arrived, and the great bowl of the land, cradled between two long shoulders of hill, held the last of the darkness pooled in its lowest places, a cool blue sediment of night that had not yet learned it was already being dissolved. The river ran through the middle of all this like a thread of pale metal drawn out and laid down by a patient hand, and above it, thickening the air just where the water met the land, the mist stood in soft columns and drifting sheets, neither rising nor falling but simply being, a whiteness that had substance without weight, that touched the willows along the bank and left them half-erased, their trailing branches dipping into a milk that was neither cloud nor water but the third and secret thing that lives briefly between them at the hinge of the night and the day.

There was no sound yet that a person would call a sound, only the low continuous murmur of the river talking to its stones, and beneath that a silence so deep and so patient that it felt less like the absence of noise than like a presence in its own right, a great held breath spread across the whole of the valley floor, waiting. The hills to the west were still purple and shapeless, mere suggestions of themselves, while to the east the sky had begun very slowly to thin, to grow translucent at its lowest edge, a bruise of grey giving way to the faintest and most tentative gold, a gold so pale it might have been imagined, a color you had to be still to see, and stiller still to believe. And the whole vast morning seemed to be gathering itself, drawing its long breath in, before the enormous and silent event that no one had come to witness and that would happen regardless, as it had happened uncounted mornings before this one and would happen uncounted mornings after.

Then the first light came, not all at once but by increments too fine to name, spilling first along the very rim of the eastern ridge so that the tips of the pines up there were suddenly touched with fire, each needle for a moment a small burning wire, and the light poured down the slope like something liquid, like a slow tide of honey finding its level, coming down through the bracken and the gorse and the tumbled grey stones until it reached at last the floor of the valley, where the mist was waiting. And where the light met the mist, the true miracle of the morning began, quietly, without announcement, for the whiteness that had seemed so solid, so permanent, so much a fixture of the world, began to loosen at its edges, to grow luminous from within, to catch the low gold light and hold it and scatter it, so that for a while the mist was not a veil but a lamp, a thing lit up from every side, glowing, radiant, a suspended brilliance through which the willows and the reeds showed only as darker suggestions, as shapes remembered rather than seen.

The dew answered the light in its own way, each droplet on each blade of grass becoming a separate small sun, so that the whole slope of the meadow seemed strewn with fragments of a shattered star, glittering and trembling with the least stir of the waking air, and where a spider had strung its web between two stems in the night, the light found the web too and made of it a wheel of fire, a delicate architecture of burning silk hung with beads of flame, and this too was there whether anyone saw it or not, offered up to the morning with a lavishness that asked for nothing in return. And still the mist over the river burned, thinning now, tearing softly into ragged streamers that lifted and curled and drifted upward as though the whole white body of it had decided at last to become the sky, to abandon the low water and ascend, dissolving as it rose, growing thinner and thinner until it was only a brightness in the air, and then only air, and then nothing at all but the clean sharp light and the river showing itself again, unveiled, running clear and quick over its bed of brown and green and gold, catching the sun full now upon its surface so that it seemed no longer to be made of water but of light itself, a moving ribbon of pure day laid down along the floor of the world.

How strange it was, and how full of a stillness that was also a kind of trembling, to stand at the edge of such a happening and to feel the great slow wheel of the morning turn, to feel the light strengthen minute by minute, warming the stones, warming the backs of the sleeping sheep on the far hillside who had not yet risen, warming the folded wings of the birds who now, one by one and then all together, began to loose their voices into the brightening air, so that the silence that had been so absolute was replaced not by noise but by a music, a spreading fabric of song woven strand by strand across the valley, thrush answering blackbird answering wren, until the whole air seemed to shimmer with it as the grass shimmered with dew. And the river ran on beneath the song, indifferent and faithful, carrying its cargo of light down through the meadows toward the distant sea, past the willows that stood dark and dripping now in the full clear day, past the reeds where the moorhen stepped delicately from shadow into gold, past the water-meadow where the mist had lain and where now only a faint dampness in the air remembered it.

And everything that had been hidden was revealed, and everything that had been grey was given back its color, the green of the grass returning as though a curtain had been drawn from before the eyes, the brown of the ploughed field on the eastern slope glowing suddenly rich and dark and warm, the white flanks of the far cottages catching the light and throwing it back, and over it all the sky opening upward and outward into a blue so deep and so tender that to look up into it was to feel oneself grow small and glad in the same breath, dissolved like the mist into the great generosity of the day. The valley lay open to the light now, its darkness spent, its secrets given over, holding nothing back, and the awe of it was not in any single thing but in the whole, in the mere fact that this should happen, that the world should turn its face once more toward the sun and be made new, quietly, completely, and for no reason that anyone could name, only that it was morning, and the mist had burned away, and the river ran shining through the valley into the day.
