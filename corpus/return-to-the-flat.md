The apartment had kept its own weather while he was gone. He noticed it the moment the door gave: a stale, settled cold that no radiator had troubled in weeks, the air holding the particular grey of rooms that have been left to themselves. He set the bag down inside the threshold and did not turn on the light. From the landing behind him the timer switch ticked, patient and mechanical, and then released the corridor into darkness.

On the table in the front room a glass stood where he had left it. He was almost certain he had left it. Half an inch of something at the bottom had dried to a brown ring, a tide line, and around it the dust had come down evenly, the way snow settles on a thing that will not move. He looked at the glass for longer than the glass deserved. There was a version of the evening he had left — the phone ringing, the coat half on, the taxi already idling in the street — in which he had not touched the glass at all, in which it had been full, and he could not now make the two accounts agree.

The power was partial. In the kitchen the refrigerator held its light and its low animal hum, but the hall lamp, when he tried it, gave nothing, and he stood a moment with his thumb still on the switch as if pressure alone might persuade it. Somewhere below a pipe knocked twice and was quiet. He thought, without wanting to, of his mother's flat in the last year, how she had moved through it by memory in the evenings to save the meter, and how he had found this frightening at the time and understood it now as a kind of economy of grief, a way of not seeing the rooms she had already begun to leave.

He went to the window. The street was doing what streets do at that hour, which is almost nothing: a man walking a dog that did not want to be walked, the wet shine of the tarmac under the one working lamp, a bus going by with three passengers arranged at careful distances from one another, each lit and separate, like figures in a diorama of a city rather than the city itself. He watched until the bus was gone and the street returned to its patient emptiness, and he was aware, distantly, of being glad no one had looked up.

He had come back to collect things. That was the story he had given, and it was true in the way that the smallest true thing can stand in for a larger one you have no words for. There were papers to be sorted, a lease to be surrendered, a life to be reduced to what would fit in the boot of a hired car. He had imagined the task as brisk. He had imagined himself brisk. But the flat had other ideas, and the first of them was the glass, and the second was the smell of the hallway, which was the smell of every hallway he had ever waited in, and the third — he could not name the third, only feel it arriving, the way you feel weather before it is weather.

In the bedroom the bed was made. He had not made it. Or he had, and had forgotten; the memory would not come when he called it. He sat on the edge of it, and the mattress gave under him with the small resigned sigh of a thing that has held the shape of an absence for a long time. On the nightstand a book lay face down, spine broken to a page he did not remember reaching. He picked it up and read a line and it meant nothing to him, a sentence in a language he had once been fluent in. He set it down again, open, exactly as it had been, out of a courtesy he could not have explained to the person he had been when he last put it there.

It was then that he heard the tap. A single drop, and then, after a considered pause, another. In the bathroom the cold tap had begun to weep, a slow punctuation against the enamel, and he stood in the doorway watching the water bead and fall, bead and fall, and he did not move to tighten it. There was something in the rhythm he did not want to interrupt, some argument the flat was making that he had come, at last, to hear. Each drop struck and spread and vanished down the dark eye of the drain, and the sound of it was the sound of the flat keeping time in his absence, counting off the weeks with the patience of a thing that expects nothing and is therefore never disappointed.

He thought of the glass again. He was sure now that it had been full. He was equally sure that it had not. Both certainties lived in him at once, and he understood that this was what the rooms were for, that a place you have loved and left does not preserve the truth of what happened in it but the truth of your not being able to settle it, holds your contradictions the way water holds the shape of the last thing to disturb it and then, slowly, lets that shape go.

The refrigerator shuddered and fell silent, its cycle complete, and in the new quiet the tap seemed suddenly loud, then ordinary, then part of the room. He went back to the front room and looked at the glass and did not pick it up. Tomorrow, or the day after, he would pick it up, and rinse the brown ring away, and pack the flat into boxes, and hand the keys to a stranger, and none of it would take as long as he feared. But not tonight. Tonight he would let the glass stand where it stood, half full or empty, and let the tap keep its slow accounts, and sit in the cold the flat had made for him, in the dark he had chosen not to lift, until the street outside went fully quiet and the last bus had carried its lit and separate passengers away to wherever it is that people go when there is no one left to wait for them.

The drop fell. The drain took it. He did not move, and the room, for once, seemed content to have him there, saying nothing, keeping its counsel, holding its small brown tide line up to the last of the light as if to ask him, one final time, whether he was certain, whether he had ever been.
