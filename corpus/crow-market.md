The awning bar shines. It runs the whole length above the fruit and it holds the sun in a long white line, and the crow drops to it because the line is good, because from here the ground spreads out full of small brightnesses. The bar is warm under the feet. It hums a little when the cloth below it snaps in the wind.

Below and to the left there is a wet gleam, a fish thing on ice, silver going to grey, and the ice throws back the sun in a thousand jumping points that pull the eye and will not let it settle. Too many edges. The crow looks past it. Fish is behind glass anyway, behind the flat hard shine that is not water, and a hand keeps moving there, a pale slow shape rising and falling, but the hand is only a hand and the crow lets it fall out of attention.

A gull screams somewhere off past the roofs. Marked, held, dropped. Not close. Not down here.

There. On the stones. A curl of something yellow-brown, torn at one end, and it is bread and it is not moving and no foot is near it. The crow tips its head so one eye takes the whole of the gap between the trestle legs. Movement everywhere in that gap — the many-legged dark churn of people, boots and hems, a wheel turning, a bag swinging low and back, low and back — but the bread lies still in a pocket of stillness under the table edge, in shadow, and the crow measures the shadow against the boots and the boots are slow and heavy and far apart.

Drop. Two beats of the wings and the stones come up. The bread is close now, huge, its torn end soft and its crust catching a little grease-light along one ridge. The beak takes it. It is lighter than it looked. A boot comes down two lengths away with a sound like a door and the crow is already up, bread held, the trestle leg flashing past, up onto the crossbar where the awning meets the pole.

Here the bread breaks well. Soft inside, the good part, swallowed. Crust harder, worked at, cracked, gone. A man below makes the loud rising noise that men make, arm thrown up, but the arm does not reach and the noise does not touch anything, and the crow keeps to the crust until the crust is finished and only the taste stays.

Wind lifts the awning cloth. Under it the light changes, red now, the whole world under the cloth washed red and moving, and out at the cloth's edge the sun-line on the bar breaks and reforms, breaks and reforms. The crow watches the breaking. It could watch the breaking a long time. But down and to the right, past the red, there is a hard small brightness that does not break — a fixed point, a made thing, round and toothed at the rim and lying flat between two crates where the stones dip. It does not move. It holds the sun the way water holds it but harder, colder, without any give.

That.

Everything in the crow leans toward it. Not food. The beak knows already it is not food, has known bright hard round things before, has held them and dropped them and held them again. Not food and still the leaning is there, the pull along the spine, the want with no eating at the end of it. The crow shifts foot to foot on the bar. Below, the churn thickens. A voice climbs over the other voices, higher, sharper, cutting — a small human shape near the fixed brightness bending, straightening, its mouth going, its arm out toward a taller shape — but the crow does not follow the mouth or the arms, only the round bright thing between the crates, which the small shape's foot now almost covers and then does not, uncovers, leaves shining.

Wait. The crow is good at waiting. It holds the bar and lets the market run under it. The fish hand rises and falls. A green heap of leaves grows and is pulled down and grows again. A dog goes through the boots nose-first and is gone. A red thing rolls — an apple, round, and roundness is nearly the bright-hard want but softer, and it rolls under a wheel and bursts and stops being round, and the crow marks the burst and lets it go.

The small sharp-voiced shape moves off at last, pulled by the taller one, both shapes folding into the churn and swallowed, and their going means nothing except that the foot is gone from near the brightness now, gone for good, and the crows drops.

Stones. The dip between the crates is cool and smells of old fruit and wet wood. The bright thing is here, right here, and it is a coin, round, milled at the edge so the rim is a run of tiny even lines that each take a splinter of sun. The crow takes it. Cold in the beak, a clean coldness, a weight that is wrong for its size, heavier than bread of the same width, and this wrongness is part of the good of it. It is not food. It will never be food. The crow lifts it anyway because it shines and because the shine is held and does not break.

Up onto a crate. The coin turned in the beak, tilted, so the sun runs off the rim in a slow gold slide, one way and then the other. A boot passes. A hem drags. A hand comes down onto the crate top very near, flat and pink and enormous, and the crow feels the wood shudder under it and lifts — coin held — to the awning bar, to the long warm line, and here it can turn the coin again in peace and the sun runs off it again and the running is the whole of the world for the length of several breaths.

Below the market goes loud all at once. Many voices at the same pitch. A crash of the fixed hard shine breaking — the not-water glass over the fish, down in bright pieces — and a running, boots hammering, the whole dark churn suddenly all flowing one way like water finding a low place. The crow marks the crash and the crash is nothing to eat and nothing to hold and it lets the crash go, but the flowing pulls at something older, the flowing means run, and the feet grip harder on the bar and the coin is very tight in the beak now.

Then in the low place where the churn has thinned, on the wet stones by the broken silver, a whole spill of the fish things, flung and scattered, the grey-silver bodies bright and unglassed and no hand near them and no boot for the length of many boots, an open field of them shining and torn and there for the taking.

Food. The word of the body, louder than shine.

The coin drops. It rings once on the bar, twice on the stones, small and bright and rolling, and the crow does not watch it roll. The crow is already down, wings wide over the scattered silver, beak open, the good stink rising, the market running the other way and leaving all this brightness that can be eaten lying open in the sun.
