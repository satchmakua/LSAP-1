The laundromat was on the corner of Ninth and Marsh. It stayed open all night. The sign in the window said so. The letters were red. One of them did not light.

A woman came in at eleven forty. She carried a blue plastic basket. The basket held wet clothes. She had washed them somewhere else. She wanted the dryers here. Her dryer at home was broken.

There were twelve machines along the wall. Six were washers. Six were dryers. Four dryers worked. She knew which ones. She had come here before.

She put her clothes in the third dryer. She fed coins into the slot. There were quarters. She had a roll of them in her coat. She counted eight. The machine took six. She kept two.

The dryer started. It made a low sound. The glass door got warm. The clothes turned inside. She sat on the plastic chair by the window.

The floor was gray. The walls were white. The light was long and white above her. It buzzed. It did not stop buzzing.

A man came in at midnight. He carried a duffel bag. The bag was green. It had a broken strap. He set it on the folding table.

He looked at the washers. He chose the fourth one. He opened the door. He took clothes from the bag. He put them in the washer. He did not sort them.

He put in soap. The soap was in a small box. He tore the box open. He poured it in. He closed the door. He put in coins. The washer began to fill.

He sat two chairs from the woman. He did not look at her. She did not look at him. They watched the machines.

The clock on the wall said twelve ten. The second hand moved. A car passed outside. Its lights crossed the ceiling. Then it was gone.

The dryer stopped at twelve twenty. The woman got up. She opened the door. She touched the clothes. Some were dry. Some were not. She put more coins in. She started it again.

She sat down. The man's washer stopped. He got up. He moved his clothes to a dryer. He chose the second one. He put in coins. It started.

He sat down again. He was closer now. One chair between them.

"Cold out," he said.

"Yes," she said.

That was all. The machines turned. The light buzzed.

A while passed. The clock said twelve forty. The woman's dryer stopped. She got up. She opened the door. The clothes were dry now. She pulled them out. She put them in the blue basket.

She carried the basket to the folding table. She set it down. She began to fold.

She folded a shirt. She smoothed it flat. She folded the sleeves in. She folded it in half. She set it in a pile.

She folded a towel. She folded it in three. She set it on the shirt.

The man's dryer was still running. He watched it. Then he stood. He walked to the table. He stood at the other end.

"Almost done," he said.

"Yes," she said.

She folded a second shirt. She folded a pair of pants. She matched the legs. She folded them at the knee.

The man watched the dryer. It stopped. He went to it. He opened the door. He took the clothes out. He carried them to the table in his arms.

He set them down. Some fell. He picked them up. He put them on the table.

He began to fold. He folded a shirt. His fold was not straight. He tried again. It was still not straight. He left it.

The woman folded. She was fast. Her piles were even. She did not look at his.

"You do it good," he said.

"Practice," she said.

He folded a towel. He folded it wrong. He held it up. He looked at it.

"Like this," she said.

She took a towel from her basket. She held it up. She folded it in three. She showed him each step. She set it down.

He looked. He took his towel. He tried again. He folded it in three. It was close.

"Better," she said.

"Thanks," he said.

They folded. The light buzzed. Outside a truck went by. The window shook a little. Then it stopped.

The woman finished her basket. She had four piles. Shirts. Towels. Pants. Small things. She stacked them in the basket. She stacked them flat.

The man was still folding. He had a pile and a mess. He worked on the mess.

The woman put on her coat. She had taken it off to fold. She buttoned it. She had five buttons. She did four. The top one was gone.

She picked up the basket. She held it against her side.

"Night," she said.

"Night," he said.

She went to the door. She pushed it open. Cold came in. She stepped out. The door closed behind her.

The man kept folding. He had six shirts. He folded them one by one. Some were better than others. He put them in the duffel bag.

He folded the pants. He rolled them. He did not know how to fold pants. He put them in the bag.

The small things he did not fold. Socks and such. He put them in loose.

He zipped the bag. The zipper stuck. He worked it. It moved. He closed it most of the way.

He picked up the bag. The strap was broken. He held it by the handle. It was heavy on one side.

He looked around the room. The machines were still. The doors were open. Nothing turned.

He went to the window. He looked out. The street was empty. The streetlight was on. The woman was not there. She had gone.

He stood a while. Then he sat down. He set the bag by his feet.

The clock said one ten. The light buzzed above him. It did not stop.

He took out a phone. He looked at it. He put it away.

A man came in at one twenty. He was old. He carried a paper sack. He went to a washer. He looked at the coin slot. He had no coins.

He turned. He looked at the man with the duffel bag.

"You got change," the old man said.

The man reached in his coat. He had the roll no. He had loose coins. He counted them. He had four quarters.

"Here," he said.

He held them out. The old man came over. He took them.

"Thanks," the old man said.

"Sure," the man said.

The old man went back. He put the coins in. He put clothes from the sack into the washer. He shut the door. It started to fill.

The man stood. He picked up his bag. He held it by the handle.

He went to the door. He pushed it open. The cold came in.

"Night," he said.

The old man did not hear. He was watching the water.

The man stepped out. The door closed. The cold air went out with it.

The room was warm again. The old man sat down. He watched his washer fill.

The clock said one thirty. The second hand moved. The light buzzed above the empty chairs. The tables held nothing now. The floor was gray. One red letter in the window did not light.

A car passed. Its lights crossed the ceiling. Then it was gone.
