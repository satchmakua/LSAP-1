17.

The lighthouse, one thousand pieces, took eleven days because Marguerite kept the sky in her apron pocket and forgot she had it there.

3.

Two women at a card table in a rented cottage. Rain. A puzzle of a ship still sealed in cellophane. Neither has spoken yet about anything real. Delia tears the plastic with her thumbnail and says, corners first, and Marguerite says, obviously, corners first, and this is how forty years begins, with the word obviously, said kindly, across a folding table that wobbles on a floor neither of them owns.

29.

The one with the kittens they both hated. Five hundred pieces of kittens in a wicker basket. They finished it out of spite and never framed it and it lived in the hall closet for a decade like a small shameful animal.

12.

Delia's husband gone by then. The puzzle was the Alps, a gift from someone who didn't know what to bring a widow. Marguerite came over every night that winter without being asked. They did the snow last. There is so much snow in the Alps. They did it a piece at a time, white on white, holding each shape up to the lamp, turning it, and this was better than talking, and it was also talking.

8.

Sunflowers. Summer. The window open. A bee came in and neither of them screamed.

41.

At the end there were still puzzles they meant to do. A stack of them in Marguerite's spare room, the covers sun-faded, the cellophane going soft. The covered bridge. The market in some city. The map of the old world with its sea monsters. Delia keeps them. She has not opened one. She is not ready and also she is saving them, which are two different reasons that feel like the same warmth.

1.

The day they met Marguerite was carrying a box down from a moving van and dropped it and out spilled forty puzzles across the shared driveway, a thousand cardboard pictures skidding on the asphalt, and Delia, who had come out to complain about the van blocking her mailbox, knelt down instead and started gathering them, and read the covers aloud, the garden, the harbor, the balloons over the desert, and said, you'll need help with all these, and neither of them ever decided that this was a joke.

22.

The night of the eclipse they did not look at the sky. They did a puzzle of the sky instead, stars on black, the hardest thing, and laughed about it later, that the moon went dark over their roof and they were bent under a lamp fitting fake stars together with their reading glasses sliding down.

35.

Marguerite's hands by this point. The tremor. She sorted by color into muffin tins so her fingers had somewhere to rest. Delia never mentioned it. She simply started buying puzzles with bigger pieces, the ones marketed to children and the very old, and pretended it was because their eyes were tired, and Marguerite let her pretend, which is its own kind of love, to accept a mercy dressed as a preference.

6.

The wedding of Delia's daughter. They finished the garden puzzle at three in the morning, the two of them in their good dresses, shoes off, because the reception had ended and neither wanted the day to be over, and the garden had roses in it, and they pressed the last piece in together, four hands, and Marguerite cried and blamed the champagne.

18.

A one-line puzzle: the barn. Done in an afternoon. Easy. They talked about men the whole time and remembered none of it.

14.

Delia found the piece under the refrigerator two years after they'd given up on the Venice one. She washed the dust off it under the tap. She walked it across the driveway in her palm like a coin. Marguerite had already glued the Venice puzzle and hung it with its one hole, the missing window of a palazzo, a small absence they had learned to see as a feature. They did not un-glue it. They taped the found piece to the back of the frame, on the wrong side, where no one would see, so that the palazzo would carry its own missing window around inside it forever, complete and incomplete at once, and this pleased them enormously and they never explained it to anyone.

9.

Autumn leaves. She said, this is too easy, and it was, and they did it anyway, because sometimes you want a thing you can finish.

27.

The year of the surgery. Delia's, this time. Marguerite brought the puzzle into the hospital, a thousand pieces of a fruit market, and set up a tray table and started the border while Delia slept, and when Delia woke the border was done and Marguerite was asleep in the chair with an orange piece still pinched between two fingers, and Delia lay still and did not wake her and watched her friend breathe and felt the whole architecture of her luck.

2.

The second puzzle, done properly, at Delia's kitchen table this time, the harbor, and halfway through Marguerite said, I don't have anyone here, I moved for a job that already feels wrong, and Delia said, you have this, and pointed at the half-built harbor, and it was not enough and it was exactly enough.

38.

They stopped counting how many they'd done somewhere in the second decade. Someone asked once, a grandchild, how many, and they looked at each other and Marguerite said, all of them, which was not a number but was the truest answer.

15.

The puzzle with the wrong box. They opened the lighthouse and inside were the pieces of a cathedral. They built the cathedral without ever seeing the finished picture, working only from the shapes and the logic of stone, and when it was done Marguerite said it was the best one they'd ever made, because they'd made it blind, on trust.

31.

Christmas. The snow globe puzzle, round, no corners, which they had sworn they'd never do because round puzzles are a cruelty, and they did it anyway, cursing, delighted, the radio on.

4.

Early on, still learning each other, Delia said she liked to do the edges and Marguerite said she liked to do the middle, and this turned out to be the whole secret, that one of them wanted the boundary and one wanted the interior, and for forty years they never once fought over a single piece.

24.

The balloons over the desert. They did this one on the porch in June and the wind kept lifting the pieces and they weighted them down with stones from the garden, and every piece had its own small stone, and from the driveway it looked like a shrine.

19.

A one-line puzzle: the covered bridge, unopened, still.

11.

They agreed, without ever saying it, that you do not do a puzzle alone. It is a two-person religion. Once Delia was tempted, a rainy Tuesday, Marguerite away visiting a sister, and she took out the market puzzle and opened the box and looked at the pieces and put the lid back on and waited four days for her friend to come home, and told her this, and Marguerite understood completely and was so moved she could not speak, and put the kettle on instead.

40.

The last one they finished together was the field of poppies. Red on red, nearly as hard as snow. They did it slow. They knew, by then, though neither said. The final piece was a poppy at the center, and Marguerite held it out and said, you, and Delia said, no, you, and they set it down together, four old hands, the way they had begun, and looked at the whole red field they had made out of chaos and patience, and Delia said, obviously, and they laughed until they had to stop to breathe.
