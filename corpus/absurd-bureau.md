The object was a small brass funnel, and Gerald wished to return it. This was not, he felt, an unreasonable ambition. People returned things every day. There were signs about it, whole departments devoted to it, employees whose sole purpose was the taking-back of goods once given out. Gerald had the funnel in a paper bag, and he had his receipt, and he had the entire day off. He anticipated the matter concluded before lunch.

He began at the hardware shop on Wexler Street, because a funnel was, broadly speaking, hardware. The man behind the counter examined the funnel with the care of a jeweller appraising a stone.

"We don't sell these," he said.

"You must," said Gerald. "I bought it here. On Tuesday."

"On Tuesday we were closed for the plumbing."

"Then Monday."

"On Monday I was here the whole day and I sold four things. A rake, a tin of paint, a doorknob, and a length of hose. No funnel."

"It could have been the funnel and not the doorknob."

The man shook his head slowly. "A doorknob is a doorknob. You don't confuse a man buying a doorknob for a man buying a funnel. They stand differently."

Gerald could not argue with this, having never studied the postures of doorknob-buyers, and so he thanked the man and left, resolving to try the kitchenware shop, since a funnel was, broadly speaking, kitchenware.

The kitchenware shop was run by a woman with an apron and enormous certainty. She held the funnel up to the window light.

"This is brass," she said.

"Yes."

"We deal exclusively in stainless steel. A brass funnel has never crossed this threshold. I would remember the colour."

"But the receipt—" Gerald began, unfolding it.

She waved it away without looking. "Receipts prove a transaction occurred. They do not prove where. A receipt is a rumour on paper."

Gerald found this unexpectedly persuasive, and left with his funnel and his rumour, and stood for a while on the pavement thinking. It was still morning. He had lost no ground, exactly, though he had gained the sense that the ground itself was somewhat provisional.

He tried the antique shop next, reasoning that a brass funnel might have aged into an antique between purchase and complaint. The antique dealer was a thin man who wore two pairs of glasses, one on his face and one on his head, and used neither.

"This is not old enough," the dealer said, without touching it. "I can tell from the smell. This funnel is at most eleven years old. I do not sell anything younger than a century. It would be like selling an infant."

"I don't need it to be old. I need you to have sold it."

"Then you have come to precisely the wrong shop. Everything I have sold is old. Everything you have is new. We are opposites, you and I. It is almost beautiful."

Gerald agreed that it was almost beautiful and went to have a coffee, during which he laid the funnel on the table and regarded it as one might regard a difficult relative. The funnel gave nothing away. The waiter came and asked if the funnel was ordering anything, and Gerald said no, the funnel was between establishments, and the waiter nodded as though this were a common and understandable condition, which perhaps in this town it was.

After the coffee Gerald grew methodical. He would try every shop on the street, in order, and eliminate them one by one until the guilty shop, cornered, confessed. He began at the chemist. The chemist said funnels were not medicine and that furthermore the shop had a strict policy against brass, on account of an incident he declined to describe. The florist said the funnel was not a flower, which Gerald conceded, though he pointed out that flowers were not the only thing florists sold, and the florist said in her shop they were, and that everything else was decoration, and the funnel was not decorative, it was merely funnel-shaped, which was the least decorative shape there was.

At the bookshop the owner said, "We sold you a book, perhaps, that mentioned a funnel."

"That's not the same."

"It is nearly the same. The idea of the funnel passed from us to you. Is that not a kind of selling?"

"I want to return the object, not the idea."

The bookshop owner sighed the sigh of a man who had explained this many times. "We cannot take back objects. Only ideas. And ideas cannot be taken back at all, which is why we don't accept returns."

By early afternoon Gerald had visited eleven shops and every one of them had, with varying degrees of philosophy, denied all knowledge of the funnel. The strange thing — the thing that gnawed at him as he sat on a bench and unwrapped the paper bag again to make sure the funnel was still a funnel and had not become something worse — was that nobody doubted the funnel existed. They all agreed it was there. They all agreed it was brass. They simply, unanimously, denied any part in bringing it into the world of commerce. The funnel, by consensus, had never been sold by anyone. And yet Gerald had paid for it. He had the receipt. The receipt said FUNNEL and a price and a date. It did not, he now noticed for the first time, say where.

This troubled him. He turned the receipt over. The back was blank, as backs of receipts are, but its blankness now seemed pointed, a deliberate withholding. He considered the possibility that the funnel had come to him by some route that did not pass through a shop at all — that it had simply arrived, the way certain relatives arrive, and that the receipt was a document forged by the funnel itself to give it the appearance of legitimate origin.

He rejected this. It was not yet two o'clock. He was not going to start believing the funnel had motives.

He tried a shop that sold nothing at all — an empty shop with a man in it and a single chair — on the theory that a shop selling nothing had the widest possible inventory to draw returns against. The man in the chair looked at the funnel with real interest.

"I've been waiting for that," he said.

Gerald's heart lifted. "You sold it to me?"

"No. But I've been waiting for it. There's a difference, and it's the difference between us." The man settled deeper into the chair. "I don't sell. I wait. You could leave it here, and I would go on waiting, and eventually something else would arrive, and I would wait for that too. But I couldn't give you money. Money is for people who sell. I only receive."

"Then take it," said Gerald, seizing the moment.

"I can't take it. I can only wait for it. If you give it to me, the waiting ends, and then what would I do?" He looked genuinely frightened. Gerald, who did not want to be responsible for the collapse of another man's occupation, apologised and left.

By evening the shops were closing. Gerald walked home with the funnel, which he had failed to return, and the receipt, which had proved nothing, and a peculiar new respect for the funnel, which had survived a full day of interrogation without once losing its shape or its shine or its total refusal to have come from anywhere. He set it on his kitchen shelf. In the morning, he decided, he would try to buy a second one, so that he might have something to return the first against. It seemed, at that hour, entirely reasonable.
