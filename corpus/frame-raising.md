We came before the light had fully climbed the ridge, walking up out of the low places where the mist still lay in the swales like something spilled, carrying our tools wrapped in oilcloth and our hats pushed back and our breath white in front of us, and we came from the near farms and the far ones both, cousins who had ridden since the small hours and neighbors who had only to cross a fence line, and we gathered in the trampled yard where the timbers lay stacked and squared and waiting, and we knew without being told that this was the day the barn would stand.

The oaks had been felled in the winter past and dragged and hewn and let to season, and now the great sills lay along the stone foundation like the bones of a thing that had lain down to sleep and would presently be woken, and we set our hands to them first while the coffee was still passing round in tin cups from the fire the women had kept since dark, and we lifted the first sill and walked it into its bed and drove the pins home, and the sound of the mauls came flat across the cold yard and rolled off into the fields, and we felt in that first blow the whole shape of the day settle onto our shoulders and be welcome there.

We were many and we moved as one, and there was no man among us who stood idle, for the old ones sat on the sawhorses and told us where the joints had gone wrong in barns raised forty years gone and how to keep them true, and the young ones ran the pegs and the pins and the wooden mallets from pile to pile with their faces bright, and the strong ones bent to the timbers, and the careful ones knelt to check the mortises with their thumbs, and we found that each of us had a place that fitted him as the tenon fits its socket, and we filled those places gladly and the morning opened up around us like a flower.

By the time the sun had cleared the trees and laid its first true warmth across our backs we had the sills all pinned and the floor joists dropped between them, and we walked out across that new-laid floor that as yet had no walls and no roof and stood open to the whole blue sky, and we stamped our boots on the planks to hear them ring solid, and we laughed at the strangeness of standing in a room that was only floor, and somewhere down by the fire a fiddle started up slow and easy, not to play us a tune yet but only to keep us company, and we bent again to the work.

Then came the bents, the great flat frames of post and beam and brace that we had pegged together lying down upon the deck, each one a wall's worth of oak assembled there upon the boards, and these were the heart of the day, and every one of us gathered to the first of them, and the master framer walked its length once and touched each joint and nodded, and we set our pike poles under the top plate and we set our shoulders low against the posts, and he called to us to make ready, and we made ready, and the whole yard went quiet but for the fiddle holding one long note, and then he called heave and we heaved.

We heaved and the great bent came up off the deck slow as a sunrise, groaning in all its green joints, and the men on the poles pushed it higher and the men below guided the post feet into their mortises in the sill, and for one long breath the whole frame hung in the balance and depended on nothing but our arms and our footing and our will not to let it fall, and we held it and we shoved it that last hand's breadth up to true, and the tenons dropped into their homes with a sound we felt in our teeth, and someone drove the first brace pin and the bent stood, it stood by itself against the sky, and a shout went up from all of us at once and rolled down the valley, and the fiddle broke into a reel.

After that first one it was almost as though the barn wished to rise, and we raised the second bent and pinned the connecting girts between them so that the two stood braced against each other, and then the third and the fourth, and each time we heaved together and each time it came up and each time we shouted, and the children who had been kept back by the fire could not be kept back any longer and ran laughing beneath the growing frame, and the dogs ran with them, and the sun stood high and hot and we shed our coats and rolled our sleeves and the sweat ran clean down our faces and we did not mind it.

At noon we ate standing, or sitting on the piled lumber, plates the women brought us heaped with ham and bread and beans and pickles, and we ate fast and hard and talked with our mouths full of what remained to do, and the old ones squinted up at the standing bents and pronounced them true, truer than any they had helped to raise, and we knew this was the thing old men say at every raising but we were glad to hear it all the same, and then we wiped our hands on our trousers and got up, for the best of the work was yet before us and the sun would not wait.

For now there remained the plates to run along the tops of the posts the whole length of the building, and then the rafters, and then the last and highest thing of all, and we climbed up into the frame itself, up onto the girts and the plates, walking that high narrow oak with the whole yard spread out beneath us, and we hauled the rafters up by rope hand over hand and set their feet and pegged their peaks, and the skeleton of the roof spread out over us like ribs against the light, and then a man went up to the very top with the ridgepole slung, that long straight timber that would run the whole spine of the barn, and we fed it up to him and he laid it in its notches and drove the pin, and the barn was whole.

And when the ridgepole was set we nailed a green bough to it up at the peak so the frame would know it was blessed, and we came down out of the high timbers one by one into the long gold light of evening, and we stood in the yard and looked up at what had not existed at dawn and stood finished now against the reddening sky, and we could hardly credit that our own hands had made it in the turning of one day.

Then the boards were laid across the sawhorses for tables and the pots came off the fire and the lamps were lit and hung in the new frame itself so that the whole open barn glowed like a lantern, and we sat down together beneath our own raised roof, cousins and neighbors, old and young, and we ate and we drank and the fiddle played and we danced upon the new floor until the stars came out through the rafters, and we were glad, we were glad, we had built the thing together and together we made merry beneath it far into the night.
