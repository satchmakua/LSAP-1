There is a particular grade of morning light that I have not seen anywhere but on that lake, and though I have gone looking for it in the sixty-six years since, on other water, in other summers, with eyes that have grown milkier and slower and more forgiving, I have never once found its equal, and I have come to suspect that the light was not a property of the lake at all but of the boy who stood at its edge, and that the boy is gone in the way boys go, absorbed rather than lost, folded into the man and then into the old fellow who sits now with a blanket over his knees writing this down while a nurse he is fond of hums somewhere behind a wall. I was fourteen. I want to say the exact word so that you will not mistake me for someone reaching after a metaphor: fourteen, an age I can hold in my two hands now the way you hold a smooth stone, turning it, feeling how much smaller it is than the weight it once had when you first pulled it from the streambed.

The lake was called Corran, or so the local people said, though I never saw it written and cannot vouch for the spelling any more than I can vouch for most of what a memory hands up to you, grinning, insisting it is the truth. It was wider than it was long, which is unusual, and it was the width that mattered to us, the whole community of children who came down to it, because the width was the thing no one under sixteen had done. You swam out to the raft, everyone swam out to the raft, that was nothing, that was the shallow currency of the place. But to strike out past the raft and keep going, to put the far shore ahead of you like a promise you had made to yourself in the dark and now had to keep in the daylight, with the water going from the warm brown of the shallows to that cold green nobody's toes had names for — that was the thing.

I had been afraid of it for two summers. I do not mind saying so now; I would have minded terribly then, at fourteen, when to admit fear was to hand a weapon to whoever was standing nearest, and there was always someone standing nearest, a cousin or a friend or that particular category of boy who is neither and worse than both. But from where I sit, an old man whose fears have all been catalogued and mostly outlived, the fear of the fourteen-year-old seems to me almost tender, almost precious, the way the first bruise on a child seems precious to a parent because it is proof the child has begun to move through the actual world. I remember the fear better than the water. Isn't that strange. I can summon the fear whole, the specific cold weight of it low in my stomach, and yet the crossing itself comes back to me only in pieces, in flashes of arm and breath, as though the doing of a hard thing erases itself in the doing, leaving behind only the before and the after, the two banks of the fear with the accomplished river running between them, invisible.

My mother had made me eat before I went down, which I resented, and which I now understand for the small act of love it was, a mother sending calories after a son she could not follow. There was porridge and there was a heel of bread with jam gone half to sugar at the edges of the jar, and I ate standing up, and she said something I did not keep, and I am sorry now that I did not keep it, because I have kept so many idle things, the color of a boy's swimming shorts, the smell of the rope on the raft, and lost the one sentence my mother said to me on the morning I first swam the width of Corran. That is how memory economizes. It is a poor and sentimental accountant. It saves the receipts and burns the will.

I went in from the small stones. The others were already out at the raft, and one of them — Declan, it would have been, it is nearly always Declan when I need a name for the boy who dared me — called something across the water, and I did not answer, because I had decided, in the private way you decide the enormous things at fourteen, without ceremony, almost without noticing, that today was the day and that I would tell no one until it was done. I swam to the raft. I hung on the edge of it a moment, and I felt the wood, and I looked back once at the shore where my whole small known world lay, the towels, the bicycles, the trodden grass, and then I looked at the far side, which from the water is a wholly different proposition than it is from the bank, lower, longer, less certain of its own existence, a smudge of trees that might have been the edge of the earth. And then I let go of the raft and I kept swimming and I did not stop.

I will not pretend to you that I remember the middle. The middle of any great crossing is a country without landmarks; you are simply there, and then still there, and the mind, having no scenery to fasten to, tends to go quiet, or to sing to itself, or to count. I think I counted. I think I told myself that I would swim to a hundred and then look up, and then to a hundred again, and that the far shore, when I finally permitted myself to see it, was closer than my faithless heart had promised it would be, which is the discovery that lies at the bottom of every hard thing a person ever does, the plain fact that the far side always comes, that the water is finite even when the fear is not.

I put my feet down in weeds I did not know and did not like, and I stood in strange shallows on the wrong side of my own lake, and I turned around, and there was the raft, small now, and the boys on it small, and beyond them my mother's world smaller still, and I understood — I am nearly certain I understood it even then, and did not only invent the understanding later to make a better story — that I had made the world larger by crossing it, that a thing you have swum across belongs to you in a way a thing you have only looked at never can.

I did not shout. I have thought about that often, in the sixty-six years, how I did not shout or wave or perform the triumph, but only stood there breathing in the reeds with the whole width of the water between me and any witness, keeping the thing for a moment entirely to myself before I gave it away by swimming back. That was the truest happiness of my life, I think, and I have had a great deal of happiness and am not being maudlin. It was the happiness of a thing done and not yet spoken of, held in the hand like the smooth stone, warm from the swimming, mine.

The nurse has stopped humming. The light through this window is ordinary and good. I was fourteen, and I swam the width of it, and I am glad, at eighty, past all grief and most of pride, simply and steadily glad, that the boy did not turn back at the raft.
