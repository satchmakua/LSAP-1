1. A tool is a confession: it declares in advance which part of the world you intend to alter.

2. The hammer does not believe in persuasion. This is why it is honest and why it is dangerous.

3. What breaks first is never the weakest thing, but the thing asked to hold two ambitions at once.

4. Repair is the argument that the past is not finished with us. Replacement is the wish that it were.

5. Maintenance is the tax levied on everything you love. The unloved thing pays nothing and rots for free.

6. A dull blade injures more than a sharp one, because it demands force where skill should have sufficed.

7. To sharpen is to remove; every edge is a small confession of subtraction.

8. The novice fears the broken thing. The master fears the thing that has never broken, for it has never been understood.

9. Rust is patience with a grievance.

10. Every machine hides its true design until the day it fails, and then it explains itself all at once.

11. A screw loosens in the direction of neglect and tightens only in the direction of attention.

12. The best repair is invisible; the worst is decorative; the honest one shows its stitches and asks nothing.

13. We do not fix objects. We renegotiate our contract with their eventual death.

14. A tool used for the wrong task will do the wrong task well, which is the most persuasive kind of ruin.

15. Wear is the diary the object keeps of your hands.

16. The wrench respects only the right size. It has no opinion of your intentions, and this is its virtue.

17. What cannot be disassembled cannot be understood, only worshipped or discarded.

18. Grease is forgiveness applied in advance.

19. A thing designed never to be opened was designed never to be trusted.

20. The amateur tightens until it stops. The professional tightens until it begins.

21. Entropy is not the enemy of maintenance; it is its employer.

22. Every household keeps one drawer where broken things go to be neither mended nor mourned. This drawer is the truest room in the house.

23. To oil the hinge is to admit that even openings grow reluctant.

24. The tool teaches humility by breaking exactly when your confidence exceeds your grip.

25. A crack repaired is stronger than the surrounding surface, and this is the only argument for suffering I have ever found convincing.

26. Duct tape is a promise you intend to keep later. Later never comes, and the tape holds anyway, which shames us both.

27. Sharpening a knife, you learn the difference between working the tool and merely holding it.

28. What is well made can be maintained; what is merely finished can only be replaced.

29. The measure taken twice is not distrust of the ruler but respect for the cut.

30. A tool left out in the rain is a lesson you paid full price for and refused to receive.

31. Repair is memory with a purpose; hoarding is memory with none.

32. The bolt that will not turn is not asking for strength. It is asking for oil, heat, or patience, and it will punish you for offering only the first.

33. Every fix is temporary. The competent simply postpone more elegantly.

34. To maintain a thing is to love it in the future tense.

35. The screwdriver that fits is worth more than the tool that impresses.

36. A house is not a possession but a subscription paid in labor, and the bill never stops arriving.

37. What you cannot repair, you do not own; you merely borrow until it fails.

38. The finest tools disappear in the hand. Only the poor ones remind you they exist.

39. Wear evenly and you last long; wear in one place and you break early. This is true of tools, and of persons.

40. A thing over-maintained becomes a museum of its own function, too precious to use.

41. The first cut teaches. The second cut earns. The third cut, if you are honest, forgives the first two.

42. To repair another's work is to read a letter never addressed to you.

43. The file removes what the plan overlooked.

44. Every broken thing offers two futures: restoration, which requires humility, and disposal, which requires only money.

45. The gauge does not lie, but it does not care whether you believe it.

46. A well-kept tool outlives its owner and passes on the discipline it demanded.

47. Nothing is truly fixed; it is only returned to service, which is a different and lesser mercy.

48. The stripped thread is the record of one moment when force replaced understanding.

49. Preventive maintenance is the courage to fix what has not yet betrayed you.

50. We call a tool reliable when it fails predictably. Reliability is not the absence of failure but its schedule.

51. The plumber's true labor is not the pipe but the diagnosis; the pipe is merely where the argument was settled.

52. A machine's noise is its speech. To ignore the new sound is to refuse a warning offered in the only language available.

53. What you sharpen, you shorten. Every capability is purchased against a lifespan.

54. The clamp holds two things together only long enough for them to learn to hold themselves. Then it must be removed, or it becomes the flaw.

55. Solder joins by melting; the strongest bonds require that something first give way.

56. To disassemble carelessly is to trust a future self who will not exist.

57. The right tool used badly beats the wrong tool used brilliantly. Competence bows to fit.

58. A leak is a truth that has found the only path we left open.

59. Maintenance is the argument against the fantasy that anything, once achieved, stays achieved.

60. The broken thing does not want your pity. It wants the correct part and steady hands.

61. Every repair leaves a scar, and the scar is where the object becomes autobiographical.

62. The apprentice asks what tool to use. The master asks what the material wants.

63. To tighten in sequence, in the pattern, is to acknowledge that force applied unevenly warps the very thing it seeks to secure.

64. A tool loaned is a tool half-lost; a tool given is a tool honestly ended.

65. Dust is the slow evidence that use has ceased. Where nothing is touched, everything is dying quietly.

66. The workbench remembers what the mind forgets: the position where the thing was last understood.

67. To calibrate is to admit the world has drifted, and that we drift with it unless corrected.

68. The best maintenance leaves no story to tell, and so goes unthanked, as all the deepest work does.

69. What we mend, we come to know; what we discard, we merely misremember as broken.

70. The tool teaches its final lesson at the moment it fails: that nothing exempts itself from wear, least of all the instrument by which we resisted it.

71. Every threshold worn smooth is a monument to passage. Repair the door if you must, but honor the hollow.

72. To keep a thing in repair is to disagree, daily and quietly, with the direction of time.

73. The strongest structure is not the one that never bends but the one built to be re-tightened.

74. In the end, maintenance is not about the object at all. It is the discipline of attending to what would otherwise decline without complaint — which is to say, everything, including ourselves.
